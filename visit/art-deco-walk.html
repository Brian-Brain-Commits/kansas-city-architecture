Self-Guided Walking Tour • Downtown Kansas City

The Art
Deco Mile

Distance 1.2 miles
Time 1.5–2 hours
Difficulty Easy — flat terrain
Best Time Morning or late afternoon
Stops 6 landmarks

The route: Power & Light → Fidelity Bank → City Hall → Jackson County Courthouse → Municipal Auditorium → Midland Theatre

◆

About This Tour

Between 1925 and 1940, Kansas City built a collection of Art Deco civic and commercial buildings that places it among the great Art Deco cities of America. The style arrived here through New York and Chicago, filtered through the sensibility of local architects who understood that Kansas City needed to project civic ambition — it was a city still proving itself. The result was a cluster of buildings in the heart of downtown that packed more ornamental invention per block than almost any American city outside Manhattan.

This tour concentrates that inventory into a 1.2-mile loop through downtown's core. Begin at the KC Power & Light Building on Baltimore Avenue, work north toward City Hall, then loop back through the civic center. Give yourself time to stop and look up — that is the instruction the buildings themselves keep issuing.

◆
Stop 1

KC Power & Light Building

1330 Baltimore Ave • Start here

The tour begins at the Power & Light Building (1931), the most ornate Art Deco tower in Kansas City and one of the finest in the country. Architect Hoit, Price & Barnes designed a shaft of warm Indiana limestone faced with extraordinary terra cotta ornament — sunbursts, eagles, geometric friezes — that builds in visual intensity as the tower rises toward its illuminated crown. Stand at the corner of 14th and Baltimore and look straight up: the setbacks spiral away from you in a rhythm of stone and shadow that rewards the vertical gaze more than almost any building in the city.

The lobby, if open, is worth entering for its bronze elevator doors and the ceiling ornamentation above the mail slots — small-scale Deco work that shows the movement at its most exquisite. This is a building where the architects clearly believed that the quality of a lobby told you something about the quality of a civilization.

Stop 2

Fidelity National Bank Building

909 Walnut St • 3 min walk northeast

Walk north on Baltimore and east on 10th Street to reach the Fidelity National Bank Building (1931), now One Kansas City Place. At 36 stories it was for decades the tallest building in Missouri — a claim that reflects less the height of the building than the ambition of the city that built it during a depression. The tower is clad in buff limestone with restrained but precise Art Deco detailing at the setbacks and summit; the effect is more vertical and severe than the Power & Light Building's warmth, leaning toward the New York corporate Deco tradition rather than the ornamental school.

What to notice: the way the massing steps back at each setback, reducing the floor plate as the building rises. This is the classic Deco ziggurat silhouette, derived from the 1916 New York setback regulations but adopted here as a purely aesthetic choice — Kansas City had no such regulations.

Stop 3

Jackson County Courthouse

415 E 12th St • 4 min walk east

The Jackson County Courthouse (1934) is a public works project with a particular political history: it was built during the height of the Pendergast machine's power, and a young county judge named Harry Truman was on the court that oversaw its construction. The building is civic Art Deco at its most composed — a large but not aggressive mass of limestone, with vertical piers and a restrained ornamental program that projects authority without ostentation.

Note the bronze hardware on the main entrance doors and the carved stone reliefs above the entrance bays. This was public building in an era when the public building was understood to be a permanent expression of civic values, and the craft investment shows.

Stop 4

Kansas City City Hall

414 E 12th St • Directly across the street

Directly across 12th Street from the courthouse stands City Hall (1937), the culminating civic monument of Kansas City's Boom Era and one of the great Art Deco towers in America. At 30 stories it is the fourth-tallest city hall in the United States — a distinction that reflects the scale of ambition the Pendergast machine was willing to project in stone and steel.

The building uses the same civic Deco vocabulary as the courthouse but at a grander scale: vertical limestone piers, eagle reliefs, a polished granite base. The observation deck at the top is open to the public and offers one of the best views of downtown from any building in the city — the Power & Light tower to your southwest, the Missouri River to your north, the bluffs of the West Bottoms below. The ascent is free and takes about ten minutes.

Stop 5

Municipal Auditorium

301 W 13th St • 6 min walk west

Walk west along 13th Street to the Municipal Auditorium (1935), a WPA-era civic building of extraordinary scale and architectural ambition. The auditorium was designed to accommodate any event the city might need to host — conventions, concerts, political rallies, boxing matches — and the building's massing reflects that promiscuous civic purpose: a vast horizontal block that occupies most of a city block, faced in warm limestone with subtle Deco ornamentation.

The arena hosted the 1928 and 1976 Republican National Conventions, a long list of boxing championships, and decades of concerts and civic events. From the street, the building reads as a self-contained civic world — an interior large enough to contain a city within a city. Note the decorative spandrel panels above the entrance bays and the way the building turns its corners with rounded masses rather than sharp angles.

Stop 6

Federal Reserve Bank of Kansas City

925 Grand Blvd • 5 min walk east, tour ends here

The tour concludes at the Federal Reserve Bank (1921, expanded 1956), a building that sits at the edge of the Deco period but captures its institutional gravitas. The Federal Reserve branches built across America in the early 20th century were designed to project permanence, authority, and financial solidity — values the Art Deco vocabulary expressed more effectively than any previous style. The Kansas City branch's neoclassical bones and restrained ornament mark the transition between the Beaux Arts civic tradition and the Deco modernism that would follow.

From this point you are two blocks from the Power & Light Building where the tour began — completing the loop and returning you to the building that best captures why this half-mile of downtown Kansas City ranks among the great Art Deco corridors of the American Midwest.
