Self-Guided Walking Tour • Crossroads Corridor

Union Station
to Liberty Memorial

Distance 1.1 miles
Time 1.5–3 hours
Difficulty Easy — slight grade south
Best Time Any time; museum open daily
Stops 3 landmarks
◆

About This Tour

This tour connects two of Kansas City's great civic monuments along a short corridor that passes through the heart of the Crossroads Arts District. The route begins in the grand hall of Union Station — one of the finest Beaux Arts train stations in America — and ends at the Liberty Memorial, the tallest World War I memorial in the United States and the site of the National WWI Museum. Between them, the walk passes through a stretch of the Crossroads where the industrial past and the creative present exist in the same city block.

Plan for more time than the walk itself requires. Union Station's grand hall merits a long pause; the Liberty Memorial's observation deck and the museum below it are half-day destinations if you are inclined toward them. The Kauffman Center for the Performing Arts — at the western edge of the Crossroads — is a short detour worth taking if you are passing on a day when it is open to visitors.

◆
Stop 1

Union Station

30 W Pershing Rd • Start here; enter through the main hall

Kansas City Union Station (1914) is one of the three largest train stations ever built in the United States. Designed by the Chicago firm Jarvis Hunt, the station was conceived at the apex of the railroad era — the moment when the train station was the grandest civic room in any city it inhabited, the place where travelers arrived in a city and formed their first impression of what the city thought of itself.

The grand hall — 95 feet high, lit by three 3,500-pound chandelier fixtures — is the largest room in Kansas City and one of the most impressive interior spaces in the American Midwest. The Beaux Arts vocabulary is deployed here at a scale that makes most American civic buildings seem modest in comparison: the arched windows, the limestone facades, the clock above the waiting room — all of it calibrated to produce the sensation of entering a space proportioned for a grander civilization than the one you came from.

The station was restored in 1999 after two decades of near-total vacancy and now operates as a science museum, restaurant, and event space. The grand hall is accessible to the public. Walk its full length; find the restored clock; stand under the chandeliers and look up at the coffered ceiling. This is the interior that greeted Harry Truman, Charlie Parker, Ernest Hemingway, and every other notable person who arrived in Kansas City by rail for thirty years. The 1933 Union Station Massacre — in which federal agents and their prisoner were shot by gangsters in the station's parking lot — is marked by a small plaque outside.

Stop 2

The Crossroads & Kauffman Center

Crossroads Arts District — walk west on Pershing then south on Broadway

Exit Union Station and walk west along Pershing Road to Broadway, then south into the Crossroads Arts District. The blocks along Broadway between Union Station and 17th Street offer a compressed version of the district's character: brick warehouse buildings from the early 20th century, now occupied by galleries, restaurants, and creative businesses; murals covering entire building facades; the KC Streetcar running through the center of the street.

At the western edge of this stretch, at 16th and Wyandotte, sits the Kauffman Center for the Performing Arts (2011), designed by Moshe Safdie. The building's sweeping steel shell — a parabolic arc that encases the concert hall and opera house — is as assertive an act of civic architecture as anything built in Kansas City in the last fifty years. From the front plaza, with Union Station to the northeast and the Liberty Memorial to the south, you can read the city's architectural ambition across three different eras: Beaux Arts, late modernism, and contemporary starchitecture, all within half a mile.

Stop 3

Liberty Memorial & National WWI Museum

100 W 26th St • Tour ends here

The Liberty Memorial (1921–1926) is the tallest World War I memorial in the United States and the site of the National World War I Museum — the only museum in the country to hold the Congressional designation as the national WWI museum. The memorial was built with funds raised by Kansas City citizens in 1919, in a campaign that collected $2.5 million in ten days — then the fastest successful major fundraising drive in American history.

The tower rises 217 feet above the memorial complex and its observation deck offers one of the best views of Kansas City available anywhere: Union Station directly to the north, the downtown skyline to the northeast, the Crossroads below, and — on clear days — the full arc of the city spread across the Missouri River bluffs. The museum beneath the tower is housed partially underground, beneath a glass floor through which you can see artifacts and a re-creation of the Western Front landscape. Admission is charged for the museum; the exterior memorial grounds and the walk to the tower base are free.

Standing at the memorial's base, looking north toward Union Station across the axis of Pershing Road, you can see the corridor this tour has walked in a single view: the great train station, the arts district between them, and the war memorial — three monuments from three different moments in Kansas City's civic life, aligned on the same axis, visible at once.
