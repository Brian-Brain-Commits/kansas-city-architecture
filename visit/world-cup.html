The City You're Coming To

Most World Cup visitors arrive with a stadium and a match day in mind. Kansas City will give them that — Arrowhead Stadium, one of the loudest venues in American sports, expanded and renovated for the tournament. But the city worth coming to is the one that exists outside the stadium: a Midwestern American city that built an extraordinary collection of Art Deco architecture between 1925 and 1940, spent decades ignoring it, and then slowly realized what it had.

No American city of comparable size has preserved its interwar skyline with the fidelity that Kansas City has. The towers are still there. The terra cotta ornament is still intact. The civic buildings — City Hall, Municipal Auditorium, the courthouse — face each other on the same streets they always have. An architecture- minded visitor arriving in Kansas City for the World Cup will find more of genuine interest than they are likely expecting.

The fountains alone justify a detour. Kansas City has more fountains per capita than any city outside Rome — a legacy of the J.C. Nichols development program of the 1920s, which imported European bronzes and placed them throughout a boulevard system that was itself one of the great achievements of American urban planning. The Country Club Plaza, the Spanish Colonial shopping district that anchors the city's southern residential neighborhoods, is unlike anything else in America. The boulevard system that runs south from the Plaza is still intact. The Nelson-Atkins Museum of Art is free, and one of the finest art museums in the country.

Kansas City is a city that rewards looking up and walking slowly. The World Cup gives you the occasion; this guide gives you the map.

The Two-Day Itinerary

Two full days is enough to see the essential Kansas City. Here is how to spend them.

Day One — Downtown & the Crossroads

Start at the KC Power & Light Building on Baltimore Avenue — the most ornate Art Deco tower in the city — and walk the Art Deco Mile north to City Hall and Municipal Auditorium. The City Hall observation deck is free and offers the best elevated view of downtown. Have lunch in the Crossroads, then walk to Union Station in the early afternoon. The Grand Hall is free to enter; allow an hour. In the late afternoon, walk south to the Liberty Memorial for the essential Kansas City panorama: Union Station below, the Art Deco towers above, the Missouri River visible to the north. If it's First Friday, stay in the Crossroads for the evening gallery walk — otherwise, the Power & Light entertainment district downtown is the evening option.

Day Two — Midtown & The Plaza

Start at the Nelson-Atkins Museum of Art — free, with the best permanent collection in the city and a sculpture garden that includes Henry Moore bronzes on the lawn. From there, drive or take a rideshare south to the Country Club Plaza. Begin at the J.C. Nichols Memorial Fountain and take the Plaza & Fountains walking tour — about an hour, ending at the Neptune Fountain on the district's western edge. Lunch on the Plaza, then drive south on Ward Parkway for the boulevard experience: the Meyer Circle Sea Horse Fountain, the Pomona Fountain near Loose Park, and the residential architecture of the Country Club District. Return to the Plaza for dinner as the evening light hits the limestone facades.

Getting to Arrowhead

Arrowhead Stadium is in the Truman Sports Complex, approximately 10 miles southeast of downtown Kansas City. It is not walkable from any of the city's walkable districts. For match days, plan on rideshare, a dedicated shuttle service, or driving — and allow substantial time for post-match traffic. The stadium sits alongside Kauffman Stadium (home of the Kansas City Royals) in a complex designed entirely for car access; it is an efficient venue for a large event and offers no particular architectural interest.

The interesting Kansas City is downtown, the Crossroads, Midtown, and the Plaza — all of which are north and west of the stadium, and all of which are worth arriving early for or staying late to see.

Practical Notes

Kansas City International Airport (MCI) has direct service from most major American cities and several international connections. The tournament is in June and July — Kansas City summers are hot and humid; early morning and late evening are the best times to walk. The KC Streetcar along Main Street is free and connects the River Market, downtown, and the Crossroads. A car or rideshare is needed for the Plaza, Midtown, and the stadium.

Hotel options concentrate in downtown, the Plaza, and the Crossroads. Staying downtown puts you within walking distance of the Art Deco core and the Crossroads, with the Plaza reachable by rideshare. Staying on the Plaza gives you the best of the southern districts and easy access to the boulevard system, with downtown about 10 minutes by car.
