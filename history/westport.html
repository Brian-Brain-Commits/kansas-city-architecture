The Town Before the City

Westport was Kansas City before Kansas City existed. Founded in 1833 by John Calvin McCoy — the same surveyor who would plat the Town of Kansas on the river bluffs five years later — Westport sat four miles south of the Missouri River landing, on a high limestone ridge where the prairie began. Its position was deliberate: it was the last outfitting point before the long grass opened up, the place where the trail traffic of a continent assembled and departed.

The Santa Fe Trail, the Oregon Trail, and the California Trail all funneled through Westport or its adjacent landing at Westport Landing — the precursor to the Town of Kansas — in the 1840s and 1850s. Merchants like Albert Gallatin Boone (grandson of Daniel Boone) and Harris & Company outfitted thousands of wagons annually with flour, bacon, powder, and rope. By the early 1850s Westport was the busiest inland outfitting town in America, its warehouses stocked against the demands of migration, commerce, and war.

The Battle of Westport, 1864

On October 23, 1864, Westport became the site of the largest Civil War battle west of the Mississippi River. Confederate Major General Sterling Price had led a 12,000-man raid deep into Missouri in a final attempt to reclaim the state for the Confederacy — and Union forces under General Samuel Curtis caught him at Brush Creek, on the southern edge of Westport.

The fighting lasted most of a day and involved nearly 30,000 men on both sides — an engagement that Civil War historians have called the "Gettysburg of the West." Price's army was shattered. Survivors retreated south through Kansas and into Indian Territory, and the Confederate cause in Missouri ended that afternoon on the ravines and meadows of what is now the Country Club Plaza shopping district. The stone outcroppings and creek lines that defined the battle's terrain are still visible in the topography of Brush Creek and the surrounding streets.

Independence and the Long Postwar Decade

After the Civil War, Westport did not disappear — but it was no longer the commercial pivot of the continent. The railroads had changed everything. The Kansas City & Santa Fe Trail trade that had made Westport prosper was replaced by rail freight that loaded and unloaded at the Union Depot in the West Bottoms, four miles north. The overland trails, which Westport had served for thirty years, fell into disuse. The cattle drives that came up from Texas went to Abilene and then Wichita; Kansas City's new prosperity was centered on the stockyards of the West Bottoms, not the limestone ridge of Westport.

Through the 1870s and 1880s, Westport remained an independent municipality — a self-governing town with its own charter, city council, and civic identity, sitting just south of the Kansas City boundary. It was proud, somewhat insular, and deeply aware that it had been there first. The rapid growth of Kansas City to the north, driven by the railroads and the livestock trade, made Westport's independence increasingly difficult to maintain. Infrastructure costs — sewers, street paving, utilities — that Kansas City could finance through tax revenues and bond markets were beyond Westport's capacity as a small independent town.

Annexation, 1897

In 1897, Westport voted to consolidate with Kansas City, Missouri — ending sixty-four years of independent municipal existence. The annexation was not a hostile takeover; it was a pragmatic recognition that the two communities had grown together economically and geographically, and that Westport's residents would benefit from Kansas City's infrastructure, schools, and financial capacity.

What the annexation did, architecturally and urbanistically, was profound. It gave Kansas City the high limestone ridge south of Brush Creek as a development corridor — a swath of well-drained, elevated ground that sat above the flood-prone bottoms and offered room for the kind of planned residential and commercial development that the older, denser downtown could not. Within thirty years, that corridor would produce the Country Club District — J.C. Nichols's masterwork of American planned suburban development — and the Country Club Plaza, the nation's first automobile-oriented shopping district, modeled on the architecture of Seville.

Every time a visitor walks the Plaza's Spanish towers and tile rooflines today, they are walking the terrain that was once Westport's common ground, and before that the bivouac of two armies fighting over Missouri's future. The layers are still there. The ridge is the same ridge.
