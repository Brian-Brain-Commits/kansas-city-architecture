The
Streetcar

Look Up, Kansas City Historical Record • Transit & Urban Form
Kansas City, Missouri — 1870s to Present — Built, Dismantled, Rebuilt

The Arc in One Sentence

Kansas City built one of the largest streetcar networks in America, tore most of it up by the late 1950s, and began slowly rebuilding it in 2016. That sentence contains most of what you need to know about how Kansas City thought of itself across 150 years.

The streetcar is the best single thread for understanding the city's physical history. The network's original extent shaped where the early twentieth-century neighborhoods grew. Its absence defined the postwar automobile city. Its return is reshaping the Main Street corridor in ways that are still being worked out.

1870s — 1957

Historic Streetcar System

Five hundred miles of track. Mule cars, cable cars, electric. The geography that the system created, and the racial boundary the Troost line reinforced. The last car ran in 1957.

2016 — present

Modern Streetcar

Free to ride. 2.2 miles originally; 5.7 after the 2025 UMKC extension. A billion and a half dollars of corridor development. A partial return of what was torn up sixty years earlier.

The Arc as a Short Timeline

  • 1870 — First horse-drawn streetcar lines begin operation.
  • 1888 — Electric streetcars arrive. The network expands rapidly.
  • 1900s–1920s — Peak system: roughly 500 miles of track, reaching every neighborhood in the metro.
  • 1930s — First signs of decline as bus service and private automobiles compete.
  • 1957 — The last Kansas City streetcar makes its final run. System fully dismantled.
  • 1970s–2000s — Multiple proposals to revive light rail fail at the ballot box.
  • 2012 — Downtown Transportation Development District approves streetcar construction.
  • 2016 — Modern streetcar opens on Main Street, 2.2 miles, free to ride.
  • 2025 — Extension to UMKC campus opens; total length 5.7 miles.
  • 2026— — Service carries World Cup visitors along the downtown corridor.
