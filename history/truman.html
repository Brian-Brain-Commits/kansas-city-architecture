Kansas City Made Him

Harry S. Truman was born in Lamar, Missouri in 1884 and grew up in Independence, a suburb east of Kansas City — close enough that he spent much of his adult life in and around the city, and that Kansas City's political culture shaped everything about his early career. He was not a Kansas City native in the strict sense, but he was a Kansas City product: his ambitions, his connections, his path to power all ran through the Pendergast machine.

Truman came to Pendergast's attention in the early 1920s through a network of Jackson County Democratic connections. Pendergast was looking for a candidate for one of the Jackson County judge seats — an administrative position managing county public works — and Truman, an honest, hard-working veteran of World War I with decent local connections, fit the bill. Pendergast supported Truman's successful 1922 campaign. A career began.

◆

The Machine's Honest Man

The paradox of Truman's career is that he was scrupulously honest in his personal conduct while operating entirely within a corrupt system. As presiding judge of Jackson County (1927-1935), Truman oversaw public works programs that were honest by the machine's standards — he did not take personal kickbacks and insisted on quality construction — but he operated within the patronage system that Pendergast controlled, and he knew perfectly well how the machine worked.

It was Pendergast who tapped Truman for the U.S. Senate in 1934. The machine delivered the Democratic primary; in Missouri's heavily Democratic era, that was tantamount to election. Senator Truman arrived in Washington as "Pendergast's man" — a designation that followed him for years and that he was unable to fully shed until Pendergast's 1939 conviction gave him no choice but to stand on his own record.

From KC to the White House

Truman's path to the presidency was entirely improbable and, in retrospect, entirely Kansas City. FDR's 1944 campaign managers needed a vice-presidential candidate who could unite the party's competing factions. Truman — by then a senator with a solid record on military procurement and domestic policy, no longer tied to a convicted machine boss, and acceptable to both Southern conservatives and Northern liberals — was the compromise choice. He was sworn in as president on April 12, 1945, eleven weeks after FDR's fourth inauguration, when Roosevelt died of a cerebral hemorrhage.

Within four months, Truman had authorized the use of atomic weapons against Japan. Within two years, he had announced the Truman Doctrine, the Marshall Plan, and the recognition of Israel. Within three years, the Berlin Airlift, NATO, and the Korean War. The man who owed his Senate seat to a corrupt Kansas City political boss shaped the architecture of the postwar world as profoundly as any American president.

Kansas City in the Truman Era

While Truman was managing the end of World War II and the opening of the Cold War, Kansas City was dealing with the aftermath of the machine's fall. Pendergast's 1939 conviction had broken the political organization that had governed the city for three decades. Reform candidates came to power. The patronage networks dissolved. The city that had built so much so quickly in the 1920s and 1930s entered a period of slower development.

Truman returned to Independence after leaving office in 1953 and lived there until his death in December 1972. The Truman Library in Independence — opened in 1957 — is one of the original presidential libraries and remains one of the most important historical sites in the Kansas City area. His home on Delaware Street in Independence, preserved as the Truman Home National Historic Site, is open to the public. If Kansas City built the man, it is worth seeing where he lived.
