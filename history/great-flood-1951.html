The Great
Flood of 1951

Kansas City, Missouri — July 13 to 15, 1951 — The Event That Ended a River City

The Setup

The spring and early summer of 1951 were catastrophically wet across the Kansas and Missouri River basins. Kansas alone received roughly twice its normal rainfall in May and June. By early July, the ground was saturated, the reservoirs were full, and the rivers were running high. Then the weather systems stalled, and the real rain began. In mid-July, storms dumped six to sixteen inches of additional rain across Kansas in a matter of days. The Kansas River — also called the Kaw, the river that joins the Missouri at Kansas City — rose faster than any gauge had ever recorded.

The Flood

On the morning of July 13, the Kansas River crested in Topeka at fifteen feet above flood stage. The crest moved downstream through the afternoon and evening, reaching Bonner Springs, then Turner, then the Kansas City levees. The levees held, then failed. By the morning of July 14, the West Bottoms was underwater.

Water levels in the industrial district reached thirty-five feet above street level in some locations. Entire blocks of warehouses, stockyard pens, meatpacking plants, and rail yards disappeared beneath the flood. Photographs from the period show the flooded district as a vast lake dotted with partially-submerged buildings, with animals, equipment, and debris floating on the surface. Roughly 500,000 acres were flooded across the metropolitan area. Between twenty-four and twenty-eight people died (estimates vary). The damage was later calculated at approximately $935 million in 1951 dollars — somewhere around $10 billion today.

The water receded slowly over the following week. What emerged from the flood was a West Bottoms that looked fundamentally different from the one that had gone under. Buildings that had been mostly intact before the flood were now structurally compromised. The soil was contaminated with industrial chemicals, meatpacking runoff, and worse. The stockyards had lost nearly all of their livestock. Major manufacturers had lost their inventory, equipment, and in many cases the buildings themselves.

The Aftermath

The West Bottoms never fully recovered as an industrial district. Individual companies rebuilt; some lines of business continued; the stockyards limped along until their eventual closure in 1991. But the commercial logic of the Bottoms — the concentrated industrial density that had made Kansas City the second-largest stockyards city in America — was broken. Major manufacturers and wholesalers began relocating to suburban and interstate-adjacent sites. The packing plants consolidated in smaller, more modern facilities elsewhere. The Bottoms became a post-industrial landscape well before American cities generally had that word for what was happening to them.

The flood catalyzed the federal flood control projects that would reshape the rivers for the rest of the century: the reservoirs built upstream in Kansas and Nebraska to buffer flood pulses, the levees improved and heightened around Kansas City, the channelization projects that turned tributary streams (including Brush Creek, south of downtown) into concrete-lined flood channels. These projects largely succeeded in preventing a repeat of 1951 at that scale. They also sealed the separation between the city and the rivers that had made it possible.

For most Kansas Citians after 1951, the Missouri River ceased to be a feature of daily life. The levees obstructed sight lines; the industrial corridor along the river was depopulated; the urban growth that the second half of the twentieth century produced happened south, toward the suburbs, away from the water.  Read about the River →

The Meaning

The 1951 flood is the moment Kansas City stopped being a river city in any meaningful sense. Before 1951, the Bottoms was a functional commercial district, the levee was a working part of civic life, and the river was a constant presence in the city's economic consciousness. After 1951, the river was a problem that had been solved by engineering and then forgotten. The warehouses that survived the flood sat empty for decades. The rail yards shrank year by year. The stockyards held on for forty more years before closing. The city that Kansas City became in the second half of the twentieth century was shaped in large part by the city it stopped being in July 1951.

The Bottoms today is having a second life — as an antiques district, as a wedding-venue corridor, as a film-production location, as a site for CPKC Stadium on the Berkley Riverfront. That second life is real and valuable. It is also operating on a fraction of the ground that the first life occupied, and in a fundamentally different economic logic. The flood did not kill the Bottoms. But the Bottoms that exists now is the one that grew up on the other side of the flood.
