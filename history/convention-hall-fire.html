Kansas City History • Episodes • April 4, 1900

The Convention
Hall Fire

Kansas City, Missouri — April 4, 1900 — The Night Before

The Building

In January 1899 Kansas City began construction of Convention Hall at 13th and Central. The building was designed to hold more than 15,000 people. It was built in ninety days. The speed of construction was itself a marketing argument: the city was bidding for the 1900 Democratic National Convention and wanted to prove it could host an event of that scale. The bid succeeded. The convention was scheduled for July 1900. Kansas City spent the spring preparing to host it.

The Fire

On the evening of April 4, 1900, three months before the convention, Convention Hall caught fire. The cause was never definitively established. The building was a total loss. The city woke up on April 5 to headlines announcing that the showpiece it had built for the national convention no longer existed, and that the convention was three months away.

The Meeting

The story that became legend: on the morning after the fire, Kansas City's civic leaders gathered and decided to rebuild. They did not decide to ask for an extension from the Democratic National Committee, or to find an alternative venue, or to accept that the convention would have to move. They decided to build a replacement building in ninety days, before the convention arrived. The fundraising began that morning. Contracts for materials were signed within days. Construction crews started pulling debris from the site while the ashes were still warm.

The Rebuild

The new Convention Hall opened in time. The Democratic National Convention convened in the new building in July 1900 and nominated William Jennings Bryan for president. Bryan lost to William McKinley in November. But the convention had happened, in a building that had not existed three months earlier, on a site that had been a pile of rubble in April.

The story became part of how Kansas City talked about itself for the next hundred years — the city that does not accept setbacks as permanent, that responds to disaster with construction, that pulls off feats of civic willpower that would be impossible elsewhere. It is the ur-example of the kind of civic ambition the city has demonstrated repeatedly since.  Read The Civic Imagination →

What Stands There Now

The 1900 Convention Hall was demolished in 1935 to make way for Municipal Auditorium, which still occupies the same site at 13th and Central. Convention Hall is in the Lost Kansas City archive — not because it was neglected, but because its purpose was superseded. The replacement is also architecturally significant. The story of the original building's rebuilding in 1900 is preserved in civic memory. The architectural record is preserved in the successor building that stands there today.
