Kansas City History • Episodes • April 9 – 12, 1968

The 1968
Riots

Kansas City, Missouri — April 9 to 12, 1968 — After Memphis

The Context

Martin Luther King Jr. was assassinated in Memphis on April 4, 1968. Riots and unrest broke out in more than a hundred American cities in the following days. Kansas City was one of them, though the unrest here did not begin until April 9 — five days after the assassination and one day after King's funeral in Atlanta. The delay was the result of a specific local failure: Kansas City schools had refused to close for the funeral day. When Black high school students walked out in protest on April 9, they were confronted by police. The confrontation escalated. By evening, the East Side was in open unrest.

The Four Days

Over the next three nights, six people died. More than 100 buildings were damaged or destroyed. The National Guard was deployed. Tear gas was used. Most of the destruction was concentrated in the Black neighborhoods east of Troost, including substantial damage to 18th and Vine, the district that had already been in economic decline for twenty years and that was in the middle of the urban renewal demolitions that would remove much of its remaining commercial fabric.

The mayor and police chief publicly framed the unrest as a law-and-order problem. Black Kansas Citians framed it as an expression of the accumulated failures of a city that had redlined their neighborhoods, segregated their schools, and demolished their commercial districts without serious investment in response. Both framings were true. They pointed to different solutions.

The Aftermath

The immediate aftermath included a series of meetings between city leaders and Black civic organizations, several of which produced specific commitments that were partially honored. The longer aftermath was less satisfying. The urban renewal demolitions continued. The 18th and Vine district did not recover until the preservation and rebuilding effort that began in the late 1980s. The physical damage from 1968 was repaired or demolished; the economic damage compounded for another generation.

The 1968 riots do not have a single monument. They are part of the context for understanding why the 18th and Vine district was as depleted as it was by 1990, when the restoration effort began. They are part of the context for the continued economic distance between neighborhoods east of Troost and those west of it.
