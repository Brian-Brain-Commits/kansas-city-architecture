George
Kessler

Look Up, Kansas City Historical Record • Figures
Kansas City, Missouri — 1882 to 1923 — The Landscape Architect Who Designed the Beauty

The 1893 Plan

George Kessler was born in Bad Frankenhausen, Germany in 1862 and trained in landscape architecture in Potsdam and Weimar before emigrating to the United States. He came to Kansas City in 1882 as a young man of twenty and found work laying out Hyde Park, one of the first planned residential districts in the city. Eleven years later he delivered the document that would reshape Kansas City more than any other single piece of paper in its history: the 1893 Parks and Boulevards Report.

The plan proposed a connected network of parkways linking the river bluffs to the southern prairies, with large parks at key nodes and small parks distributed through the residential districts. It argued that Kansas City, a fast-growing industrial city of the late nineteenth century, had the opportunity to incorporate green space into its urban structure rather than treating it as a residual afterthought. The plan was expensive, ambitious, and visionary. It passed.

◆

How the Boulevards Were Built

The mechanism was public condemnation funded by bond issues. The city used eminent domain to acquire properties along proposed boulevard corridors, paid property owners from bond proceeds, and built the boulevards as wide landscaped parkways with adjacent residential lots. The residential lots, now fronting on beautiful parkways, rose in value — recouping the public investment and often turning a profit for the city.

Paseo, Armour Boulevard, Ward Parkway, Gillham Road, Meyer Boulevard, Swope Parkway, Brush Creek Boulevard — these are Kessler boulevards, built over forty years through the mechanism his plan laid out. The City Beautiful movement that Kessler was part of produced similar plans in dozens of American cities; few were executed as thoroughly as Kansas City executed his.

◆

Legacy

Kessler died in 1923. By then his Kansas City plan had been under construction for thirty years and was largely complete. J.C. Nichols built the Country Club District along Kessler boulevards; Ward Parkway became the spine of the most prestigious residential corridor in the Midwest; the parks Kessler proposed are the parks Kansas Citians use today. Every postcard image of the city — the tree-lined boulevards, the fountains terminating vistas, the carefully landscaped parks — is in some measure a Kessler design.

His name is less recognized than it deserves. The city he designed is everywhere.
