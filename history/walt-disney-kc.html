Walt Disney's
Kansas City Years

Look Up, Kansas City Historical Record • Figures
Kansas City, Missouri — 1911 to 1923 — The Years Before Hollywood

A Kansas City Apprenticeship

Walter Elias Disney was not yet famous. He was a kid growing up in a house at 31st and Bellefontaine, delivering newspapers in the dark before school, drawing on whatever paper he could find. The Disney family moved to Kansas City in 1911 when Walt was nine; the city would be his home for most of the next twelve years. What he learned here — the commercial art craft, the early animation experiments, the first taste of running a company and watching it fail — shaped everything that came after.

After high school and military service, Disney found work at the Kansas City Slide Company and the Pesmen-Rubin Commercial Art Studio, producing advertising illustrations, movie title cards, and the simple animations that were beginning to appear in local theaters. He was learning on the job. The animation techniques he developed in Kansas City — cutout figures, pencil drawings photographed frame by frame — were crude by the standards of what he would build later, but they were the foundation.

◆

Laugh-O-Gram Studios

In 1921 Disney rented space on the second floor of the McConahay Building at 31st and Forest, and in 1922 incorporated Laugh-O-Gram Studios. The company produced animated fairy tales — modernized versions of Little Red Riding Hood, Jack and the Beanstalk, Puss in Boots — distributed as short subjects to theaters. The work was ambitious and the company hired a small staff of young animators, several of whom would follow Disney to California and become the core of the Disney Studios.

The company went bankrupt in 1923. The distribution deal fell through; the cash ran out; Disney slept in the office because he could not afford rent elsewhere. He left for Hollywood that summer with a suitcase, a prototype reel for a short called Alice Comedies, and no money. Within ten years he would be producing Steamboat Willie and by the end of the decade he would be the most famous animator in the world.

◆

The McConahay Building Today

The McConahay Building still stands. For most of the twentieth century it was an ordinary commercial building with no particular distinction; its significance was recognized only gradually as Disney's biographers tracked his early years. In recent years a preservation effort has taken shape around the building, working to restore it as a cultural site honoring Disney's Kansas City origin. Whether the restoration can sustain itself financially is an open question. The building's significance is not.

You can stand on the sidewalk at 31st and Forest and look up at the second floor and know that a young man with no money and a lot of ambition ran a company there that failed, and that the failure sent him west, and that the west eventually produced everything that Disney became. Kansas City did not make Walt Disney a success. But the city formed him, and when the success came it had a Kansas City shape to it — the small-town sentimentality, the midwestern work ethic, the belief that story and commerce could run together without either corrupting the other. All of that is Kansas City.
