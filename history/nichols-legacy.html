The Builder

Jesse Clyde Nichols arrived in Kansas City at the turn of the twentieth century with a Harvard degree in economics and an idea about how cities ought to grow. He was twenty-two years old. Within a decade he had begun assembling the land south of Brush Creek that would become the Country Club District — the most ambitious planned residential development in American history up to that time, a project that would eventually encompass 6,000 acres of housing, parkways, shopping districts, fountains, and landscaped grounds stretching from midtown Kansas City south toward the state line.

Nichols was a developer, but he thought more like a city planner — or rather, he thought like both simultaneously, always considering how the commercial and the residential reinforced each other. The Country Club Plaza, which opened in 1922 as the first planned suburban shopping center in the United States designed for the automobile, was not conceived as a commercial project separate from the surrounding neighborhoods. It was conceived as the civic anchor of a residential district — the place where the people who lived in the Country Club District would shop, dine, and gather. The architecture — Spanish Colonial Revival, modeled on Seville — was chosen to give the whole development a coherent, dignified identity that would hold its value across generations.

The Fountains and the Boulevards

Nichols's single most lasting contribution to Kansas City's physical character may be the fountains. He collected European bronze sculpture on his travels and brought pieces back to install in the Country Club District's parks and traffic circles — the Seahorse Fountain on Meyer Circle, the Pomona Fountain on Ward Parkway, the Bacchus Fountain near the Plaza. He donated approximately fifty fountains and pieces of sculpture to the city over the course of his career, more than any other individual in Kansas City's history. Kansas City's reputation as the City of Fountains is, to a meaningful degree, a Nichols project.

He worked in close collaboration with George Kessler's boulevard system, which had been planned in the 1890s as a network of landscaped parkways connecting the city's parks. Nichols built his developments along and around these boulevards — Ward Parkway, Brush Creek Boulevard, Meyer Boulevard — using the public green infrastructure as the setting for his private residential development. The result is the distinctive character of Kansas City's south side: wide, tree-lined boulevards lined with large houses, punctuated at intervals by bronze fountains and sculpture, flowing toward the Spanish towers of the Plaza.

The J.C. Nichols Memorial Fountain — four equestrian figures representing the great rivers of the world, installed in Mill Creek Park in 1960, ten years after his death — was given to the city by his family as a memorial to the man and his work. It is the most photographed fountain in Kansas City and one of the finest pieces of public sculpture in the Midwest.

The Deed Restrictions

Any honest account of J.C. Nichols must include the deed restrictions. From the earliest years of the Country Club District, Nichols's developments contained racially restrictive deed covenants — clauses that prohibited the sale or rental of properties to Black Americans, Jewish families, and others. This practice was legal and widespread in American real estate at the time; Nichols was among the most systematic practitioners of it, and he advocated for similar restrictions in neighborhoods and development projects across the country through his work with the National Association of Real Estate Boards.

The restrictions remained legally enforceable until 1948, when the Supreme Court ruled in Shelley v. Kraemer that courts could not enforce racially restrictive covenants. In Kansas City, as elsewhere, the patterns of residential segregation that the covenants had established did not disappear with the legal ruling — they had been written into the physical and economic structure of the city deeply enough to persist for generations. The southward growth corridor that Nichols developed was effectively closed to a large portion of Kansas City's population during the decades when the district was being built and its character established.

In 2020, the J.C. Nichols Memorial Fountain was renamed the Mill Creek Park Fountain by the Kansas City Parks Board, as part of a broader civic reckoning with Nichols's legacy. The fountain itself — the bronze, the water, the careful setting in the park — remains what it was. The naming question, and the history it represents, is part of the full story of how Kansas City came to look the way it does.

What Remains

The physical legacy of J.C. Nichols is everywhere in Kansas City south of Brush Creek. The Plaza, the fountains, Ward Parkway, the residential neighborhoods of Mission Hills and Brookside and Waldo — all of it reflects his vision of what a planned city could be. The aesthetic coherence of the south side, the quality of the public spaces, the density of art in the streetscape: these are real achievements that have made Kansas City a better place to live for the people who have lived there.

Holding both sides of that ledger — the fountains and the deed restrictions, the beauty of the Plaza and the history of who was and wasn't allowed to be there — is the work of honest civic memory. Kansas City is not unique in having built parts of itself on exclusion. It is part of what makes the history of any American city complicated and worth understanding fully.
