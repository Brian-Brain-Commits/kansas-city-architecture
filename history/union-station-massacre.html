Kansas City History • Episodes • June 17, 1933

The Union Station
Massacre

Kansas City, Missouri — June 17, 1933 — A Saturday Morning in the Parking Lot

The Event

Frank “Jelly” Nash, a federal prisoner convicted of bank robbery, had escaped from Leavenworth in 1930. Two FBI agents and an Oklahoma police chief captured him in Hot Springs, Arkansas on June 16, 1933. They brought him by train to Kansas City en route back to Leavenworth. The train arrived at Union Station on the morning of June 17. Three Kansas City police officers met the federal agents at the station to escort the prisoner.

In the parking lot, just outside the building's Main Entrance, men with submachine guns opened fire. Two federal agents survived the shooting. Four people died: FBI Agent Raymond Caffrey, Oklahoma Police Chief Otto Reed, two Kansas City detectives, and the prisoner Frank Nash himself. The attackers escaped.

Who Was Responsible

The case was never fully resolved. J. Edgar Hoover publicly identified Charles “Pretty Boy” Floyd, Adam Richetti, and Vernon Miller as the gunmen. Floyd and Miller were both dead within the next year; Richetti was executed in 1938 after a conviction that relied heavily on disputed evidence. Historians have debated for decades whether Floyd was actually at Union Station that morning; the evidence is contested.

The Political Consequence

J. Edgar Hoover used the massacre to argue for federal police expansion. Until 1933, FBI agents could not carry firearms or make arrests; they were investigators who had to call in local police to apprehend suspects. In the aftermath of Union Station, Congress passed a series of laws expanding FBI authority. The Bureau became an armed federal police force. The shift from “Bureau of Investigation” to “Federal Bureau of Investigation” with its modern powers dates substantially from this moment.

The building itself survived, as did most of the bullet marks in its exterior limestone. You can still find them today if you know where to look — on the east side of the north facade, near the Main Entrance. They are part of the architectural record of the building, not marked or interpreted, but there.
