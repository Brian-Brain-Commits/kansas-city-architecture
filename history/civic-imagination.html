The Civic
Imagination

Look Up, Kansas City Historical Record • Themes
Kansas City, Missouri — 1893 to Present — When the City Has Imagined Itself Bigger

“The thing about Kansas City's big civic moves is that they should not have worked. The city was too small, the ambition too oversized, the execution too compressed. But they worked anyway. Several times.”

The Argument

Every thirty or forty years, Kansas City does something that punches above its weight. The 1893 Kessler plan was the first: a landscape-architecture vision for the whole city that cost more than the treasury could comfortably absorb and succeeded anyway. The 1900 Convention Hall was built in ninety days after the original burned, and the Democratic National Convention proceeded on schedule. The 1925 Ten-Year Plan raised $40 million in Depression bond money to build an Art Deco downtown that still defines the skyline. The 1971 Truman Sports Complex built two major-league stadiums on a single site at a time when most cities were building one. The 2011 Kauffman Center committed a billion dollars to a performing arts hall in a city that was not obviously a billion-dollar-performing-arts-hall city. The 2024 CPKC Stadium was the first stadium purpose-built for a professional women's sports team. The 2026 World Cup will bring hundreds of thousands of visitors to a metro area that many of them cannot find on a map.

This is the recurring pattern. It is not steady civic governance. It is not the day-to-day mechanics of running a city. It is the intermittent appearance of a particular kind of ambition — the willingness to bet that Kansas City can pull off something that cities three times its size have trouble with. The bets do not always pay off. But they pay off often enough that the pattern is recognizable.

The 1893 Plan and What Followed

Kessler's Parks and Boulevards plan was visionary and expensive. It would take forty years to execute, using condemnation-funded boulevard construction and a series of bond issues that the voting public had to approve repeatedly. They approved them. The city built Paseo, Ward Parkway, Meyer Boulevard, Armour Boulevard — the most beautiful arterial system in the American Midwest. It was not obvious that this would happen. It happened because civic leaders decided it would.

The Ten-Year Plan

Kansas City passed a $40 million bond issue in 1925 to fund downtown infrastructure and civic buildings. By 1939, when the Pendergast machine collapsed in corruption charges, the city had built City Hall, the Jackson County Courthouse, Municipal Auditorium, and dozens of smaller projects. Most American cities spent the Depression shrinking. Kansas City spent it building. The Pendergast machine made it possible through combinations of political will, civic bond authority, and what can accurately be described as patronage; the machine was corrupt and the buildings are beautiful and those two facts are both true.  Read Pendergast →

The Kauffman Center as Turning Point

After the postwar decline, the Kansas City of the 1970s through 1990s was mostly a city of managed retreat — stadiums in the suburbs, the central city hollowing out, the civic imagination in hibernation. The Kauffman Center for the Performing Arts in 2011 was the first major sign that the hibernation was ending. Moshe Safdie's two curved shells on Broadway announced that Kansas City was prepared to invest in its own future at an ambitious scale again. The Power & Light District (2008), the streetcar return (2016), the CPKC Stadium (2024), and the World Cup (2026) follow from that moment and reinforce it. The civic imagination is awake again.

◆

What the Pattern Requires

The big civic moves work when three things align: political authority willing to spend, voter willingness to fund, and execution capacity willing to deliver. Kessler had all three in 1893. Pendergast had all three in 1925. The current moment seems to have at least two. Whether the third holds — whether Kansas City retains the civic competence to execute at scale — is the question the next twenty years will answer.

What the city has going for it is the pattern itself. Having done this before, repeatedly, over 130 years, gives the current generation a kind of institutional memory that says: this is possible. We have done it. We can do it again. That is not a trivial thing to have.
