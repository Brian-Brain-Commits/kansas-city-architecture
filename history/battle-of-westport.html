The Battle of
Westport

Kansas City, Missouri — October 21 to 23, 1864 — The Largest Civil War Battle West of the Mississippi

The Setup

By the fall of 1864, the Confederacy was losing the Civil War. Atlanta had fallen in September; Lincoln's reelection was increasingly likely; the Trans-Mississippi theater was becoming a sideshow to the war's eastern campaigns. Confederate Major General Sterling Price, a former Missouri governor, proposed a long-shot offensive into Missouri: capture St. Louis if possible, cause enough disruption to force Union troops to be pulled from other theaters, and influence the November election by demonstrating Confederate reach.

Price assembled roughly 8,000 troops, many of them poorly armed irregulars, and moved north through southeastern Missouri in late September 1864. Over the next month, his force engaged in running battles with Union troops while pushing toward Kansas City. By late October, Price's army had reached the edge of what is now Kansas City, pursued by Union Major General Alfred Pleasonton's cavalry from the east and facing Major General Samuel R. Curtis's Army of the Border — roughly 22,000 troops total — blocking his path west.

The Battle

The fighting unfolded across three days (October 21 to 23) and across several miles of what is now central Kansas City. On October 21, Price's force attacked Union positions at the Little Blue River; on October 22, fighting continued along the Big Blue River west of Independence; on the morning of October 23, the decisive engagement began on the rolling prairie south of Westport — the ground that is now Loose Park and the residential blocks surrounding it.

The terrain mattered. The battlefield was open prairie cut by creek beds (Brush Creek and its tributaries) and dotted with farmsteads, stone walls, and the occasional timber stand. Confederate troops took cover in the creek bottoms; Union forces advanced across the open ground; artillery from both sides engaged across the rolling fields. The fighting was intense and confused, with units often unable to see each other through the smoke and terrain. Approximately 3,500 casualties were sustained across the three days — more than in any other engagement west of the Mississippi.

By the afternoon of October 23, Price's army was in retreat. Pressed from the east by Pleasonton and from the north by Curtis, the Confederate force broke and withdrew south through Kansas. The pursuit continued for another week, with further engagements at Mine Creek and Newtonia before Price finally escaped south into Arkansas with the remnants of his command.

The Consequences

The Battle of Westport ended serious Confederate operations west of the Mississippi. Missouri would remain in the Union for the remainder of the war. The Confederate dream of reversing the tide through a western offensive died on the prairie south of Westport. The battle is sometimes called “the Gettysburg of the West” — a framing that reflects the scale of the engagement (the largest west of the Mississippi) more than its strategic importance (which was significant but not war-defining).

For Kansas City specifically, the battle's end meant the city could begin to grow again. The disruption of the Civil War years had slowed the population growth that the antebellum period had begun. With the Confederate threat eliminated and the war moving toward its conclusion, the commercial momentum that would produce the railroad boom of 1869 and beyond could begin to build.  Read about the Railroad Boom →

The Battlefield Today

The land where the main battle was fought is now largely residential — the blocks around 47th Street, Wornall Road, and Ward Parkway, developed in the early twentieth century as part of J.C. Nichols's Country Club District. Loose Park preserves approximately 75 acres of the battleground. A small monument in the park marks the site; the KC Parks department and the Civil War Round Table maintain interpretive markers along the park's walking paths.

It is possible to walk across the core battleground in about twenty minutes. The rose garden at the center of the park occupies ground that was contested for three days in October 1864. There is no evidence of the fighting on the surface of the land today — just grass, trees, and park benches. That is itself part of the story of Kansas City: a battlefield absorbed into a landscape that has moved on to other purposes, but whose shape still reflects the old ground underneath.
