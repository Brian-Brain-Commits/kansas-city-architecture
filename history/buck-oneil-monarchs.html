Buck O'Neil &
the Monarchs

Look Up, Kansas City Historical Record • Figures
Kansas City, Missouri — 1920 to Present — The Longest-Running Negro League Franchise

The Monarchs

The Kansas City Monarchs were founded in 1920 as charter members of the Negro National League. For the next forty years, until Major League integration gradually drained the Negro Leagues of their best talent, the Monarchs were the most consistently successful franchise in Black professional baseball. They won ten league pennants. They produced more eventual Major League players than any other Negro League team, including Jackie Robinson, Satchel Paige, Ernie Banks, and dozens of others. They played their home games at Muehlebach Field and later at Municipal Stadium, drawing crowds that, on the biggest days, rivaled anything the white Major Leagues put on a field.

The Monarchs were also an economic engine for 18th and Vine. Players lived in the neighborhood's rooming houses, ate in its restaurants, played cards in its clubs. The ballpark schedule determined the rhythm of the district for months of each year. When the team was home, 18th and Vine was at full volume. The architecture of the neighborhood — the rooming houses, the theaters, the ballroom — existed in part because of the Monarchs.

Buck O'Neil

John Jordan “Buck” O'Neil played first base for the Monarchs from 1938 through 1955, managed the team from 1948 forward, and after his playing career served as a scout for the Chicago Cubs, where in 1962 he became the first Black coach in Major League Baseball. He was a good player and a better manager, but his central contribution to the game came later. Starting in the 1980s, after baseball had mostly forgotten the Negro Leagues, O'Neil became the Leagues' most visible storyteller — giving interviews, addressing Congress, appearing in Ken Burns's 1994 Baseball documentary, and establishing himself as the historian of a generation that might otherwise have been lost to memory.

In 1990 he co-founded the Negro Leagues Baseball Museum in Kansas City. The museum moved to its current location at 18th and Vine in 1997. O'Neil served as its public face until his death in 2006. He was elected to the Baseball Hall of Fame in 2022, sixteen years after his death — the honor he had been denied in his lifetime.

The Architectural Footprint

The Monarchs' specific architectural presence at 18th and Vine is scattered but real. The Paseo YMCA at 19th and Paseo, where the Negro National League was founded in 1920, still stands. The Streetcar Diner, where players ate, is gone. Muehlebach Field, later Municipal Stadium at 22nd and Brooklyn, was demolished in 1976; a plaque marks the site. The Negro Leagues Baseball Museum and the American Jazz Museum now share a building at 18th and Vine that functions as the district's cultural anchor.

You can stand at 18th and Vine today and see traces of the Monarchs era if you know what to look for. The music plays on in the Gem Theater across the street. The memory is being preserved. The architecture that housed the life is largely gone. What remains is enough to understand what was there.
