An Architectural Glossary

Field
Guide

If you've been reading this site and wondered what a spandrel is, or what distinguishes a cornice from a frieze, this page is for you.

These are the architectural terms used throughout Look Up, Kansas City, with small visual examples where they help. The vocabulary of a building — cornice, setback, pilaster, spandrel — is worth learning. Once you have the words, you stop seeing buildings as a single block of visual noise and start seeing the decisions their designers made.

◆

Building Zones

Base / Plinth

The lowest level of a building, often visually distinguished from the floors above by a change in material, texture, or scale. Classical and Art Deco buildings usually treat the base as a substantial architectural event — rusticated limestone, granite, a taller ground floor, decorative detail.

See it: The Power & Light Building's ground-floor limestone base.

Shaft / Middle

The main, repetitive middle of a building — the floors where the windows are usually similar and the material stays consistent. In tall Art Deco towers, the shaft can run fifteen or twenty floors without major change.

Crown / Top

The upper termination of a building, often where architects put the most decorative energy. Art Deco crowns are the signature American contribution to the form — illuminated, setback, ornamented. The crown is the part of the building you see from a mile away.

See it: Power & Light's illuminated crown is one of the most photographed in the American Midwest.

Setback

A step backward from the building line at a particular floor, creating a ziggurat-like massing as the building rises. Setbacks became common after the 1916 New York zoning law and are a defining feature of 1920s-30s American skyscrapers, including most of Kansas City's Art Deco towers.

Ornamental Elements

Cornice

The projecting horizontal ornamental band that runs along the top of a wall, usually at the roofline or at a major setback. Cornices cast shadow lines that give a facade visual weight and terminate vertical compositions.

Frieze

A horizontal band of ornament or sculpture, usually just below the cornice. In Greek temples the frieze held relief sculpture; in American civic buildings it often holds an inscription or a sequence of decorative panels.

Spandrel

The panel between windows on successive floors of a multi-story building — the vertical strip that separates the window of floor one from the window of floor two directly above it. Spandrels are often decorated with relief panels, terra cotta ornament, or contrasting materials.

Dentil

A small tooth-like block ornament, repeated in series along a cornice or frieze. The name comes from Latin dens (tooth). Classical revival buildings use dentil molding heavily; you'll see it on Beaux Arts civic buildings.

Rosette

A circular ornament resembling a stylized rose, often used as a repeated decorative element on friezes, ceilings, or panels. Neo-classical and Art Deco buildings both use rosettes heavily, though the Deco versions tend to be more geometric.

Cartouche

An ornamental frame or scroll-like shield, often holding an inscription, coat of arms, or decorative relief at its center. Common on Beaux Arts and neoclassical civic buildings.

Pediment

The triangular (or sometimes curved) gable-like ornament at the top of a classical portico or over a doorway. The pediment is a quotation of the temple front; every Greek temple revival from the Parthenon onward has one.

Egg-and-dart

A repeating decorative molding pattern alternating egg-shaped and dart-shaped elements — one of the oldest classical ornaments, in continuous use since ancient Greece. Look for it along cornices and around door frames on civic buildings.

Materials

Terra Cotta

Fired clay, often glazed, used as a cladding and ornamental material on commercial buildings. Terra cotta is lighter than stone, can be mass-produced with repeated decorative detail, and was the workhorse material of American commercial architecture from the 1880s to the 1930s. Most of Kansas City's Art Deco buildings use terra cotta for their ornamental upper stories.

Limestone (Kansas)

The local limestone — quarried from Carthage, Missouri and several Kansas sites — is the defining material of Kansas City's civic architecture. Creamy-to-warm-gold in color, it takes carved ornament well and weathers to a rich patina. City Hall, the Jackson County Courthouse, and most of the Boom Era skyscrapers are clad in it.

Brick

Two distinct uses: load-bearing (older buildings where the brick walls actually hold up the floors) and veneer (newer buildings where brick is a decorative cladding over steel or concrete frame). The West Bottoms warehouses are mostly load-bearing brick; most 20th-century residential architecture is brick veneer.

Azulejo Tile

Painted ceramic tile, usually blue-and-white or polychrome, with a long tradition in Portuguese and Spanish architecture. J.C. Nichols imported Spanish azulejo to the Country Club Plaza to give his development the feel of an Andalusian town.

See it: Country Club Plaza.

Cast Iron

Molded iron used for nineteenth-century commercial facades, allowing prefabricated decorative elements to be mass-produced in factories and shipped to building sites. Cast-iron facades enabled taller buildings before steel-frame construction became standard. Some survive in the West Bottoms.

Windows, Columns & Openings

Pilaster

A flat, rectangular column attached to a wall — essentially a column that doesn't actually carry structural load but reads as if it does. Pilasters are a primary way to articulate vertical rhythm on a facade.

Column Orders (Doric, Ionic, Corinthian)

Doric: plainest, sturdiest, with a simple capital. Ionic: scrolled (volute) capitals, more slender proportions. Corinthian: leafy (acanthus) capitals, most ornamental. American neoclassical buildings use all three; Kansas City's Beaux Arts civic buildings tend toward Ionic and Corinthian.

Fenestration

The overall pattern of windows across a facade. “The fenestration” is how an architect describes the rhythm and spacing of a building's openings — paired windows, triple-hung sashes, punched windows, ribbon windows.

Bay Window

A window projecting outward from the plane of a wall, usually with three or more panes forming a multi-sided bay. Common on Victorian and Queen Anne houses.

Arched Windows (Semicircular / Segmental / Round)

A semicircular arch is a half-circle. A segmental arch is a shallower curve (less than a half-circle). A round arch or Romanesque arch is semicircular. Beaux Arts buildings use semicircular arches at grand entrances; Romanesque Revival buildings use them for everything.

Structural & Organizational Terms

Façade / Elevation

The face of a building; elevation is the technical/architectural term for any one side of the building as drawn in two dimensions. A building has four elevations (north, south, east, west); the “main elevation” is usually the most prominent one.

Bay (Structural)

The repeating structural unit of a facade — usually defined by the spacing of columns or pilasters. A building might be “five bays wide” meaning there are five repetitions of the structural module across its front.

Cupola

A small dome-like structure on top of a building, usually decorative. Courthouses and town halls often have cupolas as a civic signal.

Belvedere

An architectural structure designed to command a view — literally Italian for “beautiful view.” Penthouses, rooftop pavilions, and terraces oriented to overlook the surrounding landscape.

Style-Specific Terms

Art Deco: Zigzag, Streamline, Ziggurat

Zigzag Moderne: the angular, geometric Deco of the 1920s — chevrons, sunbursts, stepped forms. Streamline Moderne: the later, rounder Deco of the 1930s — horizontal bands, curves, speedlines. Ziggurat massing: stepped setbacks resembling Mesopotamian pyramids.

Spanish Colonial: Loggia, Mission Parapet, Red Tile Roof

Loggia: an open-sided arcade or gallery, often on an upper floor. Mission parapet: the curved, shaped gable end characteristic of Spanish mission architecture. Red clay tile roof: the signature Spanish Colonial roofing material, which Nichols imported for the Plaza.

Beaux Arts: Grand Staircase, Piano Nobile, Sculpted Pediment

Grand staircase: a ceremonial ascending stair, often with a double-return landing. Piano nobile: the “noble floor” — usually the second level of a Beaux Arts building where the grand public rooms are located. Sculpted pediment: a pediment containing relief sculpture rather than being purely geometric.
