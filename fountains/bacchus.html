Fountain
of Bacchus

Bacchus with his grape cluster raised, set into a corner of the Plaza streetscape. One walker passes, one sits on the bench — Nichols believed public sculpture should be discovered rather than announced.

Subject Bacchus, God of Wine
Origin Antique European bronze
Installed 1920s
Material Bronze
Location Country Club Plaza
◆

A Roman God in Missouri

Bacchus — the Roman god of wine, festivity, and the pleasures of the vineyard — was a fitting subject for a European antique placed in the Country Club Plaza district. J.C. Nichols spent years traveling through Europe acquiring sculpture and decorative elements for Kansas City's boulevard system and his commercial district, and Bacchus arrived as part of that deliberate campaign to furnish the Plaza's streetscape with works that carried the cultural weight of the Old World. A jovial, well-fed figure — the classical Bacchus is usually depicted wreathed in grape leaves, cup raised — he brought a note of warmth and levity to the district's otherwise more formal Spanish Colonial setting.

The gesture was characteristically Nichols. The Country Club Plaza was designed from the start to evoke the grandeur of Seville — its towers modeled on the Giralda, its tile panels depicting Andalusian scenes — and Nichols understood that the sculptural program of a European city was as important to its character as its buildings. Rome, Seville, Florence: their piazzas and street corners were punctuated by bronze figures of gods, emperors, and allegorical personages, and Nichols intended his American shopping district to have the same quality of animated, peopled urban space. Bacchus, set into the Plaza's streetscape, was a piece of that intention.

◆

The Plaza's Sculptural Program

The Bacchus fountain is best understood as one element in a larger sculptural strategy that Nichols pursued throughout the Plaza's development. The J.C. Nichols Memorial Fountain at the northern gateway — four equestrian figures representing the world's great rivers — established the scale of civic ambition. The smaller pieces scattered through the Plaza's interior, including Bacchus, worked at a more intimate register: the discovered figure around a corner, the bronze that catches your eye as you pass through an arcade. Together they created a streetscape that was never quite empty of interest.

This is an urbanism that has largely fallen out of fashion. Contemporary commercial development rarely commissions or installs sculpture; the economics don't support it and the culture doesn't demand it. The Country Club Plaza's sculptural density is therefore not only aesthetically distinctive but historically remarkable — a record of a developer who believed that the quality of the public realm was inseparable from the commercial value of the development, and who spent accordingly. Bacchus, grinning in his corner of the Plaza, is a small piece of evidence for that belief.
