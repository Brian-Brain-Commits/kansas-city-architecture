Installed 1960
Sculptor Henri-Léon Gréber

Material Bronze

◆

From Long Island to Kansas City

The J.C. Nichols Memorial Fountain has an unusual provenance for a Kansas City landmark: it began its life on a Long Island estate. The four-horse sculptural group was created in 1910 by the French sculptor Henri-Léon Gréber for Clarence Mackay, heir to the Comstock Lode silver fortune, for his Harbor Hill estate in Roslyn, New York. Gréber was a celebrated Beaux-Arts sculptor whose work ornamented public spaces and private estates on both sides of the Atlantic.

When the Mackay estate was dispersed in the late 1950s, Miller Nichols — son of Country Club Plaza developer J.C. Nichols and himself a committed civic philanthropist — recognized the fountain as the perfect memorial to his father. He acquired the sculpture and commissioned its installation at the eastern edge of Mill Creek Park, at the very gateway to the Country Club Plaza that his father had created. The fountain was dedicated in 1960, thirteen years after J.C. Nichols's death.

◆

The Sculpture

The fountain's central feature is four bronze horsemen, larger than life, riding outward from a central basin toward the four cardinal points. Each figure represents one of the great rivers of the world: their poses, their horses' forms, and the aquatic creatures they ride all differ, giving each composition its own character and narrative. The riders are not peaceful — they surge forward with the kinetic energy characteristic of Beaux-Arts sculpture at its finest.

Below the horsemen, in the lower basin, naturalistic sculptural groups represent aquatic life: alligators, turtles, and fish locked in dynamic struggle, their forms writhing in the bronze with extraordinary technical accomplishment. These lower groups are often overlooked by visitors focused on the dramatic upper figures — which is precisely why they reward the effort of close inspection. Gréber was a sculptor of rare gifts, and every surface of this fountain demonstrates why.

A Plaza Icon

No fountain in Kansas City is more recognized or more beloved than the J.C. Nichols Memorial Fountain. It operates seasonally — typically from spring through fall — when its jets of water combine with Gréber's bronze figures to create one of the most magnificent public spectacles in the American Midwest. In the morning light, water catches the sun in a thousand separate arcs. At dusk, the bronze horsemen glow gold against the Plaza's Spanish towers.

The fountain's location — at the threshold between the residential streets of the Country Club Plaza neighborhood and the commercial district itself — gives it an unusually powerful civic role. It is simultaneously a landmark, a gathering place, a memorial to one of Kansas City's most consequential figures, and a work of art that would be remarkable in any public space in the world. That Kansas City has it is a measure of the city's extraordinary cultural ambition.
