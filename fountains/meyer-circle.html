Meyer Circle
Sea Horse Fountain

The Meyer Circle fountain at quiet hour. Four bronze sea horses around a Venetian pedestal, the Ward Parkway mansions beyond, and the boulevard paths threading between the trees that frame this traffic circle a mile south of the Plaza.

Installed Early 1920s
Origin 17th-C. Venice
Acquired by J.C. Nichols
Material Bronze
Location Ward Pkwy & Meyer Blvd
◆

A Venetian Treasure

The Meyer Circle Sea Horse Fountain is one of the oldest artifacts in the Kansas City public realm. The original bronze sculpture dates to the 17th century, when it was cast in Venice as part of that city's rich tradition of public and private fountain sculpture. Venice's bronze-working tradition — rooted in the techniques and guild structures of the Renaissance — produced works of exceptional technical and expressive quality, and this seahorse piece is a fine representative of that heritage.

J.C. Nichols encountered the piece during his travels in Europe in the early 1920s and purchased it for installation in Kansas City's growing boulevard system. The acquisition was characteristic of Nichols's approach to civic beautification: seek out genuine European artworks of quality, bring them to Kansas City, and place them in prominent public locations where thousands of residents would encounter them daily. The Meyer Circle fountain was installed at the Ward Parkway traffic circle at Meyer Boulevard, one of the parkway's most visible intersections, where it became an immediate and enduring landmark.

◆

The Seahorse in Baroque Sculpture

The seahorse — in classical mythology, the hippocampus, horse of Neptune and mount of the sea-gods — was a favored subject of Baroque sculptors working in Venice and throughout the Mediterranean world. The creature's dual nature, combining the muscular foreparts of a horse with the sinuous tail of a fish, gave sculptors the opportunity to express simultaneously the power of the horse and the grace of aquatic movement. The result, when realized in bronze by a skilled Venetian craftsman, is a form that seems perpetually on the point of motion — coiled, tensed, alive.

The Meyer Circle piece achieves this quality. Modest in scale compared to the grandeur of the J.C. Nichols Memorial Fountain a mile to the north, it has instead an intimacy and refinement that rewards careful looking. It is the kind of sculpture that improves with familiarity — revealing new details of surface treatment and compositional subtlety each time it is examined.

Ward Parkway's Crown Jewel

The Meyer Circle fountain cannot be understood apart from Ward Parkway, one of the finest examples of American boulevard planning. Designed as part of the comprehensive Kansas City boulevard system — a network of tree-lined parkways begun in the 1890s and continued through the 1920s — Ward Parkway runs southward from the Country Club Plaza through the city's most affluent residential neighborhoods. Its median is planted with trees and ornamental shrubs; at key intersections, sculptures and fountains give the boulevard its distinctive civic character.

The Meyer Circle Sea Horse Fountain is the crown jewel of this system. Thousands of Kansas Citians drive past it daily — many pausing in the traffic circle to admire it, some returning on foot to examine it closely. It represents an urbanism that believed in the capacity of beautiful objects, placed in beautiful settings, to enrich the daily experience of city life. That belief is the animating philosophy behind everything J.C. Nichols built and collected.
