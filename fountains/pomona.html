Ward Parkway, Kansas City • Installed 1920s

Pomona
Fountain

Sculptor Donatello Gabbrielli
Cast c. 1910, Italy
Installed 1920s
Material Bronze
Location Ward Pkwy near Loose Park
◆

Goddess of the Orchard

Pomona was the Roman goddess of fruit trees, orchards, and abundance — the deity who presided over the cultivation of growing things and the turning of the seasons toward harvest. In Roman mythology she was courted by numerous woodland gods, but chose Vertumnus, the god of seasons and change, as her consort: a pairing that made symbolic sense for a goddess whose domain was the patient transformation of blossoms into fruit. The name survives in English in "pomme" — the French word for apple — and in the city of Pomona, California, named for its fruit-growing heritage.

The bronze figure installed along Ward Parkway was cast by the Italian sculptor Donatello Gabbrielli around 1910, in the tradition of Italian Renaissance Revival bronzework that was then in fashionable demand across Europe and America. J.C. Nichols acquired the piece during his travels in Europe in the early 1920s — one of several European sculptures he purchased for installation along Kansas City's developing boulevard system — and placed it along the Ward Parkway median near Loose Park, where it has stood ever since as one of the parkway's quieter but most graceful ornaments.

◆

Ward Parkway and the Nichols Vision

The Pomona Fountain makes most sense when understood in the context of Ward Parkway as a whole. J.C. Nichols did not think of the boulevards he built as merely transportation infrastructure — he thought of them as the civic framework of a neighborhood, as places where residents would move slowly, notice their surroundings, and encounter works of beauty in the course of daily life. The sculptural program of Ward Parkway — the Meyer Circle Sea Horse Fountain to the north, Pomona near Loose Park, the Diana and Cherubs Fountain further south — was a deliberate composition, each piece chosen for its specific location within the parkway's visual rhythm.

Pomona, as a goddess of fruitfulness and cultivation, was a particularly apt choice for a parkway lined with the landscaped gardens of prosperous families — the people who had built the Country Club District and who drove Ward Parkway daily between their homes and the Plaza. Nichols understood that the meaning of a sculpture was not exhausted by its subject matter; it was also shaped by its placement, its scale relative to its surroundings, and the manner in which it was encountered. A figure of abundance placed among the lawns and gardens of a prosperous residential corridor was a statement — quiet, graceful, and legible — about the values of the place it inhabited.
