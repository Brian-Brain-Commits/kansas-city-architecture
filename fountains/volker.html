Mr. Kansas City

William Volker came to Kansas City from Germany in the 1880s with little capital and enormous energy. He built a hugely successful wholesale house goods business — one of the largest in the Midwest — and by the early twentieth century was one of Kansas City's wealthiest citizens. But Volker's relationship to his wealth was profoundly unusual. He was constitutionally incapable of ostentation. He lived simply, gave generously, and made a point of ensuring that his philanthropy was anonymous whenever possible.

His giving was not occasional or sentimental — it was systematic, strategic, and enormous in scale. Volker's philanthropic gifts funded the University of Kansas City (now the University of Missouri-Kansas City), the Kansas City Museum, and were among the founding contributions to the Nelson-Atkins Museum of Art. He funded social service organizations, neighborhood improvement projects, and park improvements — often paying for civic projects directly when city bureaucracy moved too slowly, then donating the results to the city. He became known as "Mr. Kansas City" — not for his wealth, but for what he did with it.

◆

A Monument in Water

The William Volker Memorial Fountain was installed on Volker Boulevard in 1958, eleven years after his death. The fountain takes the form of a cascading waterfall — water descending in stages over a terraced stone structure, a form that evokes the natural waterfalls of the Missouri landscape while remaining entirely architectural in its composition. Jets of water arc across the terraces before gathering in a lower basin; the sound of falling water reaches the surrounding parkway, a constant presence for pedestrians and cyclists on Volker Boulevard.

The fountain is modest in scale and unpretentious in form — which makes it an entirely appropriate memorial to its subject. William Volker avoided the grandiose; he would have been uncomfortable with a monument too showy or too conspicuous. What the Volker fountain offers instead is something more like his own philanthropy: a genuine, ongoing contribution to the quality of public space that enriches the lives of people who may not even know whose name it bears.

Volker Boulevard

The fountain's setting on Volker Boulevard connects it to the urban planning legacy that shaped this part of Kansas City. Volker Boulevard is one of the great tree-lined parkways of the city's boulevard system, running east-west through the Midtown neighborhood near the UMKC campus — the institution whose development Volker funded and that stands nearby as a lasting expression of his vision for Kansas City's intellectual life.

The Nelson-Atkins Museum of Art is also within view of the boulevard — another institution that Volker helped create. Walking or cycling along Volker Boulevard near the fountain is to be in the presence, simultaneously, of several of his greatest gifts to the city. Few memorials are so well placed to make their subject's legacy tangible and present. The fountain does not need to be large to carry this weight — it only needs to be here, in this place, where everything around it tells the story of the man it honors.
