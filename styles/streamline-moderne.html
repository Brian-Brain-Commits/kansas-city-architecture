Building Through the Depression

Streamline Moderne is the architecture of the 1930s — shaped by the Depression, aerodynamics, and the machine aesthetic that replaced Art Deco's ornamental geometry with something faster, smoother, and more fundamentally modern. Where Art Deco borrowed from ancient Egypt and pre-Columbian America, Streamline Moderne borrowed from wind tunnels and ocean liners: horizontal banding, curved corners, flush surfaces, and the visual language of speed applied to buildings that would never move an inch.

Kansas City had an unusual advantage in building Streamline Moderne at scale: Boss Tom Pendergast's Ten-Year Bond Plan, passed in 1931, put $40 million into public construction at the exact moment the Depression was halting construction everywhere else. The result was a wave of public buildings — the Municipal Auditorium, City Hall, the Jackson County Courthouse, the Police Headquarters, the Municipal Court — that gave downtown Kansas City one of the most coherent Depression-era civic building campaigns in the United States. The Architectural Record called the Municipal Auditorium, opened in 1935, one of the ten best buildings in the world.

The Municipal Auditorium

The Municipal Auditorium at 301 West 13th Street remains the defining Streamline Moderne civic monument in Kansas City. Designed by Gentry, Voskamp & Neville with Hoit, Price & Barnes handling mechanical systems, and opened in 1935 at a cost of $6.5 million, it contains a 7,300-seat arena, a 2,400-seat Music Hall with an extraordinary Art Deco chandelier, and a 750-seat Little Theatre with bas-relief wall decoration throughout. Princeton Architectural Press later named it one of the 500 most important architectural works in the United States.

The building's exterior is defined by its massive limestone facade — a horizontal composition of setback planes and the famous central medallion whose geometric relief ornament distills the whole aesthetic into a single image. Inside, the Music Hall's chandelier is one of the great surviving examples of Art Deco interior decoration in the Midwest: an enormous illuminated pendant in bronze and glass that transforms the hall into something out of a transatlantic liner's first-class saloon, translated into civic scale.

What Makes It Streamline Moderne

Streamline Moderne buildings share a consistent visual grammar that distinguishes them from the Art Deco that preceded them. Ornament is compressed — where Deco used projecting carvings and applied decoration, Moderne uses low-relief surface patterns, flush geometric banding, and abstracted friezes that feel almost incised rather than applied. Horizontal lines dominate: windows are grouped in long bands, floors are marked by expressed string courses, and corners are often curved rather than sharp.

The materials shift as well. Polished stone, stainless steel grilles, glass block, and glazed terra cotta replace the carved limestone of the earlier Deco tradition. The overall effect is sleeker, faster-looking, and more explicitly modern — a style that drew its inspiration from industrial design rather than historical precedent. In Kansas City, where the Streamline Moderne buildings were largely government projects built under the Ten-Year Plan, the style carried a specific civic message: that the city was forward-looking, that its institutions were modern, and that the Depression had not broken its ambitions.
