A Style from Another World

Spanish Colonial Revival is an architectural style rooted in the traditions of southern Spain and the Spanish colonial territories of Latin America. It arrived in Kansas City not by geographic proximity or cultural inheritance, but by the deliberate choice of one man: developer Jesse Clyde Nichols, who saw the architecture of Seville on a European tour and decided that his new commercial district south of the city would be built in that style, and no other.

The result — the Country Club Plaza, opened in 1922 — is the most concentrated and ambitious expression of Spanish Colonial Revival architecture in the American Midwest. Its characteristics are unmistakable: red clay roof tiles in the Mediterranean tradition, towers with arcaded belvederes and domed cupolas, hand-painted azulejo ceramic tile panels embedded in facades, wrought iron gates and window grilles, arched colonnades, and a warm palette of terracotta, cream, and cobalt blue drawn directly from the Andalusian tradition.

◆

What Makes It Spanish Colonial

Spanish Colonial Revival architecture draws on several distinct traditions that converged over centuries in southern Spain and across the Spanish empire. The Moorish inheritance — geometric tile work, horseshoe arches, interior courtyards, flowing water — merged with the Renaissance and Baroque traditions of Christian Spain to produce the distinctive Andalusian aesthetic that J.C. Nichols encountered in Seville and sought to transplant to Missouri.

The defining material is the azulejo: hand-painted or glazed ceramic tile in cobalt blue, yellow, terracotta, and white, arranged in geometric or figurative patterns. On the Country Club Plaza, more than 30 large tile murals are embedded in facades, most depicting scenes of Andalusian life. Many were imported directly from Spain; others were produced by American artisans trained in the Sevillian tradition. They remain one of the most extraordinary concentrations of authentic Spanish decorative tile in the United States.

The towers are equally distinctive. Rising above the commercial rooflines, campanile-like structures punctuate the Plaza skyline with domed cupolas and arcaded upper stories modeled directly on the church towers and civic belfries of Andalusia — above all on Seville's Giralda, the 12th-century Moorish minaret that became a Christian bell tower and inspired the Plaza's own signature tower.

Where to Find It

The Country Club Plaza is the overwhelming concentration of Spanish Colonial architecture in Kansas City, and exploring it on foot is the best way to understand the style. Walk any block of the district and you will find tile panels, ironwork, towers, and courtyard spaces that would not look out of place in Seville. The Giralda Tower on 47th Street is the most explicit architectural quotation — a direct homage to the original in Spain.

Beyond the Plaza, Spanish Colonial elements appear in scattered buildings throughout the Midtown and Country Club neighborhoods — apartment buildings and commercial structures from the 1920s that adopted the style Nichols mandated on the Plaza as a local idiom. The tradition endured long enough to become part of KC's visual vocabulary: you can find its traces in everything from private garden walls to Mission-style churches throughout the south of the city.
