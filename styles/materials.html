Reading a Building

Architecture is experienced at two scales simultaneously: the scale of the whole — the massing, the silhouette, the relationship to the street — and the scale of the detail, which reveals itself only when you stop and look closely. Most people navigate cities at the first scale and miss the second entirely. This guide is for the second scale: what Kansas City's buildings are made of, why those materials were chosen, and what to look for when you look up.

The materials Kansas City's architects chose between 1900 and 1940 were not arbitrary. Each had specific properties — visual, structural, economic — that made it suited to its purpose. Terra cotta could be molded into any shape and fired to any color; it was the Art Deco ornament material by default. Limestone conveyed civic permanence. Brick built industrial muscle at a scale that no other material could match for price. Bronze aged beautifully in the open air. Understanding why a material was used is the first step toward reading the building that uses it.

◆

Terra Cotta

Terra cotta — literally "baked earth" in Italian — is fired clay that can be molded into complex three-dimensional forms, glazed to any color, and produced in quantity. It was the defining ornament material of American commercial architecture from roughly 1880 through 1940, and it reached its highest expression in the Art Deco buildings of the late 1920s and 1930s. The KC Power & Light Building is the premier Kansas City example: every square foot of its facade above the limestone base is clad in cream and gold terra cotta panels, modeled in geometric reliefs, sunbursts, and abstracted plant forms that build in density as the tower rises.

The key to reading terra cotta is to understand that it is modular: the ornamental vocabulary of a building is composed from a limited number of custom-designed tile types, repeated and recombined across the facade. Walk the base of a terra cotta building and look for the module — the individual panel or block that keeps repeating — and you begin to understand the grammar of the decoration. On the Power & Light Building, the module is relatively small; on the Municipal Auditorium, it is larger and more architecturally assertive.

Terra cotta was produced by specialist manufacturers who maintained pattern libraries. An architect could order standard ornamental pieces from a catalog, customize them slightly, or commission entirely new designs. The most accomplished Deco buildings — including several in Kansas City — used largely bespoke terra cotta designed specifically for the building, which is why no two great Art Deco facades look quite the same.

Indiana Limestone

Indiana limestone — oolitic limestone quarried from the Salem Formation in south-central Indiana — was the default cladding material for American civic and commercial architecture from the 1880s through the mid-20th century. Its properties made it nearly ideal for the purpose: it is workable (can be precisely cut and carved), durable (resists weathering and does not stain easily), aesthetically consistent (uniform in color and grain across large quantities), and available in sizes large enough to clad the facades of tall buildings without visible joints.

Kansas City's civic Art Deco buildings — City Hall, the Jackson County Courthouse, the Municipal Auditorium — are all primarily limestone. The material suited the civic Deco vocabulary perfectly: its warm buff color and precise surface allowed the restrained geometric ornament of public buildings to be carved directly into the cladding rather than applied as a separate layer. The result is a building surface that reads as monolithic even though it is assembled from individual limestone blocks — an effect that reinforces the civic values the buildings were designed to project.

What to look for: the relief carvings above entry bays — typically eagle motifs, geometric friezes, and abstracted classical ornament — and the way the limestone turns the building's corners. On well-built limestone buildings, the corner detailing is as precise and considered as the main facade ornament. City Hall's corners are worth studying closely from the street.

Brick

Brick is the material of Kansas City's industrial and residential past — the West Bottoms warehouses, the early commercial buildings of the Crossroads, the bungalows and four-squares that make up the residential fabric of most of the city. Unlike limestone and terra cotta, which were applied as cladding over a structural frame, the brick buildings of the West Bottoms are structurally self-supporting: the walls carry the floor loads, which is why they are so thick at the base and why the window openings get larger as the buildings rise (the upper floors carry less load).

The brick in the West Bottoms warehouse district is not uniform. Different buildings used different clays and firing temperatures, producing brick that ranges from warm orange-red to deep brown, from smooth to heavily textured. Walking the warehouse district, you can read the different eras of construction partly through the brick — the older buildings tend toward rougher, more varied surfaces; the later ones are more consistent. The cornices and decorative brickwork at the top of the facade — corbeling, soldier courses, ornamental panels — are worth looking up at: even strictly utilitarian industrial buildings typically received some ornamental treatment at their crowns.

Bronze

Bronze is the material of Kansas City's fountain program — and of its finest architectural hardware. The J.C. Nichols Memorial Fountain's four equestrian figures, the Fountain of Bacchus, the Pomona Fountain, the Meyer Circle Sea Horses: all bronze, all acquired by Nichols in Europe, all placed throughout the city's boulevard system and shopping district as a deliberate act of civic embellishment.

Bronze ages in a way that no other common material does. Freshly cast, it is a warm gold-brown. Over decades of exposure to weather, it develops a patina — the characteristic blue-green oxidation of the surface — that can be accelerated or retarded depending on treatment. Kansas City's outdoor bronzes have developed their patinas over 80 to 100 years; the result is a material surface that reads as simultaneously ancient and present, the color of weathered copper that carries the visual weight of time.

Beyond the fountains, look for bronze hardware on the Art Deco and Beaux Arts buildings: elevator doors, mailboxes, entry grilles, window frames. The KC Power & Light Building's lobby bronzework is some of the finest surviving Art Deco metalwork in Kansas City. Union Station's hardware is Beaux Arts in character — more restrained, heavier in section — and still intact throughout the Grand Hall.

Talavera Tile

The Country Club Plaza's defining surface detail is not the limestone towers or the wrought iron railings — it is the ceramic tile panels embedded in the facades of virtually every building in the district. These are hand-painted panels in the Talavera tradition: the polychrome tin-glazed ceramics produced in Talavera de la Reina, Spain and in Puebla, Mexico since the 16th century, characterized by bold geometric borders, painted figurative scenes, and the distinctive blue, yellow, orange, and white palette of the original Spanish craft.

J.C. Nichols commissioned hundreds of these panels for the Plaza's facades — enough that walking the district, you encounter a new one every few feet. They depict Andalusian scenes, coats of arms, floral motifs, and architectural vignettes: a visual encyclopedia of the Spanish Colonial world that Nichols was building in miniature in Kansas City. Each panel is different. Slowing down to read them is one of the specific pleasures the Plaza rewards.

Talavera tile also appears in the decorative fountain basins throughout the Plaza district — the colored tile edging around fountain pools and the mosaic work at the base of several of the smaller fountain figures. The Plaza's relationship to its water features is inseparable from its relationship to its tile: both are part of the same systematic effort to give the district the visual density and material richness of the Spanish city it was imitating.

Glass & Steel

Kansas City's 20th-century modernist buildings use glass and steel in ways that mark a deliberate break from the ornamental traditions of the earlier buildings. The Nelson-Atkins Museum's Bloch Building addition (2007), by Steven Holl, uses glass lenses set into an earth berm — buildings that are partly below grade and illuminated from above, so that the museum's addition appears to grow out of the landscape rather than stand above it. At night, when the lenses glow, the effect is of a building that has dissolved its wall and become light.

The Kauffman Center for the Performing Arts (2011), by Moshe Safdie, uses steel in a structural expressionist mode: the curved steel ribs of the shell are the building's primary visual statement, a structural system that became its own ornament. This is the opposite of the Art Deco approach — where structure is hidden behind cladding and ornamentation applied to a surface — but it follows the same logic: the building's material character is the carrier of its architectural meaning.
