The Heart of the Skyline

Downtown Kansas City is the urban core that the Ten-Year Plan built — a compact grid of Art Deco towers, civic buildings, and commercial blocks that rose in a single extraordinary decade and have defined the city's skyline ever since. The area bounded roughly by the Missouri River to the north, I-35 to the east, I-670 to the south, and I-35/I-70 to the west contains the densest concentration of architectural landmarks in the city.

Walking downtown is a genuine architectural experience. The Kansas City Power & Light Building (1931), the Fidelity Bank Building (1931), City Hall (1937), Municipal Auditorium (1935), and dozens of smaller commercial buildings from the same era line the streets in the kind of stylistic unity that results from compressed, politically coordinated construction. Almost everything here was built during the same decade, and it shows — in the best possible way.

◆

What to See

A walking tour of downtown Kansas City should begin at the intersection of 12th Street and Grand Boulevard — the civic center of the 1930s city, where City Hall and Municipal Auditorium face each other across a public space. Both buildings reward close examination: the terracotta ornament, the setback profiles, the way the buildings meet the street.

From there, walk east on 12th toward Baltimore Avenue. The view south along Baltimore on a clear day — the KC P&L Building and the Fidelity Bank Building rising above the commercial buildings of the financial district — is one of the great urban views in the Midwest. The skyline looks almost exactly as it did in 1937, when all of these buildings were newly built and Kansas City was announcing itself as a serious American city.

The Power & Light District entertainment area, developed in the 2000s around the original KC P&L Building, has brought new energy to the block north of the skyscraper. The building's lobby is generally accessible during business hours and is worth seeing for its original Art Deco interior detailing.
