Where the Tracks Met the Town

The Crossroads Arts District occupies the former industrial and warehouse district that grew up immediately south and southeast of Union Station in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. When Kansas City was the cattle and rail capital of the Midwest, this was where the commerce happened: wholesale warehouses, cold storage facilities, printing companies, light manufacturing plants, and the businesses that supplied the railroad economy occupied the blocks between Union Station and the near east side.

The buildings from this era are industrial in character — brick construction, large floor plates, freight elevators, loading docks at street level — but built with the craftsmanship that characterized early 20th-century commercial construction. The brick is textured; the cornices are elaborate; the proportions are generous. These are not beautiful buildings in the way the Art Deco towers of downtown are beautiful, but they have a solidity and a material richness that makes them excellent candidates for adaptive reuse.

The Arts District

The Crossroads began its transformation into an arts district in the 1990s, when artists and creative businesses began occupying the vacant warehouse spaces near Union Station. Low rents, large floor plates, and generous natural light made the old industrial buildings ideal studios and galleries. The First Friday gallery walk — held on the first Friday of each month — became one of the social institutions of Kansas City, drawing thousands of residents to see art, eat at the growing concentration of restaurants, and walk the neighborhood's streets.

The Crossroads today is one of the most successful arts district transformations in the Midwest. Galleries, studios, restaurants, and creative businesses occupy virtually every block. The neighborhood has also attracted new residential development, some of it in the form of loft conversions of historic warehouse buildings and some in newly constructed mixed-use projects. The tension between the neighborhood's industrial character and its growing upscale identity is ongoing — and typical of successful urban arts districts everywhere.

Union Station as Anchor

Union Station's 1999 restoration transformed not just the building but the entire neighborhood around it. The station's renovation — and its repositioning as a cultural and event destination — created a magnet that attracted further investment to the blocks immediately surrounding it. The Liberty Memorial and National World War I Museum, immediately south of Union Station, form an extraordinary civic complex: the Beaux Arts terminal facing the Art Deco memorial tower across a great open lawn, with the downtown skyline rising behind.

This view — Union Station in the foreground, the memorial tower rising behind it, the Art Deco skyscrapers of downtown completing the background — is one of the essential Kansas City views and one of the reasons the city rewards looking up. Everything about the city's architectural history is visible in that single panorama: the Beaux Arts ambition of 1914, the civic memorial tradition of the 1920s, the Art Deco tower-building of the 1930s, all in one frame.
