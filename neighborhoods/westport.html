The Oldest Corner in Kansas City

Westport Road and Pennsylvania Avenue is the oldest commercial intersection in Kansas City — the center of a trading post town that existed before Kansas City did. John Calvin McCoy laid out the settlement here in 1833, on a high limestone ridge four miles south of the Missouri River, and for the next twenty years it was the busiest outfitting crossroads in America: the place where wagons assembled, supplies were loaded, and the Santa Fe, Oregon, and California Trails began in earnest.

The terrain that made Westport a trail gateway — elevated, well-drained, sitting above the flood-prone river bottoms — is still visible in the landscape. The streets rise and fall over the same limestone geology that McCoy chose in 1833. The buildings that line Westport Road today are mostly 20th century, but they sit on ground that has been continuously occupied for nearly two hundred years, and a few of the oldest stone structures in Kansas City survive in their midst.

Kelly's and the Harris House

The most visible piece of Westport's architectural history is the stone building at 500 Westport Road that now houses Kelly's Westport Inn. The structure dates to approximately 1855 — built as a dry-goods store during the height of the trail trade — and is one of a small number of pre-Civil War commercial buildings still standing in Kansas City. Its limestone block construction and simple gabled form are typical of the utilitarian commercial architecture of the trail era: built for durability, not ornament, by craftsmen who knew the stone and how to work it.

The Harris House, at 4000 Baltimore Avenue, is the other great survivor of antebellum Westport. Built in 1846 by trader and merchant John Harris, it is one of the oldest standing structures in Kansas City and served as headquarters for Union forces during the Battle of Westport in 1864. The house is a Federal-style two-story structure in cut limestone — modest by any standard, but remarkable for having survived the annexation, the urban renewal era, and the commercial development that consumed most of what once surrounded it.

What Annexation Built

When Westport was annexed by Kansas City in 1897, it brought with it the limestone ridge and the southward development corridor that would transform the metropolitan area. The land immediately south of Westport — rolling terrain along Ward Parkway and the Brush Creek valley — became, within a generation, the Country Club District: J.C. Nichols's masterwork of early 20th-century planned residential development, laid out with curvilinear streets, deed-restricted setbacks, and a coherent landscape vision that influenced American suburb-building for decades.

The Country Club Plaza, opened in 1922 just south of Westport at the north edge of the Brush Creek valley, was the commercial heart of that district. Its Spanish Colonial Revival architecture — tile roofs, stucco facades, tower ornaments modeled on buildings in Seville — gave Kansas City one of its most distinctive urban landscapes. The Plaza exists because Westport's annexation opened the southward corridor; without the 1897 vote, the city's development logic might have pushed in a different direction entirely.

The Neighborhood Today

Modern Westport is Kansas City's principal entertainment and nightlife district — a dense, walkable block of bars, restaurants, music venues, and independent retail compressed along Westport Road between Pennsylvania and Broadway. It is one of the few genuinely pedestrian-scaled commercial streets in Kansas City, where the building scale, sidewalk width, and mix of uses create the kind of street life that the city's more auto-oriented commercial strips cannot replicate.

The architectural character is eclectic — post-war commercial buildings, rehabilitated historic storefronts, a handful of the surviving limestone structures — but the neighborhood's identity is rooted in the continuity of its street life rather than in any single building. Westport has been a gathering place for 190 years. It was the last stop before the frontier; now it is the first stop for a good night out. The limestone underfoot is the same either way.
