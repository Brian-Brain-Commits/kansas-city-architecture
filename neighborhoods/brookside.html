Brookside
Kansas City

Character Residential & Village
Developed 1920s–1940s
Developer J.C. Nichols Co.
Style Tudor & English Revival
Main Street 63rd St & Brookside Blvd

A Neighborhood Designed to Last

Brookside is the part of J.C. Nichols's Country Club District that most clearly demonstrates what Nichols actually meant when he said he intended to "build for permanence." The neighborhood — developed primarily in the 1920s and 1930s, centered on 63rd Street and Brookside Boulevard — was not a subdivision in the modern sense. It was a planned community with a coherent architectural vocabulary, a built-in commercial district scaled to the neighborhood, and a streetscape designed so that the houses, the trees, and the streets would grow together over decades into something that felt inevitable.

Nichols placed Brookside along the former streetcar lines running south from downtown — the transportation infrastructure that made the neighborhood viable before the automobile completed its dominance of American life. The Brookside Shops, a small commercial district along 63rd Street at Brookside Boulevard, was designed as a neighborhood-scale amenity: a butcher, a pharmacy, a hardware store, a small restaurant — the things that make a neighborhood function without requiring a car trip. The architecture of the shops echoes the residential character surrounding them: brick, detailed, built to the street edge, scaled for pedestrians.

◆

The Architecture of the Houses

The residential architecture of Brookside is predominantly Tudor Revival and English cottage in character — steeply pitched rooflines, half-timbering, brick in warm earth tones, casement windows, and irregular massing that gives each house an individuality within a shared vocabulary. Nichols employed architects and developers who worked within a set of design guidelines — not a rigid code, but a coherent sensibility — that prevented the architectural chaos common in speculative suburban development of the same era.

The setbacks, the lot sizes, the relationship of house to street and garage to house — all were calibrated to produce a streetscape that read as a unified composition while allowing variation house to house. The result is visible today: Brookside's streets have the quality that urban designers spend careers trying to recreate. The trees have grown into canopies that arch over the streets. The houses are clearly of a period — the 1920s and 1930s — but they have not aged in the way that mass-produced residential development ages. They have simply become themselves more fully.

Brookside Shops and the Village Model

The Brookside Shops at 63rd and Brookside Boulevard were among the earliest examples in America of what urban planners would later call a "neighborhood commercial district" — retail designed not to serve a regional market but to serve the walking radius of a single neighborhood. Nichols had studied European market towns on his travels and understood that a neighborhood without its own commercial life was not really a neighborhood but a bedroom for somewhere else.

The shops were designed in the same Tudor Revival vocabulary as the surrounding houses, with brick storefronts, display windows set at grade, and upper-story apartments over the retail space. The district has remained continuously commercial since its development in the 1920s — an unusual achievement for a neighborhood retail district in an era when most such districts were destroyed by suburban strip mall development. Today Brookside's shops house restaurants, independent retailers, and small professional offices that give the neighborhood a degree of daily street life rare in Kansas City's residential south side.

The annual Brookside Art Annual, held in the streets of the commercial district each May since 1971, draws tens of thousands of visitors and is the longest continuously running outdoor art show in Kansas City — a measure of the neighborhood's social capital as much as its physical design.

Brookside and the Nichols Legacy

Brookside cannot be understood apart from the full context of J.C. Nichols's legacy — which means it cannot be understood apart from the restrictive deed covenants that governed who could and could not purchase property in the Country Club District. Brookside was part of that covenant system. The neighborhood's beauty, its coherence, and its commercial vitality were built during decades when a large portion of Kansas City's population was legally excluded from living there.

The Shelley v. Kraemer decision of 1948 made the covenants unenforceable, but the patterns of residential segregation they had established proved more durable than the legal instruments that created them. Brookside today is a more diverse neighborhood than it was in the decades of its development, but the full history of who built it and who was kept out of it is part of what it means to understand what you're looking at when you walk its streets.

What remains, stripped of easy sentiment in either direction, is one of the best-preserved examples of 1920s planned residential development in the United States — a place where the relationship between architecture, urban design, and daily life was worked out with unusual care and has survived largely intact for a century. That achievement is real. So is the history it was built on.
