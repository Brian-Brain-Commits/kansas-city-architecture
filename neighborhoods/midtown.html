The Cultural Corridor

Midtown Kansas City is a broad swath of the city stretching roughly from I-670 in the north to the Country Club Plaza in the south, and from Main Street east to Troost Avenue. It is one of the most culturally and architecturally rich areas of the city — home to the Nelson-Atkins Museum of Art, the University of Missouri-Kansas City, the Kansas City Art Institute, and the bulk of the city's early 20th-century residential architecture.

Volker Boulevard — named for and bearing the fountain memorial of Kansas City's great philanthropist William Volker — runs east-west through the heart of Midtown and connects the UMKC campus to the Nelson-Atkins Museum. Walking or cycling this corridor, you pass through institutions that Volker himself helped create, in a landscape shaped by the boulevard system that defines Kansas City's urban character.

The Nelson-Atkins Museum

The Nelson-Atkins Museum of Art (1933) is one of the finest art museums in the United States and one of the most important buildings in Kansas City. Its neoclassical exterior — broad limestone facades, a grand entrance staircase, Ionic columns — reflects the stripped classical style that bridges Beaux Arts tradition and 1930s modernism. The building was funded largely through the estates of William Rockhill Nelson (founder of the Kansas City Star) and Mary Atkins, with significant contributions from William Volker's philanthropy.

The museum's 2007 Bloch Building addition — by architect Steven Holl — is a spectacular contrast to the original: five glass "lenses" emerging from the ground across the sculpture garden, glowing from within at night. The Bloch Building is one of the most celebrated works of contemporary architecture in the Midwest, and the dialogue between Holl's addition and the 1933 building is one of the most interesting architectural conversations in Kansas City.

What to See

Beyond the Nelson-Atkins, Midtown rewards walking for its residential architecture. The neighborhoods around Armour Boulevard, Janssen Place, and the streets immediately west of the Nelson are lined with early 20th-century houses in Tudor Revival, Colonial Revival, and Craftsman styles — the domestic architecture of the prosperous families who built Kansas City in the boom years.

The 18th and Vine district, at the eastern edge of what is broadly considered Midtown, holds the American Jazz Museum and the Negro Leagues Baseball Museum — both essential to understanding the full history of Kansas City. The rebuilt commercial block on 18th Street is a glimpse of what the district once was, and a reminder of what urban renewal erased.
