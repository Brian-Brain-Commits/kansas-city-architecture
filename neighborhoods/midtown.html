The Cultural Corridor

Midtown Kansas City is a broad swath of the city stretching roughly from I-670 in the north to the Country Club Plaza in the south, and from Main Street east to Troost Avenue. It is one of the most culturally and architecturally rich areas of the city — home to the Nelson-Atkins Museum of Art, the University of Missouri-Kansas City, the Kansas City Art Institute, and the bulk of the city's early 20th-century residential architecture.

Volker Boulevard — named for and bearing the fountain memorial of Kansas City's great philanthropist William Volker — runs east-west through the heart of Midtown and connects the UMKC campus to the Nelson-Atkins Museum. Walking or cycling this corridor, you pass through institutions that Volker himself helped create, in a landscape shaped by the boulevard system that defines Kansas City's urban character.

The Nelson-Atkins Museum

The Nelson-Atkins Museum of Art (1933) is one of the finest art museums in the United States and one of the most important buildings in Kansas City. Its neoclassical exterior — broad limestone facades, a grand entrance staircase, Ionic columns — reflects the stripped classical style that bridges Beaux Arts tradition and 1930s modernism. The building was funded largely through the estates of William Rockhill Nelson (founder of the Kansas City Star) and Mary Atkins, with significant contributions from William Volker's philanthropy.

The museum's 2007 Bloch Building addition — by architect Steven Holl — is a spectacular contrast to the original: five glass "lenses" emerging from the ground across the sculpture garden, glowing from within at night. The Bloch Building is one of the most celebrated works of contemporary architecture in the Midwest, and the dialogue between Holl's addition and the 1933 building is one of the most interesting architectural conversations in Kansas City.

Armour Boulevard and the Residential Fabric

The streets immediately north of the Nelson-Atkins form one of the most intact early twentieth-century residential neighborhoods in Kansas City. Armour Boulevard — a wide, tree-lined parkway running east-west through the district — was platted as part of the boulevard system and anchored by the kind of prosperous domestic architecture that Kansas City produced in its boom decades. The houses along Armour, Janssen Place, and the surrounding streets are predominantly Colonial Revival and Tudor Revival in character: brick, two and three stories, set back from the street behind established lawns, their proportions calibrated to a scale of comfort and permanence that speculative homebuilding rarely achieves.

Janssen Place is a private street — one of several in Midtown developed as gated residential enclaves in the 1890s — with a central landscaped median and a consistent architectural vocabulary that makes it feel like a preserved fragment of the Gilded Age city. The houses are large, the trees are old, and the street itself has the character of a place that was designed to last and has largely done so.

UMKC, KCAI, and the Institutional Fabric

The University of Missouri-Kansas City occupies the core of Midtown's southern half, its campus spreading across the limestone bluffs between Volker Boulevard and the Country Club Plaza. The university's buildings range from the collegiate Gothic revival structures of its founding decades to the institutional modernism of the postwar expansion — a mixed record architecturally, but an essential anchor for the neighborhood's residential and commercial character.

The Kansas City Art Institute, immediately east of the Nelson-Atkins, is the oldest art school in Missouri and one of the older continuously operating art colleges in the United States. Its presence, adjacent to the Nelson-Atkins, makes the stretch of Oak Street and 43rd Street one of the more concentrated clusters of art activity in the Midwest. The relationship between the museum, the art school, and the residential neighborhoods surrounding them has given Midtown a cultural density unusual for a residential district of its scale.

Hospital Hill and the Medical District

The eastern edge of Midtown, along Troost Avenue and 31st Street, is dominated by what Kansas Citians call Hospital Hill — a concentration of medical facilities that includes Truman Medical Center, Children's Mercy Hospital, and the UMKC School of Medicine. This cluster of institutions gives the eastern edge of Midtown a character quite different from the residential and cultural fabric to its west: institutional buildings at large scale, served by surface parking, with the blunt utilitarian character of postwar medical construction.

Hospital Hill also sits at the boundary of Troost Avenue — the demographic dividing line that J.C. Nichols's restrictive covenants reinforced and that has remained one of the most persistent racial and economic boundaries in Kansas City. The neighborhoods east of Troost, historically Black and historically underinvested relative to the west side, are visible from the hospital district in both directions. The medical institutions sit on the dividing line, not quite of either world.

What to See

Midtown rewards walking in a way that most of Kansas City does not. Start at the Nelson-Atkins, walk north to the Volker fountain on Volker Boulevard, then east to see the contrast between the boulevard's well-maintained western stretches and its more contested eastern miles. The streets immediately west of the Nelson — 45th, 46th, 47th — show the residential character at its best: canopy trees, consistent setbacks, houses that have been maintained and cared for across nine decades.

For the full picture of what Midtown was built to be, walk Janssen Place. For what it is now — an active neighborhood that has held its value while the institutions around it have grown and the city around those institutions has changed — walk Armour Boulevard from Main Street east to Troost and pay attention to where the maintenance ends and the disinvestment begins. The boulevard system does not end at Troost. But the quality of upkeep does.
