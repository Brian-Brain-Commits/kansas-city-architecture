Where Kansas City Was Born

The West Bottoms is the low-lying floodplain where the Kansas River meets the Missouri — the geography that made Kansas City possible and the place where its first century of industry was concentrated. Hemmed in by limestone bluffs on three sides and two rivers on the fourth, it was naturally suited to heavy industry: the flat land absorbed stockyards, packing plants, warehouses, and rail yards without the expensive grading work the blufftop required. By the 1880s it was the industrial engine of the city, drawing freight and labor from across the continent.

The Kansas City Stockyards, established at the Bottoms in 1871, grew into the second-largest livestock market in the world — surpassed only by Chicago. On peak days in the 1890s, more than 20,000 head of cattle moved through the yards. The meatpacking houses — Armour, Swift, and others — operated adjacent to the stockyards in a continuous belt of industrial processing that employed tens of thousands and produced the wealth that financed the civic ambitions of downtown.

The Livestock Exchange Building

The Kansas City Livestock Exchange Building (1910), still standing at 1600 Genessee Street, is the civic monument of the West Bottoms — a ten-story Neoclassical structure that served as the administrative headquarters of the stockyards trade and the commercial exchange where cattle prices were set and transactions recorded. Its buff-brick facade, Ionic colonnade, and mansard-roofed crown gave the building the scale and dignity of a civic institution — which, in the economy of early Kansas City, it effectively was.

The Livestock Exchange anchored a district of supporting commercial buildings: hotels for cattle buyers, saloons and restaurants for workers, commission houses, and livestock banks. The streets around the Exchange were the financial district of the beef trade — as consequential to 1890s Kansas City as Wall Street was to New York, and nearly as specialized. The buildings that survive from this era are among the most intact late-19th-century commercial streetscapes remaining in Missouri.

The 1951 Flood and What Followed

On July 13, 1951, the Kansas River crested at 36.9 feet — nearly twenty feet above flood stage — and inundated the West Bottoms under as much as 24 feet of water. The flood killed 19 people in the Kansas City area, destroyed 2,500 homes, and caused $870 million in damage (1951 dollars) across the Kansas and Missouri river valleys. For the West Bottoms, it was the event that ended the industrial era.

The meatpacking plants and many of the stockyard facilities never fully recovered. The industry that had made the district did not rebuild on the same scale — it relocated to less flood-prone sites or simply declined as refrigerated trucking made urban packing plants obsolete. By the 1960s, the West Bottoms was largely abandoned as an active industrial district, its vast brick warehouses emptied of commerce and slowly decaying.

The Bottoms Today

The West Bottoms never fully redeveloped — and that is part of what makes it one of Kansas City's most architecturally compelling places. The warehouses that survived the flood and the decades of abandonment now house antique dealers, event venues, artists, and small businesses in one of the most atmospheric settings in the city. On the first weekend of each month, the district's antique markets draw thousands of visitors into buildings that have seen the full arc of the city's industrial history.

The architecture rewards attention. The pressed-brick facades of the 1880s and 1890s warehouses — Romanesque Revival arches, corbeled cornices, cast-iron storefront columns — were built for permanence and carry the ornamental ambition of an era that did not separate industrial and aesthetic concerns. The district also offers the best ground-level views of the downtown skyline: standing in the Bottoms and looking east toward the bluffs, you see the city rising above you exactly as it has for 150 years, the limestone geology that placed the landing here still visible in the exposed cuts along the bluff face.
