A City That Built to Last

Kansas City's architectural story unfolds in three distinct chapters: a downtown skyline of extraordinary coherence born from a single decade of civic ambition, a modern era of second-wave boldness, and a century of sports venues that mark the city's evolving civic identity. Each era has a character of its own — and all of it is still standing.

1900s – 1940s

Boom Era

The Ten-Year Plan, the Pendergast machine, and a decade of national prosperity combined to build a downtown skyline of limestone towers that still defines Kansas City.

1970s – Present

Modern Era

After decades of suburban flight, Kansas City resumed building at civic scale — a Moshe Safdie concert hall, a convention center that spans an interstate, and an arena built on faith alone.

Stadiums

An Underrated Skyline

The Kansas City skyline was largely built in a single extraordinary decade — the 1930s — when a Depression-era bond program, a powerful political machine, and a city's determination to prove itself produced a concentrated cluster of Art Deco towers that still defines downtown. The Power & Light Building (1931), City Hall (1937), the Municipal Auditorium (1935), and a dozen surrounding structures were all built within a fifteen-year window that has no parallel in the city's history. The coherence of the result is not accidental: it was planned, funded, and executed as a civic statement.

The modern era added a different kind of ambition — Moshe Safdie's Kauffman Center (2011), T-Mobile Center, and the first stadium purpose-built for a women's professional sports team. Kansas City has never stopped building at civic scale. The full catalogue is below.

All Buildings — Chronological

Every building covered on this site, in order of completion.

Union Station

1914 • Beaux Arts

The second-largest train station in America at opening — a limestone Beaux Arts monument that anchored downtown's southern edge for a century.

Midland Theatre

1927 • Atmospheric Theater

The finest surviving movie palace in Kansas City — a Rapp & Rapp design with a Spanish Baroque interior of extraordinary richness.

KC Power & Light Building

1931 • Art Deco • 476 ft

The crown of KC's Art Deco skyline — 36 floors of limestone and bronze, its illuminated top visible from miles across the plain.

Fidelity National Bank Building

1931 • Art Deco • 30 Floors

Known today as 909 Walnut — a 30-story limestone tower that anchors the Art Deco civic ensemble at 9th and Walnut.

Nelson-Atkins Museum

1933 • Beaux Arts • Wight & Wight

One of the great American art museums — a Beaux Arts limestone temple on Oak Street with a striking 2007 addition by Steven Holl.

Jackson County Courthouse

1934 • Art Deco • Truman's Courthouse

Harry Truman's workplace as presiding judge — part of the civic campus built under the Ten-Year Plan, across the street from City Hall.

Municipal Auditorium

1935 • Art Deco

The civic anchor of the Ten-Year Plan — a full-block Art Deco complex that has hosted every type of event from national conventions to circuses.

Kansas City City Hall

1937 • Art Deco • 30 Floors

The 30-story civic capstone of the Pendergast era — the last major public building of the boom, completed just two years before the machine collapsed.

Arrowhead Stadium

1972 • Stadiums

Home of the Kansas City Chiefs — one of the loudest outdoor venues in the world, and the site of FIFA World Cup 2026 matches.

Kauffman Stadium

1973 • Stadiums

One of the last baseball-only stadiums built before the multipurpose era — consistently ranked among the most beautiful ballparks in America.

One Kansas City Place

1988 • Postmodern • 45 Floors

The tallest building in Kansas City at 632 feet — a reflective glass tower that dominates the skyline's upper register from nearly every direction.

Bartle Hall

1994 (expanded) • Contemporary

The convention center that spans an interstate — its space-frame pylons visible from the highway below, a piece of infrastructure that became a landmark.

T-Mobile Center

2007 • Contemporary

Kansas City's downtown arena — built on conviction before any anchor tenant was signed, and proof that a city willing to build attracts the teams to fill it.

Kauffman Center

2011 • Moshe Safdie • Contemporary

The most architecturally ambitious building in Kansas City since City Hall — a sweeping steel shell housing two world-class performance halls.

CPKC Stadium

2024 • Contemporary

The first stadium purpose-built for a women's professional sports team — privately financed, on the Missouri riverfront, a statement about what a city believes.
