A City That Built to Last

Kansas City's architectural story unfolds across two distinct eras of ambition. The first ran from the turn of the century through the 1940s — a period when civic leaders, architects, and private developers built towers, theaters, and civic halls that still define the downtown skyline. The second began in the late twentieth century with a new generation of institutions committed to keeping Kansas City at the forefront of American urban culture.

Together, these buildings make the case that Kansas City is not a city that peaked once and coasted. It has built extraordinary things in multiple eras — and continues to.

◆

Boom Era — 1900s–1940s

The Ten-Year Plan, the Pendergast machine, and a decade of national prosperity combined to produce a downtown skyline of exceptional coherence and quality. These buildings were built to assert civic identity — and they still do.

Union Station

1914 / Restored 1999 • Beaux-Arts

A Beaux-Arts colossus anchoring the southern approach to downtown — once the third-largest train station in America, now a celebrated cultural destination.

Midland Theatre

1927 • French Baroque • 3,600 Seats

An opulent atmospheric theater whose gilded interior transports audiences to a vanished golden age of entertainment and civic pride.

Read More ◆

Kansas City Power & Light Building

1931 • Art Deco • 476 ft

A thirty-six-story tower of limestone and bronze, its crown illuminated nightly — the defining beacon of the KC skyline for half a century.

Read More ◆

Fidelity Bank Building

1931 • Art Deco • 454 ft

Distinguished by its ornate terracotta facade and dramatically setback upper floors — a textbook masterwork of Deco massing.

Read More ◆

Municipal Auditorium

1935 • PWA Moderne • 2,600 Seats

One of the finest civic Art Deco buildings in the nation, born from the city's bold Ten-Year Plan and still hosting major events.

Read More ◆

City Hall

1937 • Art Deco • 443 ft

Thirty floors of Indiana limestone rise in a bold stepped silhouette — the seat of city government and the capstone of the Ten-Year Plan.

Modern Era — 1980s–Present

After decades of suburban flight and urban renewal disruption, Kansas City resumed building at civic scale in the late twentieth century. These buildings represent a second era of architectural ambition — different in vocabulary from the Deco towers, but equally committed to making a mark.

One Kansas City Place

1988 • Postmodern • 623 ft

The city's tallest tower — a 45-story SOM-designed skyscraper whose crown echoes the Deco setbacks around it while claiming the skyline for a new era.

Read More ◆

Kauffman Center for the Performing Arts

2011 • Contemporary • Moshe Safdie

A sweeping shell of glass and stainless steel housing two world-class performance halls — the most architecturally ambitious building in Kansas City since the 1930s.

Read More ◆

H. Roe Bartle Hall

1976 / Expanded 1994 • ~260 ft Pylons

Four concrete super pylons dominate the downtown skyline above an exhibit hall spanning an active interstate — one of the most structurally audacious civic buildings in the Midwest.

Read More ◆

CPKC Stadium

2024 • Generator Studio • 11,500 Seats

The first stadium in the world purpose-built for a women's professional sports team — sited on the Missouri River waterfront with the Bond Bridge as its backdrop.
