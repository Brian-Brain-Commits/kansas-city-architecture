A Kansas City Tradition • Since 1930

Plaza Lights
The Night After Thanksgiving

The switch gets flipped. The city shows up to watch.

Toggle the Plaza lights

The Ceremony

It happens every Thanksgiving Night. Somebody — the mayor, a visiting celebrity, a local kid who won the drawing — counts down and flips a switch. Eighty miles of wire and 280,000 bulbs come on at once. Every arched window and tiled roofline in the Country Club Plaza lights up, and the crowd, which has been building since mid-afternoon, does what crowds do on those rare nights when a city agrees, all at once, to show up for the same thing.

This has been the pattern since 1930. The Plaza has been lit every Thanksgiving Night, for nearly a century, with one wartime pause and one or two weather delays. The lights stay on every night from then until mid-January — six weeks of terra cotta roofs traced in small colored bulbs, their reflection doubled in the black glass of storefront windows and in the pools of the fountains the Plaza keeps running into the cold.

History

The first strand went up in 1930. It was modest — one building, one doorway, the idea of a staff electrician named Cecil Hilburn who had been asked to do something seasonal and improvised a few strings over a Plaza arch. J.C. Nichols saw it, liked it, and the next year there were more. The expansion kept going.

By the 1950s every major building was lit. By the 1970s the event had become a regional ritual — families driving in from Missouri and Kansas farms, crowds filling Mill Creek Park, local television broadcasting the countdown. Today there are roughly 280,000 bulbs across the district, strung along every roofline, arched window, tower, dome, and balcony band. The color mix — red, green, gold, in roughly equal proportion — has been essentially unchanged for forty years.

The ceremony has been cancelled exactly once. In 1963, after President Kennedy's assassination, the Plaza postponed the lighting out of respect for the national mourning. After 9/11 the ceremony went forward, quieter than usual, as a deliberate statement that public life would continue.

What You See

The Plaza's architecture was built to be looked at from the ground up, and the lights reveal what daytime obscures. The arched windows become legible as arches. The red Catalan-style tile roofs, which read as ornamental texture in sunlight, become rhythmic edges in the dark, each tile bump picked out by the horizontal strand running along the gutter. The Giralda replica's belfry, the Mill Creek clock tower, the small Seville Square dome, the wrought-iron balcony bands on the Balcony Building — each becomes an outline drawing of itself.

The effect works at two distances. Up close, you can see the individual colored bulbs and the small irregularities — a bulb missing, a string sagging slightly — that every year's strand picks up. From Brush Creek Boulevard or from the Mill Creek hill, the same strands become a continuous ribbon of colored light wrapping a whole district. Both readings matter.

When to Go

Thanksgiving Night for the ceremony itself — but be ready for crowds. The Plaza fills from around 4 p.m., with peak density from 6:30 through the 7 p.m. lighting. Parking is local-only, so walk from the neighborhoods, or use the shuttle from Crown Center. The ceremony itself takes about fifteen minutes of program and one second of payoff, which is the correct ratio.

Any evening from then through mid-January works for the lights themselves. Clear cold nights after a snowfall are ideal — the snow doubles the light and the quiet doubles the reverence. Brush Creek, walked east toward Main Street after dark, gives the strongest skyline view.
