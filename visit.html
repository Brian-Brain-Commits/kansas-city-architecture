Two Cities in One

Kansas City rewards walking, but it is not a single walkable neighborhood — it is a collection of distinct districts, each with its own character, each worth a dedicated hour or a dedicated afternoon. The two anchors are Downtown and the Country Club Plaza, about three miles apart on the Main Street corridor. Between and around them lie Midtown, the Crossroads, and the boulevard system that threads through everything.

The KC Streetcar runs along Main Street from the River Market in the north through Downtown and south to Union Station — free to ride, frequent, and the easiest way to connect the downtown core to the Crossroads. For everything south of Union Station, you'll want a car or rideshare.

Downtown: The Art Deco Core

Start at the intersection of 12th Street and Grand Boulevard — the civic center of the 1930s city, where City Hall and Municipal Auditorium face each other. Walk east on 12th to Baltimore Avenue, then south. The view down Baltimore toward the KC Power & Light Building and the Fidelity Bank Building rising above the financial district is one of the great urban views in the Midwest. The buildings look almost exactly as they did in 1937.

The KC P&L Building's lobby is open during business hours and worth entering for its original Art Deco interior. City Hall has a public observation deck on the 30th floor with views of the entire city — call ahead to confirm hours. Municipal Auditorium, on 13th Street, is best seen from the street; events occasionally allow interior access.

KC P&L Building 1330 Baltimore Ave
City Hall 414 E. 12th St
Municipal Auditorium 301 W. 13th St

Union Station & the Crossroads

Union Station anchors the southern end of downtown and the northern edge of the Crossroads Arts District. The Grand Hall — restored in 1999 after two decades of abandonment — is one of the great interior spaces in Kansas City: 95 feet of limestone and leaded glass, the scale of a European cathedral, the character of an American city that believed in grand gestures. Science City and the Royals Hall of Fame are inside; the Grand Hall itself is free to enter.

Immediately south of the station, the Liberty Memorial and National World War I Museum form an extraordinary civic complex. Walk up the memorial lawn for the essential Kansas City panorama: Union Station in the foreground, the memorial tower rising behind it, the Art Deco skyscrapers completing the background. Everything about the city's architectural history is visible in that one frame.

The Crossroads Arts District, spreading east and south of Union Station along 18th Street and the surrounding blocks, is best explored on First Friday — the monthly gallery walk held on the first Friday of each month, when hundreds of galleries, studios, and businesses open their doors and thousands of Kansas Citians take to the streets.

Union Station 30 W. Pershing Rd
WWI Museum 2 Memorial Dr
First Friday 18th & Wyandotte area

Midtown: Museums & Boulevards

The Nelson-Atkins Museum of Art is free, open Tuesday through Sunday, and one of the finest art museums in the United States. Its neoclassical 1933 building and the 2007 Steven Holl addition are worth an hour each — and the sculpture garden between them, with Henry Moore bronzes on the lawn and the glass Bloch Building glowing behind, is one of the great outdoor museum experiences in America.

From the Nelson-Atkins, walk west along Volker Boulevard to experience the boulevard system at its best: broad landscaped median, canopy of mature trees, the Volker Memorial Fountain at one end and the UMKC campus anchoring the other. The 18th and Vine district — about a mile east — holds the American Jazz Museum and the Negro Leagues Baseball Museum, both essential to understanding Kansas City.

Nelson-Atkins 4525 Oak St — Free
Jazz Museum 1616 E. 18th St
Negro Leagues Museum 1616 E. 18th St

The Country Club Plaza

The Plaza is the most visitor-friendly district in Kansas City — dense, walkable, and visually rewarding in a way that rewards looking up as much as looking in windows. Start at the J.C. Nichols Memorial Fountain at 47th and Main — one of the most photographed spots in the city — and walk south into the district.

Look for the ceramic tile murals embedded in the facades of Plaza buildings, the wrought iron grilles and balcony railings, and the towers that define the Plaza's Spanish Colonial skyline. The Giralda Tower at 47th and Jefferson is the most prominent — the conscious homage to the great bell tower of Seville Cathedral that gave the Plaza its most distinctive silhouette.

Ward Parkway, running south from the Plaza along a landscaped median, is the grandest of Kansas City's boulevards. Driving or cycling south, you pass the Meyer Circle Sea Horse Fountain (at Meyer Boulevard), the Pomona Fountain (near Loose Park), and a succession of early 20th-century houses that represent the domestic architecture of the city's prosperous boom years.

JC Nichols Fountain 47th & Main St
Giralda Tower 47th & Jefferson St
Loose Park 51st & Wornall Rd

For World Cup Visitors — 2026

Kansas City is a host city for the 2026 FIFA World Cup. If you are visiting for the tournament — or if you are visiting any time and want to understand what makes this city worth looking at — the architecture of the 1920s and 1930s is the place to start. No other American city of comparable size has preserved its interwar skyline with the fidelity that Kansas City has.

The essential Kansas City itinerary for an architecture-minded visitor takes two full days: one for downtown and the Crossroads (with an evening at Union Station and the Liberty Memorial for the view), one for Midtown and the Plaza (with the Nelson-Atkins as the centerpiece and Ward Parkway as the afternoon extension). The boulevards, the fountains, the Art Deco towers, and the Spanish Colonial district are all within a few miles of each other — and all within a city that is genuinely proud of what it built.
