Look Up, Kansas City

Start
Here

Kansas City built one of the great Art Deco skylines in America, a boulevard and fountain system that rivals European capitals, and a jazz tradition that changed American music. This guide covers all of it. Here are three ways in.

◆
I

The Architecture Track

For the reader who wants to understand what Kansas City built and why it looks the way it does

  1. 1
    Art Deco in Kansas City The style, its origins in KC, and why a mid-continent city produced one of America's finest concentrations of it
  2. 2
    Boom Era Buildings — 1900s–40s Union Station, City Hall, the Power & Light Building, the Liberty Memorial — the skyline that emerged from the Ten-Year Plan
  3. 3
    Kansas City Power & Light Building The defining tower of the KC skyline — 36 stories, seven setbacks, an illuminated crown that has been a landmark for nearly a century
  4. 4
    Union Station America's third-largest train station, a Beaux-Arts masterwork, abandoned for decades and now restored — one of the great preservation stories
  5. 5
    Beaux-Arts Style The grand civic vocabulary that built Union Station, the Federal Reserve, and the first generation of KC's monumental architecture
  6. 6
    Materials & Details What Kansas City is built of — limestone, terracotta, glazed brick — and why the choice of material determines what a building looks like in fifty years
◆
II

The History Track

For the reader who wants to understand how Kansas City came to be what it is — the boom, the boss, the losses, and the legacies

  1. 1
    The Founding — 1820s–80s The river landing, the trail trade, and how Kansas City emerged from a competition between rival towns on opposite bluffs
  2. 2
    The Boom — 1920s–30s The Ten-Year Plan, the Art Deco towers, and the improbable civic ambition of a city that decided to build its skyline during the Depression
  3. 3
    The Pendergast Machine Tom Pendergast built the city and stole from it simultaneously — understanding him is essential to understanding the 1930s boom
  4. 4
    The Nichols Legacy J.C. Nichols built the fountains, the boulevard system, and the Country Club District — and the deed covenants that excluded most of the city from it
  5. 5
    Lost Kansas City What fire, flood, and urban renewal took — Convention Hall, the 12th Street corridor, the West Bottoms that existed before 1951
  6. 6
    Urban Renewal & the Modern Era The postwar demolitions, the long restoration of Union Station, and how Kansas City is rebuilding what it lost
◆
III

The Visitor's Track

For the first-time visitor, the returning resident, or anyone who wants to know where to go and what to look for when they get there

  1. 1
    Downtown Start here — the Art Deco core, the financial towers, and the civic monuments that define Kansas City from every approach
  2. 2
    The Country Club Plaza America's first planned automobile shopping district, dressed in Spanish Colonial Revival — still the most architecturally coherent commercial district in the city
  3. 3
    The Fountains Kansas City has more working fountains than any city outside Rome — here is the collection, with notes on what to look for at each one
  4. 4
    18th & Vine The neighborhood that produced Count Basie and Charlie Parker — what was lost, what was preserved, and what the American Jazz Museum holds
  5. 5
    The West Bottoms Industrial Victorian streetscapes, the Livestock Exchange Building, and the best ground-level view of the downtown skyline — open on first weekends
  6. 6
    Walking Tours Self-guided routes through downtown, the Plaza, and the West Bottoms — with specific stops, what to look for at each, and how long each walk takes
