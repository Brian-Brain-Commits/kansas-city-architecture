The Source

When J.C. Nichols traveled through southern Spain in the early 1920s, he was not looking for an architectural style. He was looking for an idea — a vision of what a commercial district could be if it aspired to something more than commerce. He found it in Seville.

Seville is the capital of Andalusia, Spain's southernmost region, and one of the great cities of European history. Founded by the Romans as Hispalis, conquered by the Visigoths, then transformed by eight centuries of Moorish rule under the Umayyad and Almohad caliphates, Seville was reconquered by Christian forces in 1248 and became one of the wealthiest cities in the medieval world — the primary port for Spain's trade with the Americas during the age of exploration. Its wealth built cathedrals, palaces, and plazas that still define the city today.

What Nichols saw in Seville was a city that had achieved something rare: a genuine visual coherence across centuries of construction. The Moorish ornamental tradition, the Gothic and Renaissance architecture of the Reconquista, and the Baroque elaborations of the colonial period all shared a commitment to surface, to color, to the idea that buildings should be beautiful to look at as well as useful. The hand-painted ceramic tiles — azulejos — were everywhere: on facades, in courtyards, on benches, on fountains. They gave the city a warmth and a humanity that Nichols found irresistible.

He brought that idea back to Kansas City. The Country Club Plaza — opened in 1922, the first planned automobile shopping district in America — was his attempt to plant a piece of Seville in the Missouri heartland. Whether he succeeded is for visitors to judge. The ambition was sincere, and the result is unlike anything else in the American Midwest.

The Giralda

No structure in Seville has had more influence on architecture beyond Spain than the Giralda — the great bell tower that rises beside the Cathedral. Its story is one of remarkable cultural layering: a minaret built by Muslim architects that became a Christian bell tower that became one of the most copied towers in the world.

The Giralda was begun in 1184 under the Almohad Caliph Abu Yusuf Ya'qub al-Mansur, as the minaret of the great congregational mosque of Seville. The Almohad architectural tradition valued geometric ornament, careful proportioning, and the integration of architecture with landscape and light. The minaret they built — rising 70 meters in intricate geometric brickwork, with sebka screen decoration in the upper stories — was one of the finest examples of Islamic architecture on the Iberian Peninsula. Its sister tower, the Koutoubia Mosque in Marrakesh, was built at almost the same moment by the same dynasty.

When Seville was reconquered by Christian forces under Ferdinand III of Castile in 1248, the great mosque was demolished to build a cathedral — but the minaret was spared. Its sheer height and beauty made it too valuable to destroy. Christian bells were hung in its upper chamber; a Christian cross was placed where the Islamic crescent had stood. The tower was incorporated into the new cathedral complex.

In the 16th century, a new Renaissance belfry was added to the top of the original minaret structure — a Christian elaboration crowning an Islamic foundation. The bronze weathervane atop this belfry, a figure of Faith that rotates with the wind, gave the tower its modern name: Giralda, from the Spanish word for "weathervane." The tower that stands today is thus simultaneously an Almohad minaret, a Gothic bell tower, and a Renaissance landmark — a three-story palimpsest of Seville's history.

The Azulejo Tradition

The word azulejo comes from the Arabic al-zulayj — small polished stone. The tradition of using tin-glazed ceramic tile as architectural ornament arrived in the Iberian Peninsula with the Moorish occupation, rooted in the tilework traditions of Persia and the Islamic world. In Seville, the tradition found its most elaborate expression: workshops along the Guadalquivir River producing hundreds of tile patterns in cobalt blue, yellow, terracotta, and white, the geometric interlocking designs mathematically precise and visually inexhaustible.

After the Reconquista, the tradition continued under Christian patronage. The Royal Alcazar of Seville — a Moorish palace that became a Christian royal residence — is sheathed in azulejos of extraordinary complexity and beauty. The tiles became a luxury export; Portuguese and Dutch traders brought azulejo techniques to their own markets, and the tradition spread throughout the Mediterranean world and across the Atlantic to the Spanish colonies.

J.C. Nichols understood the azulejo as the defining material of Seville's visual identity. He imported ceramic tile panels directly from Spain for the Country Club Plaza, and he commissioned American artisans trained in the Sevillian tradition to produce additional pieces. More than 30 large tile murals are embedded in Plaza facades, most depicting scenes of Andalusian life. They are not imitations; they are genuine examples of the tradition, transplanted 5,000 miles from their origin and placed in service of a Kansas City merchant's vision of what American cities could be.

Sister Cities: 1980

In 1980 — nearly sixty years after J.C. Nichols opened the Country Club Plaza in deliberate homage to the city of Seville — Kansas City and Seville formalized their relationship as sister cities. The designation recognized what had been true architecturally for decades: that the two cities share a bond of aesthetic and civic aspiration that transcends geography and culture.

The Sister Cities relationship has since included cultural exchanges, educational partnerships, and civic delegations traveling in both directions. Kansas City officials have made formal visits to the Giralda and the great monuments of Seville; Sevillian architects and cultural figures have toured the Country Club Plaza and the city's fountain system. The relationship is one of genuine mutual recognition — Seville acknowledges the compliment that Nichols paid it; Kansas City acknowledges the debt its most famous district owes to Andalusian tradition.

In a deeper sense, the connection between the two cities reflects something important about how culture moves through history. Architecture is one of the primary vehicles by which ideas travel across time and space — a Kansas City developer sees a tower in Seville, decides to build something like it in Missouri, and sixty years later the two cities are formally linked by the decision he made on that journey. The buildings outlast their builders; the connections they encode outlast the buildings themselves.

Seville Today

Seville remains one of Europe's most beautiful and distinctive cities. Its historic center — containing the Cathedral, the Alcazar, the Archive of the Indies, and the old Barrio de Santa Cruz — is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The city of 700,000 receives millions of visitors annually who come to walk the narrow streets of the old quarter, to see the Giralda at golden hour from the Plaza Virgen de los Reyes, and to experience the flamenco tradition, the Semana Santa processional culture, and the Feria de Abril — the spring fair that is one of the most spectacular popular celebrations in Europe.

Its relationship to Kansas City remains unique in the Sister Cities world: few pairings can point to a specific building — the Country Club Plaza's Giralda Tower — as the physical expression of the bond between the two cities. Every time a Kansas Citian looks up at that tower on Forty-Seventh Street, they are looking at Seville. And every scholar who studies Seville's Giralda is, in a small way, also studying something about Kansas City — about the ambition of J.C. Nichols, and the remarkable reach of Andalusian architectural tradition across eight centuries and five thousand miles.
