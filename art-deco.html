What Is Art Deco?

Art Deco emerged in the 1920s as a bold reaction to the organic curves of Art Nouveau. It embraced symmetry, geometry, and the materials of the Machine Age — polished metals, glass, and richly colored stone. Buildings took on a vertical drama, with setbacks that tapered toward the sky and facades encrusted with stylized sunbursts, chevrons, and bas-relief figures.

The movement took its name from the 1925 Exposition Internationale des Arts Décoratifs et Industriels Modernes in Paris — a world's fair that showcased a new aesthetic of luxury, modernity, and optimism. Within a decade, Art Deco had spread across the globe, reshaping not just architecture but fashion, furniture, graphic design, and film. It was the visual language of prosperity, and nowhere did it flourish more enthusiastically than in the booming American cities of the late 1920s.

Key Characteristics

Geometric Ornament

Where Victorian architecture relied on organic curves and naturalistic motifs, Art Deco turned to geometry. Chevrons, zigzags, stepped pyramids, sunbursts, and abstracted floral patterns cover Deco facades in shallow bas-relief, creating surfaces of controlled complexity that reward close inspection while reading cleanly from a distance.

Vertical Emphasis

Art Deco buildings reach upward. The classic Deco skyscraper is defined by its setbacks — floors that step back as the building rises, creating a tiered silhouette that emphasizes height and dynamism. This massing was partly driven by New York's 1916 Zoning Resolution, which required setbacks to allow light to reach the street, but architects turned the constraint into an aesthetic asset of extraordinary power.

Rich Materials

Deco buildings proclaimed their ambition through their materials. Limestone and granite provided the structural backdrop; terra cotta — molded into intricate ornamental panels and glazed in vibrant colors — covered facades with unprecedented decorative richness. Bronze, aluminum, chrome, and glass added gleam and modernity. The effect was of buildings that were simultaneously permanent and dynamic, monumental and precise.

Streamline Moderne

As the 1930s progressed, Art Deco evolved toward a leaner, more horizontal variant known as Streamline Moderne. Inspired by aerodynamics and industrial design, Streamline buildings featured smooth curved corners, horizontal banding, and a machine-age minimalism that shed the dense ornament of earlier Deco in favor of speed and efficiency made visible. Many of Kansas City's New Deal-era public buildings follow this later Deco vocabulary.

PWA Moderne

The Public Works Administration — Franklin Roosevelt's Depression-era jobs program — funded thousands of public buildings across America in the 1930s. These buildings developed their own variant of Deco: bolder, more austere, with monumental scale and simplified ornament appropriate to civic dignity. Kansas City's Municipal Auditorium (1935) and City Hall (1937) are among the finest examples of PWA Moderne in the American Midwest.

The Human Figure

Unlike the purely abstract ornament of later Modernism, Art Deco retained a place for the human figure — rendered in a stylized, elongated manner derived from ancient Egyptian, Aztec, and Classical sources. Bas-relief panels depicting laborers, aviators, allegorical figures, and urban scenes animated the facades of Deco buildings, connecting their abstract geometry to human narrative and civic meaning.

Art Deco in Kansas City

Kansas City's embrace of Art Deco was not accidental — it was the product of deliberate civic policy. In 1926, city manager Henry McElroy and a coalition of business leaders launched the Ten-Year Plan: a $40 million bond program to construct a new city hall, auditorium, police headquarters, courts building, and traffic system. The timing coincided precisely with the height of the Deco era, and the architects commissioned for these buildings — primarily the Kansas City firms of Wight & Wight and Gentry, Voskamp & Neville — delivered work that stands among the finest civic Deco in America.

The private sector followed the city's lead. Banks, insurance companies, and utilities constructed Deco towers throughout the downtown core during the late 1920s and early 1930s, competing in height and ornamental ambition. The result is a downtown skyline that remains one of the most coherent Art Deco environments in the United States — a city that, despite the upheavals of the twentieth century, preserved its golden-age architecture with remarkable fidelity.

◆▲◆▲◆▲◆

Today, Kansas City's Art Deco heritage is recognized by architectural historians and preservationists as a national treasure. The city's concentration of intact Deco buildings — commercial towers, civic halls, theaters, and neighborhood commercial blocks — is matched by few American cities. For anyone interested in the architecture of the interwar period, Kansas City is an essential destination.
