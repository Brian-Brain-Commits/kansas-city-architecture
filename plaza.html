A Vision from Seville

In 1922, developer Jesse Clyde Nichols opened the Country Club Plaza — the first planned shopping district in the United States designed specifically to accommodate the automobile. But Nichols did not envision a utilitarian strip mall. He envisioned a piece of Spain transplanted to the American heartland.

Inspired by his travels through Andalusia and the grandeur of Seville's historic center, Nichols mandated a consistent Spanish Colonial Revival style for every building in the district — hand-painted ceramic tiles, wrought iron grilles, red clay roof tiles, and towers modeled after the great monuments of southern Spain. The result is one of the most distinctive and beloved commercial districts in America, and one of the most ambitious acts of civic placemaking in twentieth-century urban history.

The Architecture of the Plaza

Hand-Painted Ceramic Tile

More than 30 large ceramic tile murals and countless decorative tile panels are embedded in Plaza facades, most depicting scenes of Seville and Andalusian life. Many were imported directly from Spain; others were produced by artisans working in the Sevillian tradition. They remain one of the Plaza's most distinctive and admired features — a living connection to the craft traditions of southern Spain.

Wrought Iron & Bronze Ornament

The Plaza's ironwork — gates, lamp posts, window grilles, and balcony railings — was executed by craftsmen trained in the Spanish blacksmithing tradition. Bronze statuary, including works acquired by Nichols in Europe, punctuates the streetscape at key corners. Every surface was understood as an opportunity for ornament; every ornament was subordinate to the larger architectural composition.

Towers & Rooflines

The Plaza's distinctive silhouette is defined by its towers — campanile-like structures rising above the commercial roofline, punctuating the skyline with domed cupolas and arcaded belvederes. These towers draw directly from the church towers and civic belfries of Andalusia, most explicitly from Seville's Giralda, which inspired the Plaza's own signature tower.

Art Deco Inflections

While Spanish Colonial is the Plaza's dominant vocabulary, the district was built during the height of the Art Deco era, and its influence is visible in details throughout. Geometric ornamental panels, streamlined lettering, and the clean structural massing of several Plaza buildings reveal a Deco sensibility working within — and enriching — the Spanish stylistic framework Nichols required.

Fountains of the Plaza

No element defines the Plaza more than its fountains. Nichols and his successors installed dozens of fountains throughout the district, understanding water as both ornament and atmosphere. The great J.C. Nichols Memorial Fountain at Mill Creek Park greets visitors arriving from the north. Smaller courtyard pieces animate hidden corners throughout the district.

The Seville Light Festival

Each year on Thanksgiving evening, the Plaza is illuminated by more than 200,000 lights outlining its buildings, towers, and rooflines — a tradition begun in 1925. The Country Club Plaza lighting ceremony is one of the oldest holiday traditions in the American Midwest, drawing hundreds of thousands to see the district transformed into a glowing Andalusian fantasy.

The Giralda Connection

The most direct link between the Country Club Plaza and the city of Seville is the Giralda Tower — a conscious homage to the great bell tower of Seville Cathedral. The original Giralda was built as a minaret during the Almohad dynasty in the 12th century, converted to a Christian bell tower after the Reconquista, and crowned with a Renaissance belfry in the 16th century. It stands as one of the most celebrated towers in the world.

J.C. Nichols saw the Giralda on his travels through Andalusia and recognized in it the perfect symbol for his vision of the Plaza: ancient, European, graceful, and unmistakably Spanish. The Plaza's replica tower — rendered in the warm tones of Kansas City rather than the brick of Seville — is a deliberate act of architectural homage, a declaration that this American shopping district aspired to the cultural gravity of one of Europe's great cities.

Explore the Original — Seville, Spain

The Country Club Plaza was born from J.C. Nichols's travels through Andalusia. Explore the city that inspired it all — the Giralda tower, the azulejo tile tradition, the great plazas of southern Spain — and the Sister Cities bond that has connected Kansas City and Seville since 1980.
