A Vision from Seville

In 1922, developer Jesse Clyde Nichols opened the Country Club Plaza — the first planned shopping district in the United States designed specifically to accommodate the automobile. But Nichols did not envision a utilitarian strip mall. He envisioned a piece of Spain transplanted to the American heartland.

Inspired by his travels through Andalusia and the grandeur of Seville's historic center, Nichols mandated a consistent Spanish Colonial Revival style for every building in the district — hand-painted ceramic tiles, wrought iron grilles, red clay roof tiles, and towers modeled after the great monuments of southern Spain. The result is one of the most distinctive and beloved commercial districts in America, and one of the most ambitious acts of civic placemaking in twentieth-century urban history.

The Architecture of the Plaza

Hand-Painted Ceramic Tile

More than 30 large ceramic tile murals and countless decorative tile panels are embedded in Plaza facades, most depicting scenes of Seville and Andalusian life. Many were imported directly from Spain; others were produced by artisans working in the Sevillian tradition. They remain one of the Plaza's most distinctive and admired features — a living connection to the craft traditions of southern Spain.

Wrought Iron & Bronze Ornament

The Plaza's ironwork — gates, lamp posts, window grilles, and balcony railings — was executed by craftsmen trained in the Spanish blacksmithing tradition. Bronze statuary, including works acquired by Nichols in Europe, punctuates the streetscape at key corners. Every surface was understood as an opportunity for ornament; every ornament was subordinate to the larger architectural composition.

Towers & Rooflines

The Plaza's distinctive silhouette is defined by its towers — campanile-like structures rising above the commercial roofline, punctuating the skyline with domed cupolas and arcaded belvederes. These towers draw directly from the church towers and civic belfries of Andalusia, most explicitly from Seville's Giralda, which inspired the Plaza's own signature tower.

Art Deco Inflections

While Spanish Colonial is the Plaza's dominant vocabulary, the district was built during the height of the Art Deco era, and its influence is visible in details throughout. Geometric ornamental panels, streamlined lettering, and the clean structural massing of several Plaza buildings reveal a Deco sensibility working within — and enriching — the Spanish stylistic framework Nichols required.

Fountains of the Plaza

No element defines the Plaza more than its fountains. Nichols and his successors installed dozens of fountains throughout the district, understanding water as both ornament and atmosphere. The great J.C. Nichols Memorial Fountain at Mill Creek Park greets visitors arriving from the north. Smaller courtyard pieces animate hidden corners throughout the district.

The Seville Light Festival

Each year on Thanksgiving evening, the Plaza is illuminated by more than 200,000 lights outlining its buildings, towers, and rooflines — a tradition begun in 1925. The Country Club Plaza lighting ceremony is one of the oldest holiday traditions in the American Midwest, drawing hundreds of thousands to see the district transformed into a glowing Andalusian fantasy.

Reading the Plaza

The Plaza rewards patient looking. At street level the district reads as a Spanish-themed shopping center; at the scale of the facade, it reveals itself as a carefully orchestrated piece of architectural scholarship. Every window surround, every roof pitch, every tile panel was specified against a reference catalog of Andalusian precedents. Nichols and his architects — Edward Delk, Edward Tanner, and others working under the Nichols Company's design controls — treated the Plaza as a thesis on transplantation. The question was not whether you could build Seville in Kansas City. It was how well you could build Seville in Kansas City, and whether anyone outside the district would be able to tell.

Three things to notice as you walk:

The tile panels. More than thirty large ceramic tile murals are set into the Plaza's facades, most imported from Seville or produced by artisans working in the Sevillian azulejo tradition. They depict Andalusian scenes: bullfights, convent courtyards, village life, saints and kings. Look up at the second and third floors of buildings you'd otherwise walk past — the Mill Creek Building, the block along 47th between Pennsylvania and Jefferson, the Seville Square complex on the east side. The panels are easy to miss when you're shopping. They are the point of the district when you're looking.

The towers. The Plaza's skyline is defined by its campaniles — the Giralda replica on the southeast corner is the most photographed, but there are six or seven other towers punctuating the district, each proportioned against its neighbor rather than against the real towers of Seville. Look at how they step down in height from the Giralda outward, and how they cluster near corners where the district wanted vertical emphasis. This is composed architecture, not accidental architecture. The towers are arguments about rhythm.

The Nichols signatures vs. the later additions. The original 1920s and 30s Plaza buildings — Balcony Building, Mill Creek Building, the Pharmacy Building, the Plaza Medical Building — share specific characteristics: low pedestrian-scale ground floors, asymmetrical upper massing, deep window reveals, wrought-iron balconies that actually work. The 1970s and 80s additions (Seville Square, several blocks along Pennsylvania) are reverent homages but visibly different — cleaner lines, more regular massing, modern structural systems showing through. Once you can see the difference, the district's chronological development becomes legible in the walls.

What to See

If you have an hour on the Plaza — the short list of things actually worth stopping for.

  1. The Giralda Tower on the southeast corner at Nichols Parkway & Wornall. Stand across the street and look up. Compare the proportions to a photograph of the original in Seville — the Plaza version is smaller but faithful to the original's stepped brickwork, arched openings, and Renaissance belfry crown.
  2. The J.C. Nichols Memorial Fountain at the northwest gateway (Mill Creek Park, 47th & Main). Four equestrian figures by Henri Greber, carved in 1911, originally intended for a Long Island estate that was never built. Nichols acquired them in 1951 and installed the fountain in 1960 as the Plaza's front door. The most photographed spot in Kansas City. Read more →
  3. The Bacchus Fountain — tucked into a courtyard off 47th, easy to miss if you're not looking for it. The bronze Bacchus is small but alive; the surrounding tile work is some of the finest azulejo on the Plaza. Read more →
  4. The Seville Square courtyard. The 1978 complex is a later addition, but its inner courtyard is the best surviving example on the Plaza of a genuine Andalusian patio — four-sided, centered on a small fountain, shaded by orange trees in summer. Walk through rather than past.
  5. The Mill Creek Building facade along 47th between Main and Broadway. One of the original 1923 structures. Note the second-floor tile panels, the iron balcony running the full length, and the Spanish clay tile roofline above. This is what Nichols meant when he said "Seville."
  6. The tile mural on the east side of Pennsylvania Avenue between 47th and 48th. One of the largest and best-preserved azulejo panels on the Plaza — a full Sevillian streetscape rendered in several hundred hand-painted tiles. Look at it long enough to see the individual brushstrokes.
  7. The view from the Brush Creek side looking north across the Plaza. The creek walk (south of J.C. Nichols Parkway) is an underused vantage point. From here, the Plaza's massing reads as a Spanish hill town — stepped roofs, emerging towers, the suggestion of an Andalusian village across the water. Go at dusk. The lights come on around 5:30 in winter.
  8. If you come in late November: the Plaza Lighting Ceremony on Thanksgiving evening. Two hundred thousand lights outlining every building, every tower, every cornice. A tradition that started in 1925 and hasn't missed a year. One of the oldest continuous civic rituals in the American Midwest.

The Giralda Connection

The most direct link between the Country Club Plaza and the city of Seville is the Giralda Tower — a conscious homage to the great bell tower of Seville Cathedral. The original Giralda was built as a minaret during the Almohad dynasty in the 12th century, converted to a Christian bell tower after the Reconquista, and crowned with a Renaissance belfry in the 16th century. It stands as one of the most celebrated towers in the world.

J.C. Nichols saw the Giralda on his travels through Andalusia and recognized in it the perfect symbol for his vision of the Plaza: ancient, European, graceful, and unmistakably Spanish. The Plaza's replica tower — rendered in the warm tones of Kansas City rather than the brick of Seville — is a deliberate act of architectural homage, a declaration that this American shopping district aspired to the cultural gravity of one of Europe's great cities.
