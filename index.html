More Than Meets the Eye

Kansas City is a city that rewards looking up. Its downtown skyline is dense with Art Deco towers built in a single extraordinary decade of civic ambition — thirty-story limestone landmarks whose gilded crowns catch the sunset over the Missouri plain. Its south side opens onto one of the most ambitious commercial districts in America, styled after the grandeur of Seville, Spain. Its boulevard system — one of the finest in the country — unfolds in miles of tree-lined parkways punctuated by more than 200 public fountains.

But the architecture is only part of the story. Behind every building is a history: of political machines and civic boosters, of a 1920s bond issue that made a Depression-era building boom possible, of a Kansas City boy named Harry Truman who owed his career to a corrupt political boss — and who went on to lead the world through the end of World War II and the opening of the Cold War. The buildings of Kansas City are the physical record of that history. They are worth understanding, not just admiring.

This guide is for everyone — the newcomer arriving for the World Cup, the lifelong resident who has never stopped to read the building across the street, the architecture student, the history reader, the curious walker. Look up. There is a lot to see.
