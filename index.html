A City Built in Gold

Kansas City rose to prominence during the Art Deco era of the 1920s and 1930s, leaving behind a skyline rich with geometric ornament, gleaming terra cotta, and monumental ambition. In 1926, city leaders launched the Ten-Year Plan — a $40 million public improvement program, extraordinary in its scale and scope, that funded a wave of construction transforming the downtown skyline in less than a decade. Towers rose. Civic halls took shape. A city announced itself to the world.

The buildings of that era — the Kansas City Power & Light Building, City Hall, Municipal Auditorium, the Fidelity Bank Building — were not merely functional. They were declarations. They told the story of a city that had made its fortune in agriculture, livestock, and railroads, and was now ready to spend that fortune on beauty, permanence, and civic pride. The architecture of Kansas City is the architecture of ambition at its most optimistic.

Kansas City is also the City of Fountains — home to more than 200 public sculptures that animate its grand boulevard system, with more fountains per capita than any city on earth except Rome. This reputation was not accidental. Developer J.C. Nichols spent decades acquiring European bronzes and commissioning original works for the city's parkways and plazas, establishing a tradition of public art that defines Kansas City as much as any building in its skyline.

And then there is the Country Club Plaza. Opened in 1922, the Plaza is the first planned automobile shopping district in the United States — and Nichols built it to look like Seville. Hand-painted ceramic tile murals, wrought iron grilles, red clay roof tiles, and towers modeled after the great monuments of Andalusia: the Plaza is one of the most remarkable acts of civic placemaking in American history, a piece of southern Spain dropped into the Missouri heartland and tended for more than a century.
