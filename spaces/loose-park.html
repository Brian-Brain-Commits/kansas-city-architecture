Established 1927
Acreage 75 Acres
Civil War Site Battle of Westport, 1864
Feature Rose Garden
Address 5200 Pennsylvania Ave

Written by Look Up, Kansas City About This Site

The Park

Loose Park is 75 acres of rolling lawn, mature trees, a duck pond, and one of the finest rose gardens in the Midwest, set in the heart of one of Kansas City's most desirable residential neighborhoods. It sits adjacent to Ward Parkway — the grand boulevard that J.C. Nichols developed as the spine of the Country Club District — and its grounds bleed almost imperceptibly into the surrounding neighborhood of large brick homes and tree-lined streets. On a May morning, when the roses are at their height and the park is thick with families, dogs, and joggers, it reads simply as a very good urban park. The ground beneath it tells a different story.

The park was donated to Kansas City in 1927 by Ella Loose in memory of her husband Jacob Loose, who had died in 1902. The Loose family was one of Kansas City's great industrial fortunes — Jacob Loose had co-founded the Loose-Wiles Biscuit Company, which produced Sunshine Biscuits and became one of the largest cracker and cookie manufacturers in the United States. Their home stood adjacent to what would become the park; Ella's gift transformed a private estate into a public amenity, donating the land with the stipulation that it be maintained as a public park in perpetuity. The city accepted the gift and incorporated the property into its parks system, which was then being expanded and connected through the boulevard network that George Kessler had designed.

The Battle of Westport

On October 23, 1864, the ground that is now Loose Park was a battlefield. The Battle of Westport — sometimes called the "Gettysburg of the West" — was the largest Civil War engagement west of the Mississippi River. Union forces under General Samuel Curtis and Confederate forces under General Sterling Price clashed across a broad front that extended through what is now Brush Creek, Loose Park, and the surrounding residential neighborhoods. Price had led his Confederate army of approximately 12,000 men northward through Missouri in a desperate attempt to reclaim the state for the Confederacy and influence the 1864 presidential election. Curtis's Union force met him here, in the fields south of Westport.

The battle lasted most of the day. Union cavalry under General Alfred Pleasonton attacked from the east while Curtis's main force held the line to the north; the Confederate army, caught between converging Union forces and already weakened by weeks of campaigning, broke and retreated southward. Price's Missouri raid was effectively over. The Confederacy would never again mount a serious military challenge in Missouri. The fields where this happened were farmland for another half-century; when Ella Loose donated the property, the battle had receded from living memory. A memorial to the Battle of Westport now stands within the park, and a broader network of battlefield markers extends through the surrounding neighborhood — one of several places in Kansas City where the Civil War is present beneath the surface of an ordinary day.

The Rose Garden

The Loose Park Rose Garden occupies a formal garden space in the park's interior, laid out with hundreds of rose varieties in geometric beds surrounding a central fountain. In late May and early June, when the roses peak, the garden is one of Kansas City's most visited destinations — a dense, fragrant, almost overwhelming concentration of color in the middle of a green urban park. It is maintained by the city parks department with the assistance of the Kansas City Rose Society and is consistently ranked among the finest public rose gardens in the Midwest.

The garden is best visited in the morning, before the heat of a Kansas City summer day builds. The light comes in low from the east, picking up the colors of the roses against the dark foliage, and the brick paths are still cool underfoot. Benches are placed throughout for sitting and looking — an old-fashioned amenity that is easy to overlook until you encounter it. The garden was a deliberate feature of the park's design from the beginning; formal rose gardens were a standard element of the City Beautiful movement's vision of urban parks as spaces of cultivated beauty, and the Loose family's donation was made in the spirit of that tradition.

Jacob and Ella Loose

Jacob Leander Loose was born in 1852 in Pennsylvania and came to Kansas City as a young man seeking opportunity in the booming commerce of the American interior. He and his partner Joseph Wiles founded the Loose-Wiles Biscuit Company in 1902, building it into a national enterprise — the Sunshine Biscuits brand, which they developed, became one of the most recognized in American grocery stores. The company's Kansas City factory was one of the largest employers in the city; the fortune it generated placed the Loose family among the city's leading philanthropists.

Jacob died in 1902, the same year the biscuit company was founded, leaving Ella to manage both the business legacy and the family's civic presence. Ella Loose was a significant figure in Kansas City philanthropy in her own right — her donation of the park was the most visible of several gifts she made to the city, and she specified that it bear her husband's name as a memorial. The park's full official name remains Jacob L. Loose Park. It is a reminder that Kansas City's great parks and boulevards were not simply products of city planning but of private generosity — of individuals and families who had made fortunes in the city and chose to give something back in the form of permanent public amenities. That tradition, visible in every fountain, every donated park, every museum with a wealthy name on the door, is one of the defining characteristics of Kansas City's civic culture.
