On Honest History

Why this site holds the restrictive covenants alongside the beautiful neighborhoods, and the urban-renewal demolitions alongside the Art Deco skyline. A note on method.

A friend asked me, a few weeks into this project, why the site has so much hard material in it. The Country Club Plaza page — which is mostly about fountains and tile and the strange pleasure of walking a 1920s shopping district that has kept most of its shape — ends by naming the restrictive deed covenants J.C. Nichols wrote into every lot he sold. The urban-renewal page describes Interstate 70 the way it actually behaves in the landscape: as a wound carved east of downtown to make automobile commuting from the western suburbs faster, at the cost of the Black neighborhoods it cut through. The 18th & Vine page names the demolitions that took out most of the blocks that produced the jazz history the district is now commemorated for. My friend's question was reasonable: why not split this material off into its own section? Why not have the architecture pages be architecture pages and have a separate "difficult history" corner for readers who want it? The site would be easier to navigate. The celebration would be unconflicted. The critique would still be available, just — over there.

I want to explain why I keep deciding not to do that. The short version is that the two halves are not two halves. The same people who built the Plaza wrote the covenants. The same city that produced the Art Deco skyline chose where to run the freeway. The architecture and the exclusion are the same act, viewed from two angles. Separating them tells a lie of emphasis even when nothing in the separated sections is factually wrong, because the lie isn't in the sentences. It's in the shape of the book.

Consider what happens when the halves get separated. A reader interested in architecture reads the Plaza page, admires the Seville-inspired tilework and the Sea Horse Fountain at Meyer Circle, and moves on. A reader interested in civil rights history reads the covenants page, learns about the 1948 Supreme Court decision in Shelley v. Kraemer, the persistence of de facto segregation after the covenants became legally unenforceable, the Troost line as the long shadow of those decisions. Both readers come away informed. But neither reader has been asked to hold both things in their head at once, and that holding-both is where the real argument lives. J.C. Nichols produced the most beautiful residential developments of his generation and wrote rules that kept Black families out of them. Not alternately — simultaneously. The covenants were a condition of buying the tile and the fountains. To admire one without acknowledging the other is to accept a version of the architecture that has been sanitized, and the sanitization is itself a way of letting the exclusion continue quietly in the present.

There's a related move I try not to make, which is the one where the history gets acknowledged and then quarantined. "Yes, Nichols wrote restrictive covenants, but we should appreciate the architecture despite this." The despite does a lot of work there. It lets the reader park the uncomfortable fact in one mental compartment while continuing to enjoy the architecture unimpeded. It treats the covenants as a moral asterisk on the main achievement rather than as constitutive of what the achievement was. But the Country Club District is not a beautiful neighborhood that happens to have been racially restricted. It is a beautiful neighborhood that was restricted because that is how it became the kind of neighborhood it is — the exclusivity is the point; the covenants are a tool of the exclusivity; the architecture is what the exclusivity bought. That's a harder thing to say. It's also closer to the truth.

The same structure applies to the Pendergast machine and the Art Deco boom. Tom Pendergast was corrupt by any measure; his operation stole from the city it was simultaneously building; his ready-mix-concrete monopoly fleeced the public projects it supplied. The 1928 Ten-Year Plan was partly a civic achievement and partly a graft vehicle. The Municipal Auditorium, City Hall, the Jackson County Courthouse are Pendergast buildings in the specific sense that they wouldn't exist in their current form without the political machine that made them possible. A version of KC architectural history that praises the buildings and condemns the machine — cleanly, in separate chapters — misses the structural fact: the corruption funded the building, and the building provided the civic-pride cover that kept the corruption in business. You can't subtract one and keep the other. You have to hold them together.

The same with urban renewal. The mid-century demolitions that cleared the East Side were not unfortunate side-effects of civic improvement. They were the civic improvement, from the perspective of the people who authorized them. Interstate 70 was routed where it was routed because the neighborhoods it went through had the least political power to resist it. The slum-clearance projects east of Troost were considered successful by the standards of their era because they did what they were designed to do. Pretending that the demolitions were a regrettable mistake rather than a deliberate choice protects the reputation of the institutions that made the choice, at the cost of pretending the choice wasn't a choice. The city we live in now was shaped by people who thought they were doing the right thing when they tore out 18th & Vine. Acknowledging that is harder than calling them wrong in retrospect. It requires recognizing that this kind of decision keeps getting made, under different names, by institutions that believe they are acting civically.

I think a guide to a city has a responsibility to get this right, and I think most guides get it wrong in predictable ways. The guide written to sell real estate erases the covenants. The guide written to condemn the city forgets that the covenants came with fountains and street trees that people have loved for a hundred years and still love, and that the love is part of the story too. Neither guide has to lie to be false. Both can be factually accurate within their selected frames. The falseness is in the selection. What I'm trying to do here — not always successfully — is keep the selections honest: to let the reader feel the specific pleasure of looking up at the Power & Light Building at dusk, and also the specific weight of knowing what the Pendergast machine cost; to walk the Plaza on a spring afternoon, and also know which families couldn't buy a house three blocks north of where they were walking until 1962; to watch the streetcar extension open, and also remember that the streetcar system it replaces was dismantled partly because its ridership was Black.

The phrase I keep coming back to is loving a city fully. A half-love is not a love; it is a preference for a version of the beloved that doesn't exist. If I only love the parts of Kansas City that make it easy to love — the fountains, the jazz history as heritage, the boom-era skyline — I am loving a postcard. The actual city includes the postcard and the thing outside the postcard's frame. The Troost line, the stockyards ruin, the East Side houses that were demolished and the ones that weren't, the Black-owned businesses that survived the 1968 riots and the ones that didn't. Loving the city fully means being able to say that the Kauffman Center is one of the most beautiful buildings built in America this century and that it sits two miles from census tracts that haven't recovered from decisions made in the 1950s, and refusing to let either clause amputate the other.

The site is partly an experiment in whether that kind of writing about a place is possible. It is trying to be useful to a visitor who wants to know where to walk, and also to a longtime resident who wants to understand what they have been walking past, and also to a reader who has never been to Kansas City and wants to know what it is. These are different audiences. The book I'd like the site to be, collectively, is the one they would all recognize as accurate. That requires refusing to separate the celebration from the cost. It requires holding the whole city in view. The site doesn't always manage this. When it fails, it usually fails toward the celebration — because the architecture is beautiful, and beauty is easy to write about, and the cost is painful, and pain is harder. The corrective is deliberate: keep the hard material where a reader will encounter it, not sequestered in a corner they can choose to skip.

One last thing. Writing the site this way is not primarily about guilt, and it is not about the particular Kansas City sin the site is describing. The method travels. Every American city has versions of this same set of tensions, with different names on the streets. The particular story here — Nichols, Pendergast, the 1951 flood, the 1968 riots, Troost — is specific. The structural pattern — beautiful infrastructure produced by institutions that excluded or extracted from part of the population they governed — is general. What I'm trying to model, at a small scale, is a way of writing about the built environment that doesn't require the reader to choose between loving a place and seeing it clearly. Those aren't competing projects. They are the same project, when done honestly.
