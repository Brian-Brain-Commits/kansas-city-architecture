Watching the McConahay Building Come Back

The second-floor office at 31st and Forest where Walt Disney ran a cartoon studio into bankruptcy in 1923 is being restored. What that means.

The McConahay Building at 31st and Forest is the kind of building that, for most of the twentieth century, no one would have bothered to preserve. A three-story commercial block, built in 1914 to house doctors' offices and retail, unremarkable in its architecture, unimportant in its civic presence. A hundred buildings like it were demolished in Kansas City's urban renewal decades. This one survived. What it had going for it was a second-floor tenant, briefly, a century ago: Laugh-O-Gram Studios, the short-lived animation company that Walt Disney founded there in 1922 and ran into bankruptcy by 1923.

The bankruptcy pushed Disney to Hollywood with a suitcase and a reel. Everything that followed — Mickey Mouse, Steamboat Willie, the studio, the parks, the global cultural empire — has an origin story that traces back through that second-floor office on 31st Street. The fact that the building still stands is largely an accident of what did and did not get demolished during the postwar decades. What is not an accident is that the preservation effort of the last ten years has, as of this spring, turned into a real restoration with funding and a construction crew.

I walked past it last week. The scaffolding is up; the upper-floor windows have been reglazed; the brick is being repointed. The ground floor is boarded, pending a tenant or an interpretive program. The second floor will become a museum space focused on Disney's Kansas City years — the commercial art career that preceded Laugh-O-Gram, the studio itself, the bankruptcy, the move to LA. The organization funding it, Thank You Walt Disney Inc., has been working on this since the early 2010s and is finally in the restoration phase.

What the restoration is doing is architectural: putting the building back into the condition it was in when Disney walked up those stairs to the second floor in 1922. New roof, new glazing, repointed brickwork, preserved interior detailing where any survives. The mechanicals are modernized. The structure is reinforced. Standard work for a century-old commercial building being brought back.

What the restoration cannot do — cannot do, cannot be asked to do — is reproduce the conditions that made the building matter in 1922. Disney was a kid in a rented second-floor office running a company that would fail in under eighteen months. The building was full of other tenants doing other work; the neighborhood around it was a functioning commercial district in a functioning Black-and-white mixed residential area; the mechanics of commercial art production in 1922 — the animation stands, the cutout figures, the frame-by-frame photography — all existed because specific equipment was available and specific people knew how to use it. That whole ecosystem is gone. The building can be preserved; the conditions cannot.

This is the recurring question around restoration work in Kansas City. We've gotten reasonably good at restoring buildings. We have not figured out how to restore the ecosystems the buildings were part of. The McConahay Building will, when it opens, be a well-maintained structure at 31st and Forest with educational exhibits about Walt Disney's formative years. That's worthwhile. It is not the same as what was there in 1922. No restoration is.

Watch for the opening, when it comes. Walk up to the second floor. Look out the window toward 31st Street. Stand where a young man once tried and failed to build a cartoon studio in Kansas City. That's part of what restoration can do, even when it can't restore everything: it can keep a specific place available to be stood in. That's not nothing. That is, in fact, most of what the job is.
