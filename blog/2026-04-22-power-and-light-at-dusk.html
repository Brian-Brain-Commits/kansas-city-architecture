The Power & Light Building at Dusk

Standing at 14th and Baltimore at golden hour, watching the crown come on.

The Power & Light Building's crown is the part of the Kansas City skyline that behaves most like a performance. For most of the daylight hours it sits there — a stepped Art Deco cap, limestone and terra cotta, clearly designed to be handsome but not visibly doing anything. Then the sun drops behind the river bluffs west of downtown, and the crown starts its shift. The limestone catches the last orange light of the day and holds it for a minute longer than the buildings around it do, because the crown is higher. Then the orange fades. For maybe three minutes the whole building is in civil twilight, uniformly gray. Then the illumination comes on.

There is no announcement. The switch flips somewhere you can't see. The crown goes from dim to lit in what feels like a single frame. The color cycles — amber, purple, blue, back to amber — on a schedule that varies with the season and occasionally with whatever civic occasion the building is being lit for. In 1931, when the building opened, the crown illumination was a novelty worth writing newspaper stories about. In 2026 it is what the city does at night.

I was at 14th and Baltimore on a Tuesday in mid-April, standing across the street with a camera. It was the golden-hour window that photographers chase, 20 minutes of light that flattens the facades and saturates every color the building has. The Power & Light Building held it longer than anything else in frame. Town Pavilion, a couple of blocks north, went to silhouette first. City Hall, further east, was already dark against the eastern sky. Only the Power & Light crown was still catching light when the streetlights on Baltimore flickered on.

What I wanted to say, and couldn't in the caption for a single photograph, is that the experiential quality of the building is not something that a reference page can capture. The reference page can tell you it's 476 feet, completed in 1931, designed by Hoit, Price & Barnes, setback Art Deco, illuminated crown. What the reference page can't tell you is what it feels like to be standing at the street corner in April and have the crown come on in front of you.

It feels like Kansas City keeping a promise that a previous generation made. The generation that built Power & Light in 1931 was betting that illuminated skyline architecture was what a modern city did. They lit the crown as a claim about what Kansas City was. Every night since, the building has been cashing that claim — through the Depression, the war, the postwar decline, the revival, all of it. Ninety-five years of keeping a public promise to illuminate. The rest of the skyline picks up the lighting in pieces. Only the Power & Light crown carries the whole weight of 1931 forward.

If you want to see it, the window is narrow. Fifteen minutes after local sunset, standing somewhere south of 13th Street with a clear sight line to Baltimore Avenue. The crown will come on. If it's cloudy it glows through the cloud cover in a way that is, if anything, more dramatic. If it's clear you get the colors in sequence. Either way, you're watching a city make the same argument about itself that it was making the first night the crown lit in 1931. That's the thing worth standing around for.
