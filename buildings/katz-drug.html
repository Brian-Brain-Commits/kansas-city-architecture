39th & Main, Kansas City • 1934

Katz Drug
Building

Year Built 1934
Style Streamline Moderne
Founders Isaac & Michael Katz
Height ~40 ft (2 stories)
Address 3948 Main St
◆

A 1934 Streamline Moderne drugstore at 39th and Main. The rounded right-hand corner is the Moderne signature; the rooftop KATZ sign was lit in red neon and visible for blocks.

The Katz Brothers' Empire

Isaac and Michael Katz were two Russian-Jewish immigrant brothers who opened their first drugstore in Kansas City in 1914. By selling at unprecedented discount prices and running continuous advertising — the brothers were among the first American retailers to treat newspaper advertising as a weekly discipline rather than an occasional splurge — they built Katz Drug into one of the largest drugstore chains in the American Midwest. At its peak in the 1960s, Katz operated more than seventy stores across five states.

The 39th and Main store, completed in 1934, was the chain's flagship location and the face Katz Drug presented to Kansas City. It was designed in the Streamline Moderne style then sweeping through American commercial architecture — a reaction against Art Deco's angular geometry in favor of aerodynamic curves, horizontal banding, and smooth surfaces that suggested speed, modernity, and the future. A drugstore, in the Katz brothers' conception, should look as modern as the ideas it sold.

◆

The Sign

Every Katz Drug store in Kansas City had a rooftop sign, but the 39th and Main sign became the most iconic of them all. Red neon letters spelling KATZ sat above the building's rounded corner, visible the length of Main Street in both directions. At dusk it flickered to life — the characteristic imperfect warm-up of period-correct neon tubes — and glowed a saturated cherry red through the Midtown night. For generations of Kansas City drivers and streetcar riders, the Katz sign marked the exact center of 39th Street's commercial life.

The building still stands, though the Katz Drug chain closed in 1971 after being sold and resold through a chain of corporate acquisitions. The rounded-corner storefront has housed a succession of tenants since — record stores, restaurants, pharmacies again — and the rooftop sign has been variously restored, repainted, and re-lit over the decades. It remains one of the defining silhouettes of Midtown's commercial strip.

Streamline Moderne in Context

The Katz Drug Building is one of the finest surviving examples of the Streamline Moderne style in Kansas City — a style more strongly associated with bus terminals, diners, and movie theaters than with office towers or civic buildings. Its horizontal emphasis, curved corners, and polished-cream finishes are all characteristic of the style at its most confident. The companion piece downtown is Municipal Auditorium's northwest corner, where the PWA Moderne treatment sweeps a massive civic building into a curve at its street corner. Katz's curve does the same thing at commercial scale.

As a building type, the Moderne drugstore was short-lived. By the late 1930s the Katz brothers were already experimenting with other styles, and by the postwar era the retail typology had moved toward suburban shopping centers and chain stores of no particular design intention. The 1934 Katz at 39th and Main captures a narrow window — roughly 1930 to 1940 — when a neighborhood drugstore could also be a piece of sculpture.
