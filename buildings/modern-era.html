Kansas City, Missouri — 1980s–Present — A Second Era of Ambition

After the Long Pause

There is a gap in Kansas City's architectural timeline. After the burst of civic construction that ended in the late 1930s, the city built little of architectural consequence for nearly four decades. Urban renewal cleared neighborhoods without replacing them; suburbanization drew investment away from downtown; the civic confidence that had produced City Hall and Municipal Auditorium went largely dormant. What remained was the Boom Era skyline, aging but intact, waiting for the city to find its ambition again.

The buildings on this page represent that return. From One Kansas City Place's 1988 corporate tower — the largest private construction project in the city's history at the time — to the Kauffman Center's 2011 assertion that Kansas City belongs among America's great performing arts cities, each building here is an act of renewed civic conviction. They are different in vocabulary from the limestone towers around them, but not different in intent.

The Buildings

One Kansas City Place

1988 • Postmodern • 623 ft

The city's tallest tower — a 45-story SOM-designed skyscraper whose crown echoes the Deco setbacks around it while claiming the skyline for a new era.

Read More ◆

Kauffman Center for the Performing Arts

2011 • Contemporary • Moshe Safdie

A sweeping shell of glass and stainless steel housing two world-class performance halls — the most architecturally ambitious building in Kansas City since the 1930s.

Read More ◆

H. Roe Bartle Hall

1976 / Expanded 1994 • ~260 ft Pylons

Four concrete super pylons dominate the downtown skyline above an exhibit hall spanning an active interstate — one of the most structurally audacious civic buildings in the Midwest.

Read More ◆

CPKC Stadium

2024 • Generator Studio • 11,500 Seats

The first stadium in the world purpose-built for a women's professional sports team — sited on the Missouri River waterfront with the Bond Bridge as its backdrop.
