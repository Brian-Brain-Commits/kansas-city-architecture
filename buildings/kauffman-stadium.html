◆

The Waterfall Stadium

Kauffman Stadium opened on April 10, 1973 — six months after its twin, Arrowhead Stadium, inaugurated the Truman Sports Complex. Where Arrowhead was designed for football's compressed geometry and concentrated noise, Kauffman was designed around baseball's natural expansiveness: wide, open sightlines to the outfield, a grass playing surface, and a view beyond the fences that substituted landscape for the blank concrete walls most parks of its era offered.

That view is Kauffman's defining feature: a 322-foot-wide water spectacular beyond the outfield wall, featuring cascading fountains and a 12-story-tall waterfall that operates throughout every home game. No other major league baseball park has anything like it. The water feature was part of the original 1973 design — an act of landscape architecture embedded in a sports building, and one that has made Kauffman Stadium recognizable in photographs from anywhere in the world.

◆

The 2009 Renovation

In 2009, Kauffman underwent a $250 million renovation by 360 Architecture (now Populous) that dramatically expanded and modernized the facility while preserving its essential character. The renovation added a new main entrance, expanded concourse, club levels, and premium seating — and crucially, retained and enhanced the waterfall feature rather than replacing it with the digital scoreboard infrastructure that had transformed most parks of that vintage.

The result is a stadium that feels simultaneously vintage and contemporary: the original 1973 geometry is intact, the grass and the water are still there, and the additions layer new amenities without altering the view from the seats that made the building beloved in the first place. It was voted the best baseball stadium in the American League multiple times after the renovation.

Named for Ewing Kauffman

The stadium was named for Ewing Kauffman, who purchased the Kansas City Royals expansion franchise in 1968 and built one of the most successful small-market franchises in baseball history. Kauffman was a self-made pharmaceutical entrepreneur — founder of Marion Laboratories — whose civic investment in Kansas City extended well beyond baseball. The Kauffman Foundation, which he established, became one of the nation's leading funders of entrepreneurship education.

The stadium was officially renamed from Royals Stadium to Kauffman Stadium in 1993, the year after his death — an acknowledgment that the building and the franchise were as much his civic contribution as any of his philanthropic work. The Royals won the World Series at Kauffman in 1985 and again in 2015, the only championship won at the Truman Sports Complex other than the Chiefs' Super Bowl returns.
