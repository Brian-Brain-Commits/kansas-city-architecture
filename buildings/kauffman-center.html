Kauffman
Center

Year Built 2011
Architect Moshe Safdie
Style Contemporary
Venues Helzberg Hall • Muriel Kauffman Theatre
Capacity 1,800 / 2,200 Seats
Address 1601 Broadway
◆

A New Landmark for a New Century

The Kauffman Center for the Performing Arts is the most architecturally ambitious building constructed in Kansas City since the Art Deco civic halls of the 1930s. Designed by Israeli-Canadian architect Moshe Safdie and opened in September 2011, it introduced a silhouette unlike anything in the city's existing skyline — a sweeping, shell-like form of curving glass and stainless steel that rises dramatically against the downtown backdrop.

The project was made possible by a $326 million campaign anchored by a $150 million lead gift from the Kauffman Foundation, the largest private gift to an arts organization in Kansas City's history. The result is a building that signals civic ambition in the same register as City Hall or Union Station — a statement that Kansas City intends to be a city of cultural consequence, not merely historical distinction.

◆

Safdie's Design

Moshe Safdie is best known for Habitat 67, the modular housing complex he built for Expo '67 in Montreal, and for a series of bold civic projects around the world — the National Gallery of Canada, the Yad Vashem Holocaust Museum in Jerusalem, the Crystal Bridges Museum of American Art in Arkansas. The Kauffman Center belongs to this tradition of landmark public buildings designed as objects in landscape rather than additions to an existing urban fabric.

The building's most distinctive exterior feature is its shell — a continuous curved surface of structural glass panels and stainless steel louvers that wraps around the two performance halls like an acoustic carapace. The shell is not purely decorative: it controls light and sound, protects the interior from the thermal extremes of the Kansas City climate, and creates the building's recognizable silhouette. From Broadway, the effect is of a building that appears to be in motion — a quality deliberately opposed to the static permanence of the limestone towers nearby.

Two Halls, Two Acoustics

Inside the shell are two fully independent performance venues, each designed as a world-class acoustic environment by the firm Kirkegaard Associates. Helzberg Hall seats approximately 1,800 and serves as the home of the Kansas City Symphony — a shoebox-form concert hall modeled on the great European halls of the nineteenth century, its warm wood surfaces and precise geometry tuned for orchestral music. The Muriel Kauffman Theatre seats approximately 2,200 in a horseshoe configuration designed for opera and large-scale theatrical productions; it is the home of the Lyric Opera of Kansas City and the Kansas City Ballet.

The acoustic isolation between the two halls is engineered to allow simultaneous performances without bleed — a significant technical achievement given their physical proximity. The backstage areas are correspondingly expansive: full fly lofts, large wing spaces, and loading facilities capable of receiving the sets of touring Broadway productions.

Place in the City

The Kauffman Center occupies a site on the west side of downtown, on a bluff above I-670 with views of the western skyline. Its placement creates a western anchor to the civic arts district that includes the Nelson-Atkins Museum of Art to the south and the Crossroads Arts District to the east — a triangle of institutions that together constitute one of the densest concentrations of cultural facilities for a city of Kansas City's size anywhere in the United States.

Where the Deco towers of the 1930s expressed civic ambition through height and ornament, the Kauffman Center expresses it through gesture — through a building that announces itself as an event rather than a backdrop. Whether one finds its sculptural exuberance a natural successor to Kansas City's tradition of architectural ambition or a departure from it, there is no question that it belongs in the lineage of buildings that have defined this city's skyline in their respective eras.
