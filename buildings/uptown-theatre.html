37th & Broadway, Kansas City • 1928

Uptown
Theatre

Year Built 1928
Architect Boller Brothers
Style Spanish Colonial Revival
Capacity 2,000 Seats
Address 3700 Broadway Blvd
◆

Opened 1928 by the Boller Brothers, who designed more than a hundred theaters across the Midwest. The vertical UPTOWN marquee above the parapet is the building's signature — visible down Broadway in both directions, lit from dusk through the last show.

The Bollers' Theater Empire

Carl and Robert Boller were brothers from Kansas City who together designed an estimated one hundred and fifty theaters across the American Midwest and Mountain West between roughly 1905 and the early 1940s. They were neither national celebrities like Thomas W. Lamb (who designed the downtown Midland Theatre) nor architects of single-commission masterpieces. They were workhorses of the movie palace era — men who understood what a neighborhood theater needed to be and produced those buildings at volume, with a consistent vocabulary and generally excellent results.

The Uptown, opened in 1928 at 37th and Broadway, is among their best surviving Kansas City works. It was commissioned as a first-run movie house to serve the rapidly developing residential neighborhoods of south Midtown, at a time when the streetcar line along Broadway was one of the city's main commercial spines. The Spanish Colonial Revival exterior was deliberate — the style was widely associated in the 1920s with entertainment and leisure (an association the Plaza would extend a few years later) and the Bollers deployed it across several of their Missouri theaters in similar form.

◆

The Marquee

The Uptown's vertical marquee sign is the building's signal. Rising above the parapet in tiered neon letters spelling UPTOWN, it was designed to be read from blocks away down Broadway. Period-correct neon meant a slow warm flicker at dusk as the tubes came to life, settling into a steady warm red glow that carried the block. The horizontal canopy-marquee below the vertical sign projected over the sidewalk entry — a smaller lit field that announced the current feature in interchangeable letters.

Broadway in 1928 was a streetcar boulevard — paving, overhead lines, a density of pedestrian-oriented commerce. The Uptown's signage was sized and placed for that context. Even today, walking south on Broadway, the silhouette of the vertical marquee tells you exactly where 37th Street is.

From Movies to Music

Like most of America's neighborhood movie palaces, the Uptown's half-century as a first-run cinema ended in the 1970s as television and suburban multiplexes pulled audiences away. After a period of declining bookings and eventual closure, the building underwent several adaptive reuses before reopening as a live music venue in the 1990s. Today the Uptown operates as one of Midtown's premier mid-sized concert venues, hosting touring acts in the original 1928 auditorium.

The Uptown is a survivor. Most of the Bollers' Kansas City theaters are gone — demolished for parking lots, lost to fire, converted to other uses beyond recognition. The Uptown remains because its location on a still- vibrant commercial strip made reuse economically viable, and because successive owners have preserved the essential features: the Spanish Colonial facade, the vertical marquee, the interior auditorium's good bones. It is one of the clearest surviving signals of what Midtown looked like when Broadway was the spine of a streetcar city.
