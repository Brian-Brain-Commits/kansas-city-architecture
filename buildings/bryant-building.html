Style Art Deco / Commercial

Completed 1931, 17 floors. Each floor plate is separated from the next by an engraved bronze spandrel — the building's Art Deco detail.

The Deco Building That Isn't Famous

The Bryant Building sits on the southwest corner of 11th and Grand, three blocks south of the Power & Light Building and across the street from the Jackson County Courthouse. It was completed in 1931, which means it was designed at the same moment as the P&L Building, the Fidelity Bank, City Hall, and the Courthouse — the cluster of civic and commercial Art Deco towers that gives downtown Kansas City its 1930s character. The Bryant is smaller than any of them, quieter than any of them, and, as a result, almost never named when people list the KC Art Deco skyline. It should be.

At 16 stories and 205 feet, the Bryant was a mid-sized speculative office building of its era, constructed for the real-estate firm that gave it its name. It was never meant to be a landmark. It was meant to be a reliable Class A office address on a prominent downtown corner, leased to established tenants, providing its investors a steady return. That it has done for ninety-four years. Unremarkable longevity is its own kind of remarkable.

Reading the Facade

What you see walking past the Bryant at street level is a compact Art Deco commercial tower with the typical tripartite composition of its era: a rusticated limestone base occupying the first three floors, a plain middle shaft of buff brick with regular window bays, and a capital at the top expressed through stepped setbacks and ornamental spandrel panels above the top floor. The ornamental vocabulary is restrained — geometric patterns in the spandrels, fluted pilasters framing the corner bays, a cornice line marked by a band of ornamental terra cotta.

The building does not aspire to the theatrical verticality of the P&L Building or the civic monumentality of City Hall. It is a commercial building doing what commercial Art Deco buildings did well: presenting a dignified, ornamented, slightly-stepped facade to the street while maximizing leasable square footage inside. The same architects who worked on the more famous towers of the 1930s designed buildings like the Bryant by the dozen across American cities, and this particular one has survived in better condition than most.

Survival Through the Quiet Decades

The Bryant's quieter history is its survival through the mid-century period when so many buildings in its weight class were lost. Downtown Kansas City from roughly 1960 through 1990 had too much vacant office space; the smaller and older commercial buildings were the first to go vacant, the first to become economically marginal, and often the first to be demolished for surface parking. The Bryant was not demolished. It held its occupants through the bad years at reduced rents, maintained its lobby, kept its elevators running, and remained a functioning address while a dozen similar buildings in the same radius did not.

The downtown residential conversion wave of the 2010s caught up with the Bryant. Upper floors have been converted to apartments; the ground floor continues to serve retail and office tenants. The building is once again a stable, working downtown address — now housing people who live here as well as businesses that rent here, the same mix the 1931 designers would have recognized in a smaller city, just reallocated across the floor plan.

In the Art Deco Constellation

Walking the downtown Art Deco cluster — P&L Building, 909 Walnut (Fidelity Bank), City Hall, the Jackson County Courthouse, the Municipal Auditorium, the Bryant — the Bryant is the one people miss. It sits between the headline buildings on a corner most visitors don't walk past. But if you stop at 11th and Grand, look up at the stepped crown with its ornamental spandrel panels, and notice the fluted pilasters framing the corner bays, you are reading the same design vocabulary that produced the more famous skyline two blocks north. The Bryant is the supporting cast of the KC Art Deco story — the commercial middle class of the skyline, doing the patient work of continuity.
