The Tower That Ended the Drought

For forty-nine years — from the completion of City Hall in 1937 through the mid-1980s — no new skyscraper rose in downtown Kansas City. The Art Deco towers that the Ten-Year Plan had produced in the 1930s held the skyline alone while Kansas City spent the postwar decades building suburbs, highways, and stadiums on the periphery. When the drought finally broke in 1986, it broke with Town Pavilion: a 590-foot postmodern tower at 1111 Main Street, designed by the Kansas City-headquartered firm HOK.

One Kansas City Place followed two years later at 624 feet, taking the tallest-building title. Together the two towers ended the era in which the Art Deco skyline was still the complete skyline. They also defined the downtown profile for the next forty years — no new tower has exceeded either of them in the decades since.

HOK and the Local Firm at Global Scale

Helmuth, Obata & Kassabaum — now better known as HOK — was founded in St. Louis in 1955 and opened a Kansas City office that grew into one of the most consequential architecture firms in the world. By the 1980s HOK was designing airports, sports stadiums, and corporate headquarters across the United States and abroad. Town Pavilion was a hometown project for a firm whose work was increasingly global.

The building reflects the design language HOK was using at the time for corporate towers: a stepped postmodern crown that echoes the Art Deco setbacks of the 1930s buildings nearby without imitating them, a restrained glass-and-granite facade, and a base that tries to reconnect the tower to the street-level shopping arcades that were then a fashionable urban-design idea.

The Street-Level Arcade

The original Town Pavilion included a multi-level indoor shopping arcade that occupied the lower floors and connected via skywalks to adjacent buildings. The arcade reflected the 1980s faith that downtown retail could be revived through climate-controlled indoor environments — a theory that proved incorrect across most American cities. The Town Pavilion arcade gradually lost its retail tenants through the 1990s and 2000s and was largely converted to office and service use. The skywalks remain. The retail vision did not.

In the Skyline Today

Town Pavilion shares its block with One Kansas City Place, and the two buildings read together from a distance as a paired postmodern crown on the eastern edge of the downtown cluster. Its stepped top echoes Art Deco massing without being an Art Deco building — a self-conscious reference to the skyline it joined rather than an extension of that skyline's original language. That is about as good a summary of postmodern civic architecture as you could ask for.
