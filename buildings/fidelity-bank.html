Building

Year Built 1931

Banking in the Deco Era

The Fidelity National Bank and Trust Building was commissioned in 1929 by one of Kansas City's most prominent banking institutions. Like several of its contemporaries — including the Power and Light Building a block away — it was designed in the boom years before the Depression and completed in the economic crisis that followed. Its construction required both the optimism to begin and the tenacity to finish in the face of collapsing real estate and banking markets across America.

The building was designed by the Kansas City firm of Keene & Simpson, who brought to the project a sophisticated command of the Art Deco vocabulary and a deep understanding of what a bank building needed to communicate: permanence, solidity, prosperity. In an era when depositors were watching banks fail across the country, the Fidelity Building's ornamental richness was not mere aesthetics — it was a declaration of confidence.

◆

Terracotta Splendor

The design by Keene & Simpson is notable for its sophisticated blending of Art Deco massing with Gothic-derived ornamental detail — a combination found in the great bank towers of 1920s Manhattan that Kansas City architects studied and adapted for the midwest. The lower floors are clad in a rich base treatment of ornamental stonework; the tower rises through a series of setbacks typical of Deco skyscraper design, its shaft covered in terracotta panels of extraordinary variety and intricacy.

Gothic pointed arches, grotesque figures, and spire-like pinnacles at the crown recall the bank buildings of Wall Street — a deliberate reference to financial prestige and permanence. The terracotta skin of 909 Walnut is among the finest examples of architectural ceramics in the Midwest. The panels were custom-fabricated and applied in a palette running from warm gold to deep bronze, catching the light differently at different times of day. The effect gives the building a surface richness that flat stone cannot achieve — a building that rewards the eye the closer you look.

The Building Today

Now known by its address, 909 Walnut, the building has been sensitively converted to mixed-use occupancy, with residential units on upper floors and commercial space at street level. Its exterior has been carefully maintained, preserving one of downtown Kansas City's most elaborately ornamented facades for future generations to study and appreciate.

909 Walnut stands as proof that the finest Art Deco architecture does not age — it deepens. Each passing decade reveals new layers of craft in its terracotta panels, new subtleties in its massing, new pleasures in its ornamental program. In a city rich with Deco architecture, it remains among the most rewarding buildings to know.
