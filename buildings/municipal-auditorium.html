Municipal
Auditorium

Year Built 1935

The Ten-Year Plan

The Municipal Auditorium was born from the most ambitious civic planning initiative in Kansas City's history: the 1926 Ten-Year Plan. Championed by city manager Henry McElroy and backed by a $32 million bond issue approved by voters, the plan called for a suite of new public buildings — a city hall, auditorium, police headquarters, and courts building — that would transform Kansas City from a regional commercial center into a true metropolitan capital.

The Auditorium was the plan's most prestigious assignment: a multipurpose civic venue capable of hosting symphony concerts, boxing matches, political conventions, and graduation ceremonies. Construction began in 1934, funded in part by the federal Public Works Administration, and the building opened in 1935 to widespread civic celebration.

◆

PWA Moderne Architecture

The building was designed by the Kansas City firm of Gentry, Voskamp & Neville, working in the PWA Moderne vocabulary — a variant of Art Deco developed for Depression-era government buildings, characterized by monumental scale, geometric simplicity, and classical allusion executed in a restrained Deco idiom. The main facade — a bold composition of limestone piers, geometric relief panels, and horizontal spandrels — projects an authority appropriate to the city's premier public gathering place.

Entrance portals are surmounted by sculptural groups in the classic PWA Moderne manner: abstracted figures in relief, symbolizing the arts and civic life. Inside, the Music Hall's Art Deco interior is among the finest in America: ornamental bronze railings, geometric ceiling coffers, and a proscenium arch of exceptional richness frame the 2,600-seat hall. The building contains not just the Music Hall but also the Auditorium Arena — a larger sports and entertainment venue that once hosted major NBA and NHL events.

A Legacy of Events

The Municipal Auditorium has been the site of some of the defining moments in Kansas City's public life. The 1976 Republican National Convention was held here — a rare presidential contest in which the nomination remained genuinely in doubt until the ballots were cast, as incumbent Gerald Ford held off a challenge from Ronald Reagan. Jazz legends, classical orchestras, rock royalty, and championship boxing matches have all filled these halls across nearly nine decades.

The Kansas City Philharmonic and its successor, the Kansas City Symphony, called the Music Hall home for generations. Sarah Vaughan, Frank Sinatra, Elvis Presley, and the Rolling Stones have all performed here. The building remains in active use today, one of the few Depression-era civic auditoriums in America still serving its original purpose — a living monument to the ambition of the Ten-Year Plan.
