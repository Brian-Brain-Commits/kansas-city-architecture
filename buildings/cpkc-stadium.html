Capacity 11,500 (exp. 20,500)
Team KC Current • NWSL

◆

A World First on the Missouri River

CPKC Stadium opened on March 16, 2024, and immediately entered the record books as the first stadium in the world purpose-built and privately financed exclusively for a professional women's sports team. The Kansas City Current, a National Women's Soccer League club, built it themselves — owners Angie and Chris Long funded the $117 million project without public subsidy, a fact that distinguishes this building from nearly every major sports venue constructed in America in the last three decades.

The stadium sits at Berkley Riverfront Park on the north edge of downtown, on the south bank of the Missouri River. Its backdrop is the Christopher S. Bond Bridge — the cable-stayed I-35 crossing that arches over the river just half a mile to the north — whose towers and cables frame the stadium's setting from the field, from the stands, and from the approach roads. No Kansas City sports venue has a more dramatic natural and structural context.

◆

Generator Studio and the Design

The stadium was designed by Generator Studio, a Kansas City–based firm, in close collaboration with the Current's ownership and leadership. The design prioritizes intimacy over scale: 11,500 seats arranged in a steeply raked bowl that puts every spectator within 100 feet of the touchline — a proximity that transforms the experience of watching a match relative to the cavernous multi-purpose stadiums in which most American soccer has historically been played.

The exterior reads as a series of overlapping steel canopies and glass panels that step down toward the river, connecting the stadium visually to the water rather than turning its back on it. The main concourse is open-air on the river side, offering sightlines to the Missouri and the Bond Bridge from inside the stadium — a feature rare in any American sports venue and one that establishes the building's relationship to its site as a deliberate design decision, not an incidental condition.

What It Means for Kansas City

Kansas City has built stadiums before — Arrowhead and Kauffman opened in 1972 and define the sports campus in the eastern suburbs. But CPKC Stadium is something different: an urban stadium, built by its team's owners without public money, on the riverfront that urban renewal left empty for half a century. Its construction is as much a civic act as an athletic one.

The stadium sold out every home match in its first season, the Current winning the 2024 NWSL Championship on their home pitch. It hosted the 2025 NCAA Women's College Cup. The Berkley Riverfront neighborhood that had been largely inaccessible and underdeveloped for decades is now anchored by a building that draws tens of thousands of people to the Missouri River's edge, which is exactly what Kansas City's most expansive civic plans have imagined for that site for generations.

In the lineage of Kansas City buildings that bet on the city's future — City Hall in 1937, One Kansas City Place in 1988, the Kauffman Center in 2011 — CPKC Stadium takes its place as the most recent act of civic conviction in built form. That it was built for women's soccer, and built privately, makes it a new kind of entry in that tradition.
