Kansas City, Missouri • 1933

Nelson-Atkins
Museum of Art

Year Built 1933
Architect Wight & Wight
Style Beaux Arts / Neoclassical
Admission Free
Address 4525 Oak St
◆

Opened 1933, a William Rockhill Nelson bequest. The Ionic colonnade across the south portico runs 270 feet.

Written by Look Up, Kansas City About This Site

Overview

The Nelson-Atkins Museum of Art stands on a bluff above Brush Creek and the Country Club Plaza, its long limestone facade looking south over a lawn where four enormous shuttlecocks by Claes Oldenburg have rested since 1994 as if dropped there by a passing giant. The image is Kansas City's most reproduced architectural photograph: the classical columns, the pale stone, the absurdist sculpture, and the wide open sky. But the humor of that juxtaposition should not distract from what the building actually represents — one of the great acts of civic philanthropy in American history, and one of the finest art museums in the United States.

The museum opened on December 11, 1933, the product of two separate bequests that were merged into a single institution. Mary McAfee Atkins left the bulk of her estate — approximately $300,000 — to found a museum upon her death in 1911. William Rockhill Nelson, founder of the Kansas City Star and the most powerful figure in Kansas City's civic life, left instructions that his estate be used to purchase art for the city. The Nelson Fund, managed by a board of trustees, acquired works from European collections throughout the 1920s. When the two bequests were combined, the result was a museum endowed with both a building and a collection of extraordinary depth — and the most generously funded art acquisition fund in the country at the time of its opening.

The Architecture

Wight & Wight — the same firm that would later design Kansas City's City Hall — received the commission for the Nelson-Atkins building in the late 1920s, and their response was a masterwork of American Beaux Arts design. The building's exterior is clad entirely in Carthage limestone, a Missouri stone of warm buff tone that reads as cream in morning light and gold in the late afternoon. The main facade on Oak Street presents a colonnade of Ionic columns above a broad flight of steps — a temple front in the grand tradition, scaled to suggest permanence and institutional authority without the cold monumentality that afflicts lesser examples of the type.

The interior organization follows Beaux Arts logic: a central axis from the main entrance leads through a sequence of galleries arranged symmetrically around a grand central hall. The proportions throughout are classical — high ceilings, top-lit galleries where possible, stone floors and plaster walls in muted tones designed to subordinate themselves to the works on display. Thomas and William Wight understood that a museum building is not a monument to itself; it is a setting for art. The Nelson-Atkins achieves that balance with unusual skill. The building is magnificent, and yet it consistently makes you look at the paintings rather than the architecture.

The Bloch Building

In 2007, the Nelson-Atkins opened a major addition: the Bloch Building, designed by Seattle-based architect Steven Holl and widely regarded as one of the most significant works of American architecture of the early twenty-first century. Where the original building is heavy, symmetrical, and stone, the Bloch Building is light, fragmented, and glass. Holl conceived it as a series of five "lenses" — translucent white forms that emerge from the east lawn at different angles, connected below grade by a sequence of gallery spaces that burrow into the hillside.

The design is best experienced at night. After dark, the lens forms glow from within, their frosted glass skins diffusing the gallery light into the surrounding landscape — a series of luminous objects scattered across the lawn like artifacts from another civilization. The effect is deliberately mysterious. Holl wanted the addition to be discovered rather than announced; it is not visible from the main Oak Street facade, and its relationship to the original building is one of contrast and dialogue rather than subordination or imitation. The Bloch Building does not try to be the Nelson-Atkins, and in refusing to do so, it becomes a worthy companion to it. The addition was funded by Henry W. Bloch, co-founder of H&R Block, whose support for the museum extended over decades.

The Collection

The Nelson-Atkins collection is genuinely encyclopedic, spanning five thousand years across every major cultural tradition. Its particular strengths are in European paintings from the Renaissance through the nineteenth century — the museum holds significant works by Caravaggio, Rembrandt, El Greco, and a collection of French Impressionists that would be exceptional in any city. The Asian art collection is among the finest in the Western Hemisphere; the Chinese Buddhist sculpture galleries are objects of serious pilgrimage for scholars of East Asian art. The photography collection, built with unusual ambition over the past three decades, has become one of the best in the country.

None of this would surprise visitors who know their American art museums. What is surprising, for many first-time visitors, is encountering it in Kansas City — a city whose cultural institutions are systematically underestimated by people who have never been there. The Nelson-Atkins is not a regional curiosity; it is a world-class institution that holds its own against any comparable museum in the United States. And it is free. William Rockhill Nelson, who built the Kansas City Star into the dominant newspaper between Chicago and the Pacific Coast, understood that a city's claim to greatness rests not only on its commerce but on what it makes available to its citizens. The Nelson-Atkins, which he funded and whose name bears only half his own, is the most lasting monument to that conviction.
