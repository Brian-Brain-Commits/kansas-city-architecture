217 feet tall, dedicated 1926. The bronze bowl at the top holds a simulated flame — steam and light, visible from the plaza below.

Five Commanders, One Hill

On November 11, 1921 — the third anniversary of the Armistice — Kansas City held a dedication ceremony for its proposed war memorial on a hill in Penn Valley Park south of Union Station. What made that ceremony unprecedented in American history was who came: all five Allied supreme commanders of World War I attended together. France's Ferdinand Foch, Britain's Admiral David Beatty, Italy's Armando Diaz, Belgium's Jacques Baron de Stuers, and America's General John Pershing stood together on the same ground for the only time in history. The five men who had commanded the Allied victory gathered in Kansas City because Kansas City had asked, organized, and made it worth their while.

More than 100,000 people attended the dedication. The site was chosen. The architect was selected. But the memorial itself — Harold Van Buren Magonigle's Egyptian Revival tower, flanking pavilions, and underground museum — would take another five years to complete. The structure was finally finished in 1926, and has stood above the southern end of downtown Kansas City ever since, visible for miles in every direction.

◆

The Architecture of Permanence

Harold Van Buren Magonigle won the Liberty Memorial design competition in 1920 with a scheme that was deliberately unlike anything in contemporary American architecture. Where the dominant idiom of civic monuments in 1920 was Beaux-Arts Neoclassicism — columns, domes, pediments — Magonigle proposed an Egyptian Revival composition: a single massive tower with slightly tapering walls and almost no ornament, flanked by two pylon-fronted pavilions named "Memory" and "Future," connected by low walls and terraces to form a unified complex atop the hill.

The choice was intentional. Egypt suggested antiquity, permanence, and a kind of monumental silence appropriate to grief. The tower — a pylonic shaft rising 217 feet above the terrace — carries almost no decorative program, only the names of the dead carved into its surfaces and the eternal flame at its crown. The sphinxlike figures at the base of the central staircase, cast in 1921, face toward the city with expressions of indeterminate solemnity. The whole composition speaks the language of the ancient world, deliberately removed from the fashions of its own time.

An observation deck at the top of the tower offers one of the finest views in Kansas City: south to the country club district and Ward Parkway, west to the Missouri bluffs, north across downtown to the river. The view clarifies the city's geography in a way that no map can replicate — the limestone geology, the river confluences, the boulevard grid laid over the rolling terrain.

Decline, Rescue, and the National Museum

By the 1990s, the Liberty Memorial was in serious structural distress. Decades of deferred maintenance had compromised the stonework, the interior stairways, and the museum spaces beneath the terrace. In 1994, the building was closed to the public as unsafe. For several years it sat empty on its hill — one of the most distinctive landmarks in the city inaccessible to the city that owned it.

A restoration campaign, largely driven by a ballot measure passed by Kansas City voters in 1998, funded a $102 million reconstruction of the complex. The work took six years. The tower reopened in 2002, and the new National World War I Museum — purpose-built beneath the memorial terrace, with a glass floor suspended over a field of 9,000 poppies representing the Allied dead — opened in 2006. Congress designated the complex the National World War I Museum and Memorial in 2014.

The museum is among the finest military history museums in the United States, with a collection that traces the full arc of the war — its causes, its conduct, and its consequences — with exceptional depth on the American experience. The building above it, Magonigle's Egyptian tower, is what you see from across the city. They belong to each other: the architecture of mourning above, the architecture of understanding below.
