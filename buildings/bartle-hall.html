H. Roe
Bartle Hall

Original Year 1976
Expansion 1994
Pylon Height ~260 Feet
Exhibit Space 388,800 Sq Ft
Sky Stations R.M. Fischer, 1994
Address 301 W 13th St
◆

The four pylons rise 298 feet above the plaza. The Sky Stations at their tops are hollow aluminum sculptures by R. M. Fischer, installed 1994.

The Pylons on the Skyline

Four concrete pylons rise above the roofline of downtown Kansas City with an authority that has no precedent in the city's architecture. At approximately 260 feet, they clear the surrounding buildings and announce themselves from every approach to the city center — an unignorable structural statement that makes H. Roe Bartle Hall one of the most visually distinctive convention centers in the United States.

The pylons were not part of the original 1976 building. They arrived with the 1994 expansion, which doubled the convention center's size and added the engineering achievement that defines its identity: an enormous new exhibit hall suspended above active I-670, spanning the interstate with column-free floor space on a scale that had rarely been attempted in American commercial construction. The pylons are the structural expression of that span — the visible evidence of the forces at work beneath the surface.

◆

Sky Stations

Atop each pylon sits a Sky Station — an aluminum and steel sculpture designed by New York artist R.M. Fischer for the 1994 expansion. The sculptures are large-scale abstract compositions of spheres, discs, and geometric armatures that draw consciously on the vocabulary of 1930s industrial design and Art Deco ornament. Seen from street level, they appear as elaborate mechanical crowns; from a distance, they give the pylons a distinctly figurative silhouette that prevents them from reading as purely structural objects.

The decision to commission public art for the tops of structural pylons was not inevitable — it was an act of civic intention, a statement that the 1994 expansion intended to do more than add square footage. The Sky Stations connect Bartle Hall to Kansas City's long tradition of integrating ornament into civic infrastructure, a tradition visible everywhere from the bronze light standards at City Hall to the terra cotta friezes of the Power and Light Building. They are, in their own way, a form of loyalty to the city's architectural character.

Engineering Over the Interstate

The most significant technical achievement of the 1994 expansion is one that most visitors never consider: the exhibit hall floats above I-670, the six-lane interstate that cuts through the south edge of downtown. The building does not simply bridge the highway — it occupies the airspace above it, creating approximately 388,800 square feet of column-free convention space across a span that required the pylons to carry tremendous compressive and tensile loads simultaneously.

The result, at the time of its completion, was claimed to be the largest column-free convention floor in the world — a superlative that has since been surpassed by larger facilities, but that still speaks to the engineering ambition behind the expansion. The highway continues to operate below, the convention traffic operates above, and four concrete pylons hold the whole arrangement together in plain sight.

The Mayor Who Made the Chiefs

The building is named for H. Roe Bartle, the outsized mayor of Kansas City from 1955 to 1963 whose negotiations persuaded the Dallas Texans of the American Football League to relocate to Kansas City in 1963, becoming the Kansas City Chiefs. Bartle's involvement in that recruitment — and his personal guarantee of season ticket commitments — is credited as the decisive factor in the franchise's move. That the city's convention center bears his name is an acknowledgment of the degree to which that single act reshaped Kansas City's civic identity.

Bartle Hall occupies the same civic tier as Union Station and Municipal Auditorium — large public buildings whose purpose is explicitly to attract people and commerce to Kansas City from outside. That function, and the architectural ambition brought to it in the 1994 expansion, place it squarely in the tradition of civic building that has defined this city at its best.
