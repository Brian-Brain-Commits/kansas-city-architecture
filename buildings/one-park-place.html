700 W 31st St, Kansas City

One
Park Place

Stories ~20
Height ~220 ft
Style Late Modernist
Use Residential
Address 700 W 31st St
◆

Roughly 220 feet of modernist glass slab on the Hospital Hill bluff. Twenty stories of continuous horizontal floor banding, a flat roofline with no crown or ornament — a late-modern counterpart to the Art Deco towers downtown.

A Modernist Tower on the Bluff

One Park Place rises on the northern edge of Midtown, where the Hospital Hill bluff drops away north into Penn Valley Park and the Union Station valley beyond. Its siting is unusual. Most of Kansas City's tall residential buildings sit within the downtown core or cluster along the Plaza's southern edge. One Park Place stands alone on its bluff — a single twenty-story tower in a neighborhood otherwise composed of low-rise residential fabric, hospitals, and institutional buildings. From below, climbing up Penn Valley Park or driving along Southwest Boulevard, it reads as the signature vertical of the Midtown skyline.

The building is a product of the late-modernist moment in American urban residential architecture — roughly 1965 through 1985 — when a handful of tall residential towers were built in American cities that had previously had no high-rise residential stock. The style is defined by what it omits more than what it adds: no cornice, no decorative stringcourses, no stone ornament, no crown. What remains is pure tectonic expression — floors stacked as horizontal bands, windows as continuous ribbons, the facade's whole language carried by structural rhythm rather than applied detail.

◆

The Curtain Wall

The defining feature of One Park Place is its curtain wall — the glass-and-metal skin that hangs from the structural frame behind it, making the building read as a continuous envelope of ribbon windows wrapping a vertical core. This is the International Style vocabulary that originated in European modernist theory in the 1920s (Le Corbusier, the Bauhaus) and arrived in American commercial architecture in the postwar decades. By the time One Park Place was built, the curtain wall was a mature commercial typology — well understood structurally, efficient to construct, and legible as a signal of modern institutional or residential quality.

The horizontal banding that dominates the facade is not just visual. Each band corresponds to a floor slab behind the glass, with the spandrel panel between ribbon windows indicating the floor's structural edge. The building carries its structure on its face. That directness — seeing how the building is made by looking at it — is part of what modernism valued most, and it is still legible here six decades later.

Context on the Bluff

Hospital Hill is a distinct piece of Midtown geography — a bluff that runs east-west along roughly 23rd-to-31st Streets, overlooking the Union Station valley to the north and Penn Valley Park to the northwest. The hill has historically hosted Kansas City's concentration of hospitals (Truman Medical Center, the former Menorah Medical Center site, Children's Mercy), along with institutional and residential buildings at varying scales. One Park Place's siting takes advantage of the elevation — from its upper floors, residents look out over Penn Valley Park, the KCTV5 tower, the downtown skyline, and on clear days as far south as the Plaza.

The building's address, 700 W 31st Street, places it at the edge of where Midtown meets the southern foot of Hospital Hill. From Penn Valley Park looking south, One Park Place is the most visible modern building on the horizon — a vertical counterpoint to the Liberty Memorial shaft a half-mile to the west. Together they frame the bluff: one from 1926 in stone and ornamented classicism, the other from the 1970s in glass and structural modernism. The Midtown skyline's full range of twentieth-century American architectural attitudes lives in that pairing.
