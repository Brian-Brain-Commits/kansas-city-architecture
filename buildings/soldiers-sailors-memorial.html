Soldiers & Sailors
Memorial

Dedicated May 30, 1905
Original Location 17th & Broadway
Relocated 1959 — Budd Park
Material Granite obelisk
Commemorates Union Civil War veterans
◆

Dedicated 1905, 80 feet of granite in Penn Valley Park. The oldest war memorial in Kansas City — 21 years older than Liberty Memorial on the hill above it.

Kansas City's Civil War Monument

The Soldiers & Sailors Memorial is the older of Kansas City's two major war memorials — dedicated in 1905 to commemorate the service of Union soldiers and sailors in the Civil War. Liberty Memorial, the larger and more famous tower on the bluff above Union Station, was dedicated twenty-one years later to commemorate World War I. The two monuments represent the same civic impulse expressed in different generations: the obligation of a city to build something permanent in stone to name its dead.

The monument was funded by Civil War veterans' organizations — primarily the Grand Army of the Republic, the Union veterans' fraternal group — along with donations from Kansas City civic organizations and individual subscribers. The Civil War was forty years past when the monument was dedicated; the veterans who attended the 1905 ceremony were in their sixties and seventies, the last generation of men who had fought in a war that increasingly felt like history to the city that was rapidly becoming a twentieth-century metropolis around them.

The Obelisk

The memorial is a granite obelisk rising from a stepped base, a form chosen for its classical associations with commemorative monuments of antiquity. The choice of granite — durable, monochromatic, somber — was deliberate. Bronze statuary, of the kind that populated Civil War monuments in older Eastern cities, was rejected in favor of a single vertical stone, a decision that reflects both the prairie city's sensibility and the practical constraint of the available budget. Inscribed panels at the base name the units raised from the Kansas City area that served in the Union Army and Navy. The overall effect is understated and permanent rather than heroic and commemorative.

The 1959 Relocation

The monument was originally installed at 17th and Broadway — an intersection that was, in 1905, at the edge of Kansas City's commercial downtown and therefore a visible public location. By the 1950s that corner had been transformed: the Penn Valley Park and Crown Center developments were reshaping the area, automobile traffic had increased dramatically, and the 1905 monument was visually diminished by larger surrounding buildings and widened streets. The decision to relocate the memorial to Budd Park in 1959 moved it from a declining downtown edge to a quieter residential neighborhood context on the northeast side of the city.

Budd Park — part of the Kessler-designed parks and boulevards system, connected to the broader network of green spaces that traces the city's early twentieth-century civic ambition — was a sympathetic new setting. The monument sits on a small rise within the park, approached by a paved walk, surrounded by mature trees. It is a calmer location than the downtown street corner, and a location where the monument can be seen at appropriate distance. It is also a less visible location than its original home, and most Kansas Citians today have never seen it.

The Parallel with Liberty Memorial

The Soldiers & Sailors Memorial is interesting partly on its own terms and partly as the precedent and shadow for Liberty Memorial. The 1905 monument established that Kansas City would mark its war dead with a major public monument; the 1926 Liberty Memorial demonstrated that Kansas City's civic ambition could match or exceed that of any American city in the scale of its commemoration. The two monuments exist in a relationship: the older one modestly Civil War, the newer one theatrically WWI, each honoring a different generation of Kansas Citians killed in a different war fought for different reasons.

You can visit both in the same afternoon if you have a car. Start at Budd Park on the northeast side, where the 1905 granite obelisk stands quietly in a residential park. Drive south to Liberty Memorial on the Union Station bluff, where the 1926 tower dominates the skyline approach from the south. The progression from one monument to the other is a reading of how Kansas City's sense of its own civic weight changed between 1905 and 1926 — and of how the city's commemorative ambitions grew along with its architectural ones.  Read about Liberty Memorial →
