Built for the Golden Age of Rail

In the first decade of the twentieth century, Kansas City's existing train station was overwhelmed by the explosion of rail traffic accompanying the city's rapid growth. Railroad executives and civic leaders agreed that a new station was needed: not merely adequate, but grand. Chicago architect Jarvis Hunt was commissioned, and his design for the new Union Station became one of the defining monuments of the Kansas City landscape.

The station opened in October 1914. It was, at the time, the second-largest train station in the United States — surpassed only by New York's Pennsylvania Station, which had opened three years earlier. At the height of the railroad era, more than 200 trains per day passed through its platforms. During World War II, when Kansas City was a major troop transit hub, the station handled more than 80,000 passengers per day. The Harvey House restaurant, operated by the legendary Fred Harvey company, was one of the finest dining establishments between Chicago and San Francisco.

◆

The Grand Hall

The Grand Hall is one of the great interior spaces in American architecture. Measuring 95 feet from floor to ceiling, 125 feet wide, and 385 feet long, it was designed to convey — without ambiguity — that Kansas City stood among the great cities of the continent. Three original chandeliers, each 22 feet in diameter and weighing 3,500 pounds, hung from the coffered ceiling. The floor was laid in travertine marble. Ornamental plaster in the classical manner covered walls and ceilings throughout.

The Hall was designed to be the first and last impression of Kansas City for millions of travelers. For the immigrants and travelers who arrived here in the early twentieth century, stepping into the Grand Hall — with its soaring ceilings, its marble floors, its light flooding in from the great windows — must have been a genuinely overwhelming experience. The architecture was making an argument about what Kansas City was and aspired to be.

The Kansas City Massacre

On June 17, 1933, Union Station was the site of one of the most significant events in FBI history. Four law enforcement officers and the fugitive they were escorting — Frank Nash, being transferred back to Leavenworth — were shot and killed by gunmen in the station's parking lot in what became known as the Kansas City Massacre. The killings, attributed to associates of gangster Pretty Boy Floyd, shocked the nation and galvanized public support for expanding the FBI's authority and resources. J. Edgar Hoover used the incident to push successfully for legislation allowing FBI agents to carry weapons and make arrests — transforming the Bureau into the national law enforcement institution it became.

Decline and Rebirth

Like Union Stations across America, Kansas City's terminal was a casualty of the postwar decline of passenger rail. Interstate highways drew away leisure travelers. Airline deregulation made coast-to-coast air travel affordable. By the 1970s, train traffic had dwindled to a fraction of its wartime peak. In 1985, the last train departed, and the station closed entirely.

The building sat empty for more than a decade, its grand spaces dark, its future uncertain. In the 1990s, a coalition of Kansas City businesses and civic leaders mounted a restoration campaign that resulted in a $250 million renovation — one of the largest historic preservation projects in American history. The restored Union Station reopened in 1999, housing Science City (a hands-on science museum), restaurants, event spaces, and — fittingly — an Amtrak station. The Grand Hall was returned to something approaching its original magnificence. It is once again one of the most visited destinations in Kansas City.
