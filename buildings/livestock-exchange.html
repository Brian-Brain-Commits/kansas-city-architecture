The Civic Monument of the Beef Trade

Completed in 1910 at 1600 Genessee Street, the Kansas City Livestock Exchange Building was the administrative heart of the second-largest livestock market in the world. Nine stories of buff brick over a rusticated limestone base, crowned with a mansard roof and dormer windows — the scale and dignity of a civic institution, which, in the economy of early Kansas City, it effectively was.

The Exchange housed commission firms, livestock banks, the stockyards telegraph offices, and the cattle-pricing exchange floor. Thousands of buyers, sellers, commission agents, and clerks worked in this single building. When the Stockyards were running at peak capacity in the 1890s–1920s, the Exchange was functionally the commodity-trade floor of the American beef economy — as consequential to the Midwest as Wall Street was to the East Coast finance industry, and nearly as specialized.

After the Yards Closed

The Kansas City Stockyards closed in 1991 after a century of decline. The Exchange Building survived because its bones were too good to tear down — the steel-and-brick construction that defined 1910 commercial architecture was overbuilt by modern standards, and the building adapted to office use with minimal structural work. Today the Exchange hosts law firms, event space, loft apartments, and an ongoing program of preservation — an anchor for West Bottoms redevelopment without displacing what was there.
