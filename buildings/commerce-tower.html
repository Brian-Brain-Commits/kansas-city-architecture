Kansas City, Missouri • 1965

Commerce
Tower

Year Built 1965
Height 418 ft
Floors 32
Style International Style / Modernist
Address 911 Main St
Architect Keene & Simpson & Murphy
◆

418 feet, 32 floors, opened 1965. No setbacks, no crown — Kansas City's first pure International Style tower, built at the peak of mid-century modernism.

The First New Tower in Thirty Years

When Commerce Tower opened at 911 Main Street in 1965, it was the first new downtown skyscraper Kansas City had built since 1937. For twenty-eight years the Art Deco skyline had held alone — the P&L Building, City Hall, the Jackson County Courthouse, 909 Walnut, the Federal Reserve, the Municipal Auditorium — while the city spent the postwar decades building suburbs and highways. Commerce Tower was the first sign that downtown itself would continue to accumulate buildings. It was also, stylistically, a clean break from the skyline it joined.

Commerce Bank, the tower's anchor tenant and namesake, commissioned the building as a modern headquarters in an era when KC's older office stock was looking dated. The design, by the firm of Keene & Simpson & Murphy, was uncomplicated International Style: a rectangular slab with a grid of windows, a restrained granite-and-glass skin, and a flat roof. It was what corporate headquarters looked like in American cities in 1965, and Kansas City was, for a moment, building one.

The Modernist Silhouette

Commerce Tower is a flat-topped slab. It has no crown, no setbacks, no ornamentation at the top to speak of. This is the opposite of the 1930s Art Deco vocabulary, where the top of the building was the building's argument — the stepped crown of the P&L, the pyramid of City Hall, the ornamental spandrels of 909 Walnut. Commerce Tower's argument was efficiency: a clean rectangle maximizing leasable floor space, expressed in the same international modernist vocabulary that was reshaping every American downtown in the 1950s and 60s.

In the context of the KC skyline, Commerce Tower reads as the first visible piece of evidence that the 1930s consensus had ended. After Commerce Tower, the next downtown towers would be postmodern (Town Pavilion, One Kansas City Place, both mid-1980s), then contemporary glass (the Westlight, One Light, Two Light). The Art Deco skyline remained the dominant visual element, but it was now a skyline with other centuries of buildings in it.

The Mid-Century Moment That Almost Happened

Commerce Tower was supposed to signal a 1960s downtown revival that never quite materialized. The original plan for the Commerce Bank block envisioned additional new construction, a pedestrian plaza, and integration with nearby modernist projects. Some of this was built; much of it was deferred as the city's population continued to shift south and the retail anchor of downtown continued to erode. Commerce Tower ended up as a somewhat solitary modernist intervention rather than the leading edge of a wave of similar buildings.

The 1960s and 70s did produce a few other downtown modernist towers (the Federal Reserve Bank's mid-century additions, Crown Center's buildings to the south) but the wholesale downtown replacement that cities like Pittsburgh and Cleveland went through in the same decades did not happen in Kansas City at the same scale. The Art Deco skyline survived partly because nothing was built with enough ambition to displace it. Commerce Tower stands as both a sign of the era that was trying to happen, and a marker of how little that era ultimately built.

Residential Conversion

Commerce Bank relocated its headquarters to another downtown building in 2015, freeing up the tower for redevelopment. Beginning around 2018, the upper floors have been converted to apartments, joining the broader wave of downtown office-to-residential conversions of the late 2010s and early 2020s. The tower now houses residents on most of its office floors; ground-level retail and a portion of the lower floors continue as commercial space. It is one of several mid-century downtown towers that have found a second life as the city's central business district has added residents for the first time in generations.
