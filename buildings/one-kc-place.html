Elevation — One Kansas City Place

624 feet, 45 stories — the tallest building in Kansas City. Skidmore, Owings & Merrill's modernist curtain-wall tower stands 148 feet taller than the KC Power & Light Building it displaced as the city's tallest in 1988. The contrast between the two defines the downtown core.

624 feet of glass, 42 floors, opened 1988. Still the tallest building Kansas City has built.

The Tallest Tower

One Kansas City Place is the tallest building in Kansas City and one of the tallest in the Great Plains — a 45-story tower of glass and granite that has anchored the downtown skyline since its completion in 1988. At 623 feet, it stands roughly 150 feet taller than the KC Power and Light Building, the Art Deco tower it displaced as the city's tallest structure. The shift in that record marks a meaningful transition in Kansas City's architectural history: from the civic ambition of the 1930s to the corporate investment of the 1980s.

The building was originally developed as AT&T Town Pavilion, designed by Skidmore, Owings & Merrill — the Chicago firm that shaped more of twentieth-century American commercial architecture than any other office. SOM's portfolio at the time included the John Hancock Center, the Sears Tower, and the Lever House; their Kansas City commission was the largest private construction project in the city's history at the time of its completion.

◆

Architecture and Context

The building's design reflects the postmodern sensibility of 1980s corporate architecture — a reaction against the austerity of high Modernism that reintroduced historical reference without full historical commitment. The tower's base engages the street with a glassed winter garden and retail arcade; its midsection is a straightforward glass curtain wall in SOM's characteristic precise manner; its crown steps back in a sequence of setbacks that self-consciously echo, at larger scale, the Deco setbacks of the buildings around it.

Whether that gesture of homage succeeds is a question Kansas City's architects and critics have debated since the building opened. The Deco setback was driven by zoning requirements and aerodynamic practicality, and its aesthetic power came from those functional origins. The One KC Place setbacks are purely formal — a quotation rather than a statement — and they read accordingly at close range. From a distance, however, the building's sheer height gives it a commanding presence in the skyline that transcends the debate about its surface.

The Skyline Conversation

One Kansas City Place does something architecturally significant despite its aesthetic ambiguities: it anchors the downtown skyline's verticality at a scale that the Art Deco towers alone could not achieve. Seen from the Liberty Memorial, from the bluffs of the West Bottoms, or from any of the major approaching highways, the tower reads as a modern statement in conversation with the Deco cluster around it — not a displacement but a continuation of Kansas City's tradition of building tall and building conspicuously.

The building also marks the moment when private corporate investment returned to downtown Kansas City after decades of suburban flight and urban renewal disruption. In that sense its significance is as much economic and civic as it is architectural. Like the Ten-Year Plan buildings of the 1930s, One Kansas City Place was a bet on downtown — a statement that the center of the city was worth investing in at the highest possible level.
