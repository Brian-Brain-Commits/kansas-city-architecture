A City Designed to Be Lived In

Kansas City's public spaces were not afterthoughts — they were the organizing principle. J.C. Nichols and landscape architect George Kessler understood that a great city needed not just great buildings but great places to be between them: boulevard promenades, fountain squares, shaded parks, and a commercial district grand enough to rival the plazas of Europe. The result is one of the most thoughtfully designed urban landscapes in America.

From the 200-plus public fountains that gave Kansas City its unofficial title as the "City of Fountains" to the Country Club Plaza's Andalusian streetscape to the Kessler boulevard system that stitches the city together with parkway green, Kansas City's public realm is the outdoor counterpart to its architectural heritage — and equally worth exploring.
