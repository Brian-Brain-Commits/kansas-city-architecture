How Kansas City Is Laid Out

Kansas City's public spaces were not afterthoughts — they were the organizing principle. Landscape architect George Kessler planned the boulevard system in 1893 as a network of green corridors connecting parks, neighborhoods, and civic anchors across the blufftop terrain. J.C. Nichols built his Country Club District along that framework, adding fountains, plazas, and a Spanish Colonial commercial center that became one of the most imitated commercial districts in American history. The result is a city legible from the ground: follow the boulevards and the fountains appear; follow the fountains and the Plaza appears.

This section covers the systems and spaces that define Kansas City's urban form — the Plaza, the parks and boulevard network, Ward Parkway, and the overlooks from which the city's geography becomes visible. For the fountains themselves, which deserve their own extended attention, see the Fountains section.
