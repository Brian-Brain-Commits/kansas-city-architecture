Why This Site Exists

Kansas City built a skyline in one decade and then mostly forgot to brag about it. The Art Deco towers downtown, the Spanish Colonial discipline of the Country Club Plaza, the Beaux-Arts grandeur of Union Station, the boulevard-and-fountain system that connects them — this is one of the most coherent and underappreciated architectural environments in the United States.

Most cities that built like this in the 1920s and 30s have turned their downtown cores into destinations. Kansas City largely hasn't, which means the buildings are still there but the stories around them have gone untold. This site is an attempt to tell them.

It is not a tourism brochure, a Wikipedia mirror, or a directory of opening hours. It is an attempt to explain Kansas City through its built environment — what was constructed, when, by whom, why it looks the way it does, and what was lost. The intended reader is someone standing on a downtown street corner, looking up, and wanting to understand what they're seeing.

How It's Built

The site is plain HTML and CSS — no JavaScript framework, no content management system, no build step. Every page is a hand-authored HTML file linked to a single stylesheet. It runs on GitHub Pages and loads fast on any device.

The writing and the site architecture were developed collaboratively with Claude Code, Anthropic's AI coding tool. The editorial voice, the selection of subjects, and the factual claims are the author's own. Claude Code handled the structural work — building consistent page templates, managing the nav across ~70 files, generating the CSS components — which meant less time on plumbing and more time on substance.

On the Writing

The site aims for the register of a well-researched magazine feature, not an encyclopedia entry and not a press release. That means: full sentences, an editorial point of view, honest acknowledgment of what's complicated (J.C. Nichols's racial deed restrictions, the human cost of urban renewal, the political machinery behind the Ten-Year Plan), and no padding. If a building is mediocre, it's described as mediocre. If a decision was destructive, it's described as destructive.

Primary sources and secondary scholarship were consulted where available. The Kansas City Public Library, the Missouri Valley Special Collections, and published architectural histories of the region were the main reference points. Where the record is disputed or uncertain, the text says so.

Dates, heights, architect attributions, and construction years were cross-referenced against multiple sources. Errors are possible and corrections are welcome — see below.

Photography

All current photography is sourced from Wikimedia Commons under public domain and Creative Commons licenses. A full credits listing with attribution, license type, and source link for every image used on the site is available on the Photo Credits page.

Original photography of Kansas City — shot specifically for this site — is in progress. When it is ready, it will replace the Wikimedia images on key pages and will be labeled with the site's .image-detail component. The Wikimedia images will remain credited in the archive.

Errors & Corrections

If something on this site is factually wrong — a wrong date, a misattributed architect, an error of historical record — please flag it. The goal is accuracy, not the appearance of authority. This is a personal project without an editorial staff, which means mistakes will happen, and catching them depends on readers who know better.

The site is open source. Corrections, additions, and improvements can be submitted as pull requests or issues on GitHub. The repository link is in the footer.

For everything else — questions, suggestions, a building or neighborhood you think belongs here — the same channel applies. This site is meant to grow.
